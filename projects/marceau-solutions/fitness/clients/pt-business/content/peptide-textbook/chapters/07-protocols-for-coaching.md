## CHAPTER 7: PEPTIDE PROTOCOLS FOR FITNESS COACHING

This is the chapter where everything converges. Chapters 1 through 6 gave you the science, the pharmacology, the evidence base, and the individual peptide profiles. Chapter 7 gives you the framework for putting it all into practice with real clients, in real coaching scenarios, grounded in real evidence.

### 7.1 The Peptide-Informed Coaching Model

The peptide-informed coaching model rests on a fundamental distinction: **you are not prescribing peptides. You are programming around them.**

Your client's physician handles the medical side: prescribing, dosing, blood work interpretation, safety monitoring, and protocol adjustments. Your role is to optimize everything the physician cannot reasonably manage in a 15-minute appointment: training periodization that complements the peptide's mechanism, nutrition timing that respects pharmacokinetics, recovery programming that capitalizes on enhanced tissue repair, and progress tracking that captures the metrics that matter.

This model works because peptides do not operate in isolation. A growth hormone secretagogue amplifies recovery capacity, but only if the training stimulus is appropriate. Semaglutide suppresses appetite and drives weight loss, but without resistance training and adequate protein, approximately 26–40% of the weight lost will be lean mass (Locatelli et al., *Diabetes Care*, 2024; DOI: 10.2337/dci23-0100). BPC-157 may accelerate tendon healing, but if the client returns to full loading too soon, the tendon will fail regardless.

The peptide-informed coach sits at the intersection of pharmacology and programming. That is where you add irreplaceable value.

#### The Three Pillars of Peptide-Informed Coaching

**Pillar 1: Education Without Prescription.** You explain mechanisms, evidence levels, and realistic expectations. You help clients understand what their peptide does and does not do. You never recommend a specific compound or dose for a specific condition — that is the physician's role. You do say: "The research on CJC-1295 plus ipamorelin suggests that GH pulses peak approximately 30–60 minutes post-injection, which is why many protocols recommend evening dosing to complement natural nocturnal GH release."

**Pillar 2: Programming Integration.** You design training, nutrition, and recovery protocols that complement the client's peptide regimen. If a client is on a GH secretagogue, you program to exploit the enhanced recovery window. If a client is on semaglutide, you structure resistance training and protein intake to preserve lean mass. If a client is using BPC-157 for a tendon injury, you build a progressive loading protocol that gives the tissue the mechanical stimulus it needs while respecting healing timelines.

**Pillar 3: Monitoring and Communication.** You track outcomes that matter — body composition changes, strength progression, recovery metrics, sleep quality, subjective well-being — and you communicate these observations to the client's medical team. A coach who can email a physician a structured progress report with body composition trends, training logs, and relevant observations is invaluable. A coach who just programs sets and reps while ignoring the peptide protocol is replaceable.

#### Evidence-Based Confidence Calibration

Not all peptides deserve the same level of confidence in your coaching recommendations. Calibrate your language and your programming specificity to the evidence level:

| Evidence Level | Your Confidence | Coaching Approach | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Strong RCT data** in humans | High — program with specificity | Design protocols around documented outcomes with specific timelines | Semaglutide: program 3–4x/week resistance training, 1.6 g/kg/day protein, expect 15–20% weight loss over 68 weeks |
| **Moderate human data** (small RCTs, observational) | Moderate — program with flexibility | Design protocols around plausible mechanisms, monitor closely | Tesamorelin: program evening training sessions, monitor IGF-1 and fasting glucose quarterly |
| **Primarily animal data** | Low — program conservatively | Support general recovery; do not over-attribute outcomes to the peptide | BPC-157 for tendon: follow standard rehab progressions, note if recovery appears faster than expected |
| **Anecdotal only** | Minimal — acknowledge uncertainty | Do not modify programming based on unsubstantiated claims | Epithalon for longevity: no programming adjustments warranted |

### 7.2 Client Archetypes and Protocol Frameworks

The following archetypes represent the most common client presentations in peptide-informed coaching. Each includes the relevant pharmacology, evidence-based programming principles, and the specific role of the fitness coach.

#### 7.2.1 The Body Recomposition Client

**Profile:** Wants to lose fat and gain or maintain muscle simultaneously. May be using GH secretagogues (CJC-1295/ipamorelin, MK-677) and/or metabolic peptides (AOD-9604, MOTS-c). Often a male aged 30–55 with 18–30% body fat who has been training for 1+ years.

**Relevant Pharmacology:** Growth hormone secretagogues increase GH pulsatility, which promotes lipolysis (particularly in visceral fat) and supports muscle protein synthesis when combined with resistance training. A review by Sinha et al. (*Translational Andrology and Urology*, 2020; DOI: 10.21037/tau.2019.11.30) found that GHS compounds including ipamorelin are "potent GH and IGF-1 stimulators that can significantly improve body composition while ameliorating specific hypogonadal symptoms including fat gain and muscular atrophy."

**Programming Framework:**

*Training:*
- 4x/week upper/lower split or push/pull/legs rotation
- Emphasize progressive overload — GH enhancement supports recovery but does not replace stimulus
- Program compound movements (squat, deadlift, bench, row, overhead press) as primary movers
- Include 2–3 sets of isolation work for lagging body parts
- Training volume: 12–20 sets per muscle group per week, adjusted based on recovery capacity
- If using evening peptide injection: train in the afternoon/evening when possible. GH secretagogues administered at bedtime complement the natural nocturnal GH pulse; training earlier in the day provides the anabolic stimulus, and the enhanced GH pulse during sleep supports recovery and protein synthesis overnight

*Nutrition:*
- Moderate caloric deficit (300–500 kcal/day) or maintenance calories for true recomposition
- Protein: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day, distributed across 4–5 meals
- Carbohydrates: prioritize peri-workout (before and after training)
- Fats: minimum 0.7 g/kg/day for hormonal health
- GH secretagogues are best administered on an empty stomach (GH release is blunted by elevated blood glucose and insulin). Instruct clients to avoid eating 1.5–2 hours before and 30–60 minutes after injection

*Monitoring:*
- Body composition: DEXA scan or skinfold measurements every 8–12 weeks
- Strength progression: track working weights weekly
- Recovery: monitor resting heart rate, sleep quality (subjective and wearable data)
- Blood work: IGF-1 (target: 200–300 ng/mL for adults), fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel — every 12 weeks during the first 6 months, then every 6 months

*Coach's Red Flags:*
- Fasting glucose consistently >100 mg/dL → refer to physician (GH can impair insulin sensitivity)
- Persistent joint pain or edema → possible fluid retention from elevated GH/IGF-1
- Significant fatigue despite adequate sleep → check thyroid panel (GH can increase T4-to-T3 conversion demands)

#### 7.2.2 The Injured Athlete

**Profile:** Competitive or recreational athlete dealing with a tendon, ligament, or muscle injury. May be using BPC-157, TB-500, or both. Eager to return to sport as quickly as possible. Often impatient.

**Relevant Pharmacology:** BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound-157) is a 15-amino-acid peptide derived from human gastric juice that has demonstrated accelerated healing of tendons, ligaments, muscles, and bone in extensive animal studies (Sikiric et al., multiple publications, University of Zagreb). Its proposed mechanisms include upregulation of growth factor receptors (VEGF, FGF), nitric oxide system modulation, and FAK-paxillin pathway activation. TB-500 (a fragment of thymosin beta-4) promotes cell migration, angiogenesis, and anti-inflammatory effects through actin sequestration.

A 2026 review in the *Journal of the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons* (Rahman, Lee, Seeds; DOI: 10.5435/JAAOSGlobal-D-25-00236) confirmed that "wound-healing peptides such as BPC-157, TB-500, and GHK-Cu promote angiogenesis, integrin-mediated extracellular matrix remodeling, and fibroblast activation." However, the same review and a companion paper in the *American Journal of Sports Medicine* (Mayfield et al., 2026; DOI: 10.1177/03635465251357593) both emphasized that "BPC-157 demonstrated potential benefits in tendon and muscle repair, but these findings are largely unvalidated in human trials."

**Critical coaching note:** The evidence for injectable BPC-157 and TB-500 in humans is almost entirely preclinical. Program conservatively. Do not compress standard rehabilitation timelines based on the assumption that these peptides will accelerate healing. If healing appears to be progressing faster than expected, take that as a bonus — not as a reason to skip phases.

**Programming Framework:**

*Phase 1 — Acute (Weeks 0–2):*
- Protect the injured tissue. Follow physician/PT protocols exactly
- Maintain cardiovascular fitness with non-impacting modalities (cycling, swimming, if cleared)
- Train uninjured body parts to maintain neural drive and prevent detraining
- If cleared for gentle range-of-motion work, follow PT guidelines
- BPC-157/TB-500 timing: typically administered 1–2x daily subcutaneously near the injury site (per physician protocol). No programming modifications required from the coach; focus on compliance with medical rehab

*Phase 2 — Subacute (Weeks 2–6):*
- Progressive loading of the injured tissue begins (isometric → eccentric → concentric)
- For tendons: heavy slow resistance protocol (Kongsgaard et al., 2009) — 3 sets of 15 reps progressing to 6 reps over weeks, 3x/week
- Eccentric loading: standard evidence-based protocol (Alfredson heavy eccentric for Achilles, for example)
- Continue full-body training for uninjured areas
- Monitor pain: use a 0–10 pain scale. Training should not exceed 3/10 pain during exercise or cause pain that persists >24 hours after

*Phase 3 — Remodeling (Weeks 6–12):*
- Progressive return to sport-specific movements
- Gradually increase load, speed, and complexity
- Maintain tissue-specific strengthening 2–3x/week
- Plyometric loading when appropriate (typically week 8+ for tendons)

*Phase 4 — Return to Full Activity (Week 12+):*
- Sport-specific drills at full intensity
- Maintenance strengthening program continues
- Peptide protocol typically discontinued per physician guidance (common duration: 4–12 weeks)

*Monitoring:*
- Weekly pain assessment (0–10 scale, location, pattern)
- Range of motion measurements every 2 weeks
- Strength testing relative to contralateral limb (goal: >90% symmetry before full return)
- Functional testing specific to sport demands

*Coach's Red Flags:*
- Pain increasing despite progressive loading → refer back to physician/PT
- Swelling that does not resolve within 24 hours post-training
- Any new symptoms (numbness, clicking, instability)
- Client attempting to skip rehabilitation phases

#### 7.2.3 The Anti-Aging Executive

**Profile:** High-earning professional, typically aged 40–60, focused on longevity, cognitive performance, energy, and physical appearance. May be using a stack: GH secretagogue (CJC-1295/ipamorelin or MK-677), BPC-157 for joint maintenance, epithalon for longevity, and possibly selank or semax for cognitive enhancement. Often time-constrained.

**Relevant Pharmacology:** This archetype often runs a multi-peptide protocol targeting several systems simultaneously. The coach must understand how each compound interacts with training:

- GH secretagogues enhance recovery and body composition (evidence: moderate, primarily observational and small RCTs)
- Epithalon targets telomerase activation (evidence: animal studies and small human studies; Khavinson et al.)
- Selank and semax are anxiolytic and nootropic peptides, respectively (evidence: approved for clinical use in Russia; limited Western clinical data)
- BPC-157 at low doses for joint maintenance (evidence: preclinical)

**Programming Framework:**

*Training (3–4x/week, time-efficient):*
- Full-body sessions or upper/lower split to maximize frequency within limited time
- Superset and tri-set structures to keep sessions under 60 minutes
- Prioritize: compound strength (3 sets), hypertrophy (2–3 sets), and mobility/flexibility (10 min)
- Include 2x/week zone 2 cardio (30–45 min) for cardiovascular and metabolic health — walking, cycling, or rowing at conversational pace
- 1x/week high-intensity interval session (20 min) for VO2max maintenance

*Nutrition:*
- Moderate protein (1.4–1.6 g/kg/day) — sufficient for muscle maintenance without the aggressive targets needed for recomposition
- Emphasize micronutrient density: vegetables, fruits, omega-3 fatty acids, fiber
- Time-restricted eating (16:8 or 14:10) is compatible with GH secretagogues (fasted state enhances GH release) — but ensure adequate total calories and protein within the eating window
- Minimize alcohol: ethanol suppresses GH secretion and impairs sleep architecture

*Recovery and Lifestyle:*
- Sleep optimization is paramount for this archetype. GH secretagogues enhance nocturnal GH pulsatility, but only if sleep quality is adequate
- Sleep protocol: consistent bedtime, dark room, no screens 60 min before bed, room temperature 65–68°F
- Stress management: this archetype often has high cortisol. Chronically elevated cortisol antagonizes GH signaling and promotes visceral fat deposition. Program dedicated recovery: yoga, meditation, walking in nature
- If using DSIP (delta sleep-inducing peptide): may enhance sleep onset — complement with sleep hygiene practices, do not use as a substitute

*Monitoring:*
- Comprehensive blood work panel every 6 months: full hormonal panel (testosterone, estradiol, DHEA-S, cortisol, thyroid), metabolic markers (fasting glucose, HbA1c, insulin, lipids), liver/kidney function, IGF-1, CBC
- Body composition: DEXA annually at minimum
- Cognitive function: subjective tracking (energy, focus, mood) with validated instruments (e.g., POMS, SF-36)
- Cardiovascular: resting HR, HRV trends (wearable data), blood pressure

#### 7.2.4 The GLP-1 Client (Semaglutide/Tirzepatide)

**Profile:** Client on a GLP-1 receptor agonist (semaglutide, tirzepatide, or liraglutide) for weight management. This is the fastest-growing client archetype in peptide-informed coaching and the one where a knowledgeable coach adds the most measurable value.

**Relevant Pharmacology:** GLP-1 receptor agonists suppress appetite through central nervous system signaling, slow gastric emptying, and improve insulin sensitivity. They produce substantial weight loss — the STEP trials showed 14.9% average weight loss with semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly over 68 weeks; the SURMOUNT trials showed 20.9% with tirzepatide 15 mg weekly. However, lean mass loss constitutes approximately 26–40% of total weight lost (Locatelli et al., *Diabetes Care*, 2024; DOI: 10.2337/dci23-0100). This rate of lean mass loss is comparable to a decade or more of natural aging.

The coaching imperative is clear: **resistance training and high-protein nutrition are non-negotiable for GLP-1 clients.** A 2025 case series (Preservation of lean soft tissue during weight loss induced by GLP-1 and GLP-1/GIP receptor agonists; DOI: 10.1177/2050313X251388724) demonstrated that structured resistance training with adequate protein intake significantly mitigated lean mass loss during GLP-1 therapy.

A 2026 review in the *British Journal of Pharmacology* (DOI: 10.1111/bph.70355) specifically warned about GLP-1 agonists and muscle strength changes in older adults, noting risks beyond simple mass reduction — including functional decline and increased fall risk.

**Programming Framework:**

*Training (3–4x/week, resistance-focused):*
- **Resistance training is the single most important intervention for this client.** Prioritize it over cardio
- Full-body or upper/lower split, 3–4x/week minimum
- Focus on progressive overload: increase weight or reps weekly
- Compound movements: squat variations, hip hinge variations, horizontal and vertical pressing, rowing
- Volume: 10–15 hard sets per muscle group per week initially, increasing as the client adapts
- Supervised resistance exercise training interventions >10 weeks can elicit approximately 3 kg gains in lean mass and 25% gains in strength (Locatelli et al., 2024) — communicate these expected outcomes to the client to build adherence
- Cardio: 2x/week zone 2, kept brief (20–30 min). Excessive cardio on a GLP-1 client in caloric deficit will accelerate lean mass loss

*Nutrition (the critical variable):*
- Protein: **minimum 1.6 g/kg/day of current body weight**, ideally 2.0 g/kg/day. This is the single most important nutritional target
- GLP-1 clients often struggle to eat enough due to appetite suppression. Practical strategies:
  - Prioritize protein at every meal — eat protein first before vegetables and carbohydrates
  - Use protein shakes/supplements if whole food intake is insufficient
  - Distribute protein across 4+ eating occasions (not 1–2 large meals)
  - Track protein intake daily — this client needs accountability here
- Total calories: the GLP-1 is already creating a significant caloric deficit through appetite suppression. The coach's role is to ensure the deficit is not so severe that lean mass loss accelerates. Target 1,200–1,500 kcal minimum for women, 1,500–1,800 kcal minimum for men, adjusting based on body composition trends
- Common GI side effects (nausea, bloating, constipation) can interfere with eating. Strategies: smaller, more frequent meals; avoid high-fat meals; ginger tea for nausea; adequate fiber and hydration for constipation

*Monitoring (critical — track lean mass rigorously):*
- Body composition: DEXA or bioimpedance every 8–12 weeks. Scale weight alone is misleading for this client — you must separate fat loss from lean mass loss
- Strength tracking: if strength is declining, lean mass is likely declining. Adjust programming or nutrition
- Protein intake audit: weekly review of food logs. If protein is consistently below 1.6 g/kg/day, this must be addressed immediately
- Energy and training performance: GLP-1 clients may fatigue more easily due to reduced caloric intake. Adjust volume if performance drops significantly
- Blood work: HbA1c, fasting insulin, lipid panel every 12 weeks (physician manages, but coach reviews for context)

*The Hard Conversation:*
Many GLP-1 clients come to you with the expectation that the medication does all the work. Your job is to reframe: the medication handles appetite and fat loss. You handle lean mass preservation. Without your programming, they will lose muscle, strength, bone density, and metabolic rate. They need you. Communicate this clearly, with evidence, from day one.

*Coach's Red Flags:*
- Losing >2 lb/week consistently after the first month → deficit may be too aggressive, lean mass loss likely accelerating
- Strength declining across multiple lifts for >2 weeks → nutrition review needed
- Client eating <1,000 kcal/day → medical referral, this is dangerous regardless of peptide use
- Client reporting muscle cramps, hair loss, fatigue → possible micronutrient deficiencies (common on severe caloric restriction)

#### 7.2.5 The Competitive Athlete

**Profile:** Athletes competing in drug-tested or non-tested sports who are using or considering peptides for recovery, performance, or injury management. This archetype requires the most nuanced scope-of-practice awareness.

**Programming Considerations:**

*Drug-Tested Athletes:*
- **WADA (World Anti-Doping Agency) bans virtually all peptides discussed in this textbook.** Growth hormone secretagogues (all classes), IGF-1, BPC-157, TB-500, SARMs, and most other performance-relevant peptides are on the Prohibited List
- As a coach, you must be explicitly clear about this. An athlete who tests positive for a banned substance faces career-ending consequences
- Your role: optimize training, nutrition, sleep, and legal supplementation (creatine, collagen peptides, caffeine, beta-alanine) to the highest possible level
- If the athlete is working with a physician who has prescribed a peptide for a legitimate medical condition, they may be able to apply for a Therapeutic Use Exemption (TUE). This is exclusively a medical and regulatory process — do not advise on TUE applications

*Non-Tested Athletes:*
- Programming principles are the same as for the body recomposition or injured athlete archetypes
- Additional consideration: competitive athletes have higher training volumes and intensities, which may both enhance and complicate peptide responses
- GH secretagogues: athletes training 2x/day may benefit from enhanced recovery. Program adequate sleep (8–9 hours) to maximize nocturnal GH pulse
- Healing peptides: for athletes managing chronic overuse injuries while continuing to train, a conservative approach with modified training volume in affected areas is essential

*Coach's Red Flags:*
- Any athlete in a tested sport asking you to help them "use peptides without getting caught" → refuse unequivocally. This is an ethical and legal line you cannot cross
- Signs of peptide misuse: rapid unexplained body composition changes, acromegalic features (thickened jaw, enlarged hands/feet from chronic GH excess), persistent edema
- Stacking multiple compounds without physician oversight

#### 7.2.6 The Post-Menopausal Client

**Profile:** Women aged 45–65 experiencing perimenopause or post-menopause, dealing with declining estrogen, increased visceral fat, reduced bone density, and loss of lean mass. May be using HRT (hormone replacement therapy) alongside peptides: GH secretagogues for body composition, BPC-157 for joint health, collagen peptides for connective tissue.

**Programming Framework:**

*Training (3–4x/week):*
- **Resistance training is essential** — this population loses muscle and bone density at an accelerated rate post-menopause
- Emphasize progressive overload with compound movements
- Include impact loading for bone health: jumping, step-ups, stair climbing (if cleared by physician)
- Balance and stability work: single-leg exercises, proprioceptive challenges
- Minimum 2x/week resistance training; ideally 3x with a day of yoga/flexibility

*Nutrition:*
- Protein: 1.6–2.0 g/kg/day (post-menopausal women have increased protein needs for muscle maintenance)
- Calcium: 1,200 mg/day (food sources preferred: dairy, leafy greens, fortified foods)
- Vitamin D: 2,000–4,000 IU/day (verify with blood work; target 25-OH-D of 40–60 ng/mL)
- Omega-3 fatty acids: anti-inflammatory support
- Collagen peptides: 10–15 g/day (evidence supports benefits for joint pain and skin elasticity in this population — Zdzieblik et al., *Nutrients*, 2015; DOI: 10.3390/nu7075239)

*Peptide-Specific Considerations:*
- GH secretagogues may partially compensate for the decline in GH that accompanies menopause. Monitor IGF-1 closely — post-menopausal women may be more sensitive to GH effects
- MK-677 (oral GH secretagogue) has specific data in elderly populations showing increased lean mass and improved sleep. Nass et al. (*Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism*, 2008; DOI: 10.1210/jc.2007-2752) showed improved body composition in healthy elderly subjects. However, MK-677 also increases appetite (ghrelin mimetic) — this must be managed nutritionally
- BPC-157 for joint maintenance: low-dose protocols are common in this population for generalized joint health. Evidence is preclinical; program standard joint-protective training practices regardless

*Monitoring:*
- DEXA scan annually: track both lean mass and bone mineral density
- Balance assessment quarterly (functional reach test, single-leg stand)
- Blood work: hormonal panel (estradiol, testosterone, DHEA-S, SHBG), metabolic markers, IGF-1, 25-OH vitamin D, calcium, PTH

#### 7.2.7 The Young Lifter (Under 25)

**Profile:** College-aged or younger individual, typically male, interested in peptides for muscle growth and physique development. This archetype requires the most cautious approach.

**The Coach's Position:**

**Most peptides are inappropriate for individuals under 25.** The rationale is straightforward:

1. The hypothalamic-pituitary axis is still maturing. Exogenous manipulation of GH signaling during this period carries unknown risks to natural endocrine development
2. Young individuals have the highest natural GH production of their lives. The marginal benefit of a GH secretagogue is lowest in this population
3. Growth plates may still be open in individuals under 21. IGF-1 elevation from GH secretagogues could theoretically affect bone growth — the risk is largely theoretical but non-trivial
4. The long-term effects of peptide use initiated in youth are completely unknown. There are zero long-term studies
5. The young lifter can achieve dramatic results through training, nutrition, and sleep optimization alone. Peptides offer diminishing returns in a population that responds maximally to basic interventions

**What to do with this client:**
- Redirect their enthusiasm toward mastering the fundamentals: progressive overload, nutrition periodization, sleep hygiene, and stress management
- Educate them honestly: "Your natural hormone profile is better right now than any peptide could make it. The best thing you can do is protect it by training hard, eating well, and sleeping 8+ hours"
- If they are already using peptides (especially SARMs, which are popular in this demographic), do not refuse to coach them, but document that you recommended against it and referred them to a physician
- If they insist on using peptides: refer to a physician for medical evaluation and supervision. Do not assist with protocol design for this population

### 7.3 Protocol Design Principles

#### 7.3.1 Start Low, Titrate Slow

This principle applies universally across peptide classes and is the single most important safety guideline in peptide protocol design.

**Why it matters:** Peptide responses are dose-dependent and individually variable. A dose that produces optimal results in one person may cause significant side effects in another due to differences in receptor density, metabolic clearance, body composition, and baseline hormonal status.

**Practical application:**
- GH secretagogues: common starting doses in clinical practice are often 50–60% of the "typical" dose reported online. For CJC-1295/ipamorelin, many physicians start at 100 mcg of each (rather than the commonly cited 200–300 mcg) and titrate based on IGF-1 response and side effects at 4-week intervals
- Semaglutide: the FDA-approved titration schedule exists for good reason — 0.25 mg weekly for 4 weeks, then 0.5 mg for 4 weeks, then 1.0 mg, progressing to 2.4 mg only if tolerated. Rushing titration increases GI side effects and dropout
- BPC-157: typical reported doses range from 200–500 mcg 1–2x daily. Starting at the lower end and assessing response over 2 weeks is standard practice

**As a coach, your role:** You do not set the dose. But you monitor the client's response to the dose the physician has set: training performance, recovery, subjective well-being, sleep quality, side effects. This data feeds back to the physician for dose adjustment decisions.

#### 7.3.2 Cycling and Desensitization Management

Receptor desensitization is a real concern with several peptide classes. Prolonged exposure to a receptor agonist can downregulate receptor density or sensitivity, reducing the compound's effectiveness over time.

**GH Secretagogues:**
- GHRP-class peptides (GHRP-2, GHRP-6, hexarelin) show the most pronounced desensitization. Hexarelin in particular loses efficacy after 4–8 weeks of continuous use
- Ipamorelin shows less desensitization than other GHRPs — one of the reasons for its popularity
- CJC-1295 (DAC version) provides sustained GHRH stimulation; whether this leads to desensitization is debated, but many clinicians cycle it (8–12 weeks on, 4 weeks off)
- MK-677 can be used continuously for longer periods based on the clinical trial data (Nass et al., 2008 used 12-month continuous dosing), though many practitioners still cycle it

**Common cycling approaches:**
- 5 days on, 2 days off (weekly micro-cycle — most common for GH secretagogues)
- 8–12 weeks on, 4 weeks off (standard macro-cycle)
- 6 months on, 2 months off (extended protocol for metabolic peptides)

**Programming implications:** If a client cycles off a GH secretagogue, you may notice a temporary dip in recovery capacity. Plan a deload week or reduce training volume during the first 1–2 weeks of the off-cycle to match the reduced recovery support.

#### 7.3.3 Stacking Logic: Synergistic vs. Redundant Combinations

The most common peptide stacks in practice follow a pharmacological logic: combining compounds that work through complementary pathways produces synergistic effects, while stacking compounds that work through the same pathway produces redundant (and potentially harmful) effects.

**Synergistic stacks (evidence-supported logic):**
- CJC-1295 (GHRH analog) + Ipamorelin (GHRP): complementary receptor activation. GHRH stimulates GH release; ghrelin mimetics amplify the pulse. This is the most widely used GH secretagogue stack and the combination with the strongest pharmacological rationale
- BPC-157 + TB-500: different healing mechanisms (BPC-157: growth factor upregulation and NO modulation; TB-500: actin sequestration and cell migration). Combined in practice for comprehensive tissue repair
- GH secretagogue + BPC-157: GH supports systemic recovery; BPC-157 targets local tissue repair

**Redundant stacks (avoid):**
- Two GHRH analogs simultaneously (e.g., CJC-1295 + sermorelin): both act on the same GHRH receptor. Redundant, not synergistic
- Two GHRPs simultaneously (e.g., ipamorelin + GHRP-6): both act on the GHS-R1a receptor. Adding a second GHRP does not amplify the signal; it competes for the same binding site
- Multiple GLP-1 agonists: never combine semaglutide with liraglutide or tirzepatide. These act on the same receptor family and combining them dramatically increases side effect risk with no benefit

**Coach's role:** You do not design stacks. But you should understand the logic behind your client's stack so you can program appropriately. If a client reports that their physician added BPC-157 to their existing CJC/ipamorelin protocol for a nagging shoulder injury, you understand what each compound targets and can adjust training accordingly.

#### 7.3.4 Timing: Training, Sleep, and Meal Considerations

Peptide timing relative to meals, training, and sleep significantly affects efficacy.

**GH Secretagogues (all types):**
- Administer on an empty stomach — insulin and blood glucose blunt GH release
- Most common timing: before bed (enhances the natural nocturnal GH surge) or first thing in the morning (fasted)
- If using 2x/day: morning (fasted) and bedtime
- Do not inject within 1.5–2 hours of a meal
- **Training timing:** train 3–6 hours before the evening injection. The training stimulus primes the GH axis; the peptide amplifies the recovery response during sleep

**BPC-157 and TB-500:**
- Can be administered with or without food (these are not GH-axis compounds)
- For localized injuries: inject subcutaneously near the injury site
- Timing: consistent daily timing matters more than meal timing. Morning or evening are both acceptable
- **Training timing:** no specific timing constraints relative to training. Some practitioners inject post-training near the worked tissue to capitalize on the inflammatory and repair signaling already active

**GLP-1 Agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide):**
- Once-weekly injection; day of week does not matter but should be consistent
- No meal timing restrictions
- **Training timing:** train on days when nausea is lowest. Many clients report peak nausea 24–48 hours post-injection and improvement by days 4–7. Schedule the most demanding training sessions for the second half of the week if injection is early-week

**MK-677:**
- Oral, once daily. Most practitioners recommend evening dosing because:
  1. MK-677 increases appetite (ghrelin mimetic); evening dosing means the appetite surge occurs during sleep
  2. GH release is complemented by the natural nocturnal pulse
  3. Some users report drowsiness, which is beneficial at bedtime
- Take on an empty stomach or with a light meal (less stringent than injectable GH secretagogues)

### 7.4 Blood Work: What to Order, When, and How to Read It

Blood work is the objective anchor of peptide-informed coaching. Subjective improvements in energy, sleep, and recovery are meaningful but insufficient. Blood work reveals what is actually happening at the physiological level.

**Important scope-of-practice note:** You can recommend blood work categories and explain what the markers mean in general terms. You cannot interpret results for medical decisions — that is the physician's role. What you can do is review blood work for coaching-relevant context: Is IGF-1 elevated in a way that explains the client's improved recovery? Is fasting glucose trending up in a way that should inform their nutrition plan?

#### The Peptide-Informed Blood Panel

**Tier 1: Essential (every peptide client, every 12 weeks initially):**

| Marker | Why | Peptide Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| IGF-1 | Primary biomarker of GH axis activity | Directly reflects GH secretagogue response. Target: 200–300 ng/mL for adults. >350 warrants physician review |
| Fasting glucose | GH impairs insulin sensitivity | All GH secretagogues can elevate fasting glucose. Monitor closely |
| HbA1c | 3-month average glucose | Captures glucose trends that fasting glucose may miss |
| Fasting insulin | Insulin resistance screening | Elevated insulin with normal glucose = early insulin resistance |
| Complete metabolic panel (CMP) | Liver and kidney function | Baseline safety monitoring for any exogenous compound |
| CBC with differential | Immune and hematological baseline | General health screen; thymosin alpha-1 and other immune peptides affect WBC |

**Tier 2: Recommended (every 6 months, or as clinically indicated):**

| Marker | Why | Peptide Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Lipid panel (TC, LDL, HDL, TG, apoB) | Cardiovascular risk | GLP-1 agonists improve lipids; GH may transiently worsen them |
| Testosterone (total and free) | Hormonal context | GH secretagogues may mildly increase testosterone via IGF-1 signaling |
| Estradiol | Estrogenic context | Important in males on GH protocols (aromatase activity) |
| DHEA-S | Adrenal reserve | Declines with age; provides context for anti-aging protocols |
| Thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4) | Thyroid function | GH increases peripheral T4→T3 conversion; may unmask subclinical hypothyroidism |
| 25-OH Vitamin D | Bone health and immune function | Relevant for all clients, particularly post-menopausal women |

**Tier 3: Specialized (specific situations):**

| Marker | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| PSA | Males >40 on GH protocols | GH/IGF-1 are growth factors; prostate screening is prudent |
| Prolactin | On GHRP-2, GHRP-6, or hexarelin | These specific GHRPs can elevate prolactin |
| Cortisol (morning) | On GHRP-2, GHRP-6, or hexarelin | GHRP-2 and GHRP-6 can elevate cortisol; ipamorelin does not |
| GGT, FIB-4 | On tesamorelin or GH protocols | Liver health markers; tesamorelin has shown improvement in liver fat (Stanley et al., *JAMA*, 2014; DOI: 10.1001/jama.2014.8334) |
| Hemoglobin A1c + fasting insulin (repeated) | On MK-677 long-term | MK-677 has the most pronounced effect on glucose metabolism among GH secretagogues |

#### Blood Work Timeline

| Timepoint | What to Check | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Baseline (before peptide initiation) | Tier 1 + Tier 2 complete panel | Establish true baseline for all markers |
| 6 weeks | Tier 1 only | Early safety check; catch glucose issues early |
| 12 weeks | Tier 1 + Tier 2 | Full reassessment; first meaningful efficacy data point |
| 6 months | Tier 1 + Tier 2 + any Tier 3 indicated | Comprehensive review; dose adjustment opportunity |
| Annually thereafter | Full panel | Ongoing monitoring |

### 7.5 Progress Tracking and Outcome Measurement

Peptide-informed coaching demands more rigorous tracking than standard fitness coaching because you are monitoring the integrated effect of pharmacological and lifestyle interventions.

#### Body Composition

**Gold standard: DEXA scan.** Provides accurate measurement of lean mass, fat mass, bone mineral density, and regional distribution. Recommend every 8–12 weeks during active peptide protocols.

**Practical alternative: multisite skinfold measurements.** Less accurate than DEXA but more accessible, cheaper, and useful for trend tracking when performed by the same technician using the same protocol (e.g., Jackson-Pollock 7-site).

**Scale weight alone is insufficient.** A GLP-1 client losing 2 lb/week on the scale may be losing 1.2 lb of fat and 0.8 lb of muscle — the scale says "great progress" but the body composition trajectory is concerning.

**Waist circumference:** Simple, free, and specifically relevant for clients on tesamorelin or GH secretagogues targeting visceral fat. Measure at the navel, relaxed, first thing in the morning. Track weekly.

#### Strength Metrics

Track 3–5 key lifts and monitor trends:
- Upper body push (bench press or overhead press)
- Upper body pull (barbell row or weighted pull-up)
- Lower body (squat or leg press)
- Hip hinge (deadlift or Romanian deadlift)
- Carry or grip (farmer's walk or dead hang)

Strength trending upward = lean mass is at minimum being maintained. Strength trending downward for >2 weeks = red flag for lean mass loss (especially in GLP-1 clients).

#### Recovery Metrics

- **Resting heart rate (RHR):** Track via wearable. A rising RHR trend may indicate overtraining, poor sleep, or illness
- **Heart rate variability (HRV):** Higher HRV generally indicates better recovery status. Clients on GH secretagogues often report improved HRV — track to confirm
- **Sleep quality:** Subjective (1–10 rating) and objective (wearable sleep tracking). GH secretagogues and DSIP are expected to improve sleep metrics
- **Rate of perceived exertion (RPE):** Track RPE for key sets. If the same weight at the same reps feels harder over consecutive sessions, recovery may be compromised

#### Subjective Wellness

Use a simple weekly check-in form:
- Energy (1–10)
- Sleep quality (1–10)
- Mood (1–10)
- Joint pain/discomfort (0–10, with location)
- GI symptoms (relevant for GLP-1 clients)
- Any new or unusual symptoms (open text)

This data, aggregated over weeks, reveals patterns that neither you nor the client would notice from session to session.

#### Progress Reports for Physicians

One of the most valuable services a peptide-informed coach can provide is a structured progress report for the client's prescribing physician. Include:

1. Body composition trends (weight, waist circumference, DEXA results if available)
2. Strength progression data
3. Relevant subjective observations (sleep improvement, energy changes, side effects)
4. Nutrition summary (average daily protein, caloric intake)
5. Training summary (frequency, volume, adherence)
6. Any concerns or red flags observed

This report elevates the physician's ability to make informed protocol adjustments and positions you as a professional collaborator rather than a disconnected service provider.

### 7.6 When to Refer: Red Flags and Scope Boundaries

Knowing when to refer is as important as knowing what to program. These red flags require immediate referral to the client's physician:

#### Immediate Referral (Same Day)

- Fasting glucose >126 mg/dL or HbA1c >6.5% (diagnostic threshold for diabetes)
- Chest pain, shortness of breath, or irregular heartbeat during or after training
- Severe headaches that are new or different from baseline
- Visual changes (blurred vision, visual field defects)
- Signs of injection site infection: spreading redness, warmth, pus, fever
- Acute joint swelling disproportionate to training load
- Numbness or tingling in extremities (carpal tunnel symptoms — can occur with elevated GH/IGF-1)
- Psychiatric changes: new anxiety, depression, or mood instability (possible with multiple compound stacks)

#### Prompt Referral (Within 1 Week)

- Fasting glucose consistently 100–125 mg/dL (prediabetic range)
- Persistent edema (fluid retention) despite adequate hydration
- Persistent joint pain that does not respond to training modifications
- Skin changes: new moles, acanthosis nigricans (dark patches indicating insulin resistance)
- Unexplained weight gain despite adherence to nutrition plan
- Significant hair loss or thinning
- Menstrual irregularities in female clients
- Persistent GI issues beyond the expected GLP-1 adaptation period (>8 weeks)

#### Scope Boundary Situations

These are moments where the line between education and medical advice gets thin. Default to referral:

- Client asks: "Should I increase my dose?" → "That's a question for your physician. What I can tell you is that your recovery metrics and body composition are [improving/stagnating], which your physician can factor into their decision."
- Client asks: "Can I stop this peptide?" → "That's a medical decision. I can tell you what I've observed about your progress, and you should discuss that with your prescriber."
- Client reports symptoms they believe are from a peptide → "I want you to report that to your physician this week. In the meantime, I'll note it in our training log and we can adjust your training if needed."
- Client wants to add a new peptide to their stack → "I'm not in a position to recommend adding compounds. Let me share your current progress data with your physician, and they can evaluate whether an addition makes sense."

### 7.7 Building a Peptide-Informed Coaching Business

#### 7.7.1 Pricing Premium Services

Peptide-informed coaching is a premium service because it requires specialized knowledge that most trainers lack and delivers measurable results that most trainers cannot produce.

**Pricing framework:**
- **Standard personal training:** $60–120/session (commodity market, high competition)
- **Peptide-informed coaching:** $150–300/month (retainer model) or $200–400/session (premium session model)
- **Premium peptide-informed package:** $300–500/month including programming, nutrition, blood work review, progress reports, and physician communication

The retainer model works best because peptide coaching is inherently longitudinal — you are managing a protocol over months, not delivering isolated training sessions.

**Justification for premium pricing:**
- Specialized knowledge that requires ongoing education
- Blood work review and progress tracking that standard trainers do not provide
- Physician communication and collaboration
- Customized programming that accounts for pharmaceutical interventions
- Measurable outcomes: clients on peptide-informed coaching can quantify their results with body composition data, strength metrics, and blood work trends

#### 7.7.2 Marketing Compliantly (Avoiding FDA and FTC Issues)

**The legal landscape is strict.** The FDA regulates claims about drugs and supplements. The FTC regulates advertising claims. As a fitness coach marketing peptide-informed services, you must be meticulous:

**What you CAN say:**
- "I help clients optimize their training and nutrition around their physician-prescribed peptide protocols"
- "Peptide-informed coaching for body composition, recovery, and performance"
- "Specialized programming for clients on GLP-1 medications like semaglutide"
- "Evidence-based coaching that integrates with your medical team"

**What you CANNOT say:**
- "Peptides will [cure/treat/fix] your [condition]" → making drug claims without FDA approval
- "I prescribe/recommend [specific peptide] for [specific condition]" → practicing medicine
- "Guaranteed results" → FTC violation
- Before-and-after photos with claims attributed to peptides → FDA/FTC risk if the peptide is not FDA-approved for that use
- "My clients lose [X] pounds using peptide protocols" → implies you are the prescribing authority

**Content marketing strategy:**
- Write educational content about peptide science (what the research says, not what you recommend)
- Share general programming principles for clients on metabolic medications
- Discuss the importance of resistance training during GLP-1 therapy (well-supported by published research)
- Position yourself as the bridge between the physician and the gym
- Use disclaimers consistently: "This is educational content, not medical advice. Consult your physician before starting any peptide protocol."

#### 7.7.3 Building a Referral Network with Prescribers

The highest-leverage business development activity for a peptide-informed coach is building relationships with local prescribers.

**Target physicians:**
- Functional medicine practitioners
- Anti-aging and longevity medicine physicians
- Endocrinologists who are open to peptide therapy
- Integrative medicine practitioners
- Telehealth peptide clinics (for online coaching clients)

**How to build the relationship:**
1. **Lead with value.** Offer to provide structured progress reports for their patients who are also your clients. Physicians receive data that helps them make better decisions; you receive referrals
2. **Demonstrate competence.** Share your knowledge through a brief introductory meeting. Show that you understand the pharmacology, the evidence hierarchy, and — most importantly — the boundaries of your scope of practice
3. **Make it easy.** Create a standardized progress report template that you send monthly. Include only relevant metrics. Do not include medical advice or dose recommendations
4. **Co-educate.** Offer to co-host a workshop or webinar on exercise optimization during peptide therapy. The physician brings the medical credibility; you bring the programming expertise
5. **Reciprocate referrals.** When your clients need peptide prescriptions, refer to the physicians in your network. When their patients need programming, they refer to you

This mutual referral network is the foundation of a sustainable peptide-informed coaching business.

#### 7.7.4 Continuing Education and Staying Current

The peptide landscape evolves rapidly. New compounds, new clinical data, regulatory changes, and emerging safety signals require that you stay current.

**Recommended continuing education:**
- **Conferences:** American Academy of Anti-Aging Medicine (A4M), Age Management Medicine Group (AMMG), Metabolic Health Summit
- **Journals:** *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism*, *Obesity*, *The Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology*, *Diabetes Care*, *Nature Reviews Endocrinology*
- **Databases:** PubMed, Semantic Scholar (use the academic research tool to set up periodic searches on key topics)
- **Certifications:** Consider certifications in sports nutrition, functional medicine coaching, or integrative health coaching that provide a defensible credentialing framework for peptide-informed practice
- **Community:** Engage with evidence-based peptide education communities, but always evaluate claims against the evidence hierarchy in Chapter 1

**A practical system for staying current:**
- Set up monthly literature searches on PubMed for your key peptides (tesamorelin, semaglutide, BPC-157, ipamorelin)
- Read the abstract and methods section of any new RCT in your domain
- When a new compound enters your clients' conversations, do a systematic literature search before forming or sharing an opinion
- Maintain a personal evidence library: save key papers organized by peptide and topic, with your own notes on evidence level and coaching relevance
