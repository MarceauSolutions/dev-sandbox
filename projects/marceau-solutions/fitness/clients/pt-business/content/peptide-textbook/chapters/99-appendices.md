## APPENDIX A: COMPLETE PEPTIDE REFERENCE TABLE

- **Strong**: Multiple RCTs and/or meta-analyses in humans
- **Moderate**: Limited RCTs or well-designed observational studies in humans

- **Anecdotal**: Insufficient formal research; evidence is largely community-reported

Use this table to understand what markers are relevant and why, and to have informed conversations with clients and their physicians. Always defer to the client's physician for medical interpretation.

## APPENDIX C: RECONSTITUTION AND DOSING CALCULATOR

### C.1 Understanding Peptide Forms

Most injectable peptides are supplied as lyophilized (freeze-dried) powder in sealed vials. Before use, the powder must be reconstituted (dissolved) with a sterile solvent, typically bacteriostatic water (BAC water) containing 0.9% benzyl alcohol as a preservative.

**Key terms:**

- **Lyophilized:** Freeze-dried to remove water; produces a white or off-white powder cake or pellet in the vial
- **Bacteriostatic water (BAC water):** Sterile water with 0.9% benzyl alcohol preservative; standard reconstitution solvent; allows multi-use over ~28 days
- **Sterile water:** Water without preservative; must be used within 24 hours of reconstitution; single-use
- **Reconstitution:** The process of dissolving lyophilized peptide into solution for injection

### C.2 Step-by-Step Reconstitution Guide

**Materials needed:**
1. Lyophilized peptide vial
2. Bacteriostatic water vial
3. Insulin syringe (1 mL / 100 units) or appropriate syringe
4. Alcohol swabs
5. Clean, well-lit workspace

**Procedure:**

**Step 1:** Remove the plastic cap from both the peptide vial and the BAC water vial. Wipe the rubber stopper of each vial with a fresh alcohol swab. Allow to air dry for 10-15 seconds.

**Step 2:** Draw the desired volume of BAC water into the syringe. The amount you add determines the concentration (see concentration table below).

**Step 3:** Insert the needle into the peptide vial at an angle, piercing the rubber stopper. Slowly dispense the BAC water down the inside wall of the vial. Do NOT spray the water directly onto the lyophilized powder, as this can damage the peptide and cause foaming.

**Step 4:** Allow the water to gently dissolve the powder. Do NOT shake the vial. Gently swirl or roll the vial between your palms if needed. Most peptides dissolve within 1-5 minutes.

**Step 5:** Once fully dissolved, the solution should be clear and free of particles. If the solution is cloudy or contains visible particles, do not use it.

**Step 6:** Store the reconstituted vial in the refrigerator (36-46 degrees F / 2-8 degrees C). Use within 28 days when reconstituted with BAC water.

### C.3 Concentration and Dosing Math

The fundamental equation:

```
Concentration = Total peptide in vial / Volume of solvent added
```

**Example 1: BPC-157 (5 mg vial)**
- Vial contains: 5 mg (5,000 mcg) of BPC-157
- You add: 2 mL of BAC water
- Concentration: 5,000 mcg / 2 mL = 2,500 mcg per mL
- Since 1 mL = 100 units on an insulin syringe:
- Each unit (tick mark) = 2,500 / 100 = 25 mcg
- For a 250 mcg dose: 250 / 25 = 10 units on the insulin syringe

**Example 2: Ipamorelin (5 mg vial)**
- Vial contains: 5 mg (5,000 mcg)
- You add: 2.5 mL of BAC water
- Concentration: 5,000 / 2.5 = 2,000 mcg per mL
- Each unit on insulin syringe = 2,000 / 100 = 20 mcg
- For a 200 mcg dose: 200 / 20 = 10 units
- For a 300 mcg dose: 300 / 20 = 15 units

**Example 3: CJC-1295 no DAC (2 mg vial)**
- Vial contains: 2 mg (2,000 mcg)

- For a 100 mcg dose: 100 / 10 = 10 units

## APPENDIX E: GLOSSARY OF TERMS

**Actin** — A globular protein that forms microfilaments in the cell cytoskeleton. TB-500 sequesters G-actin monomers to promote cell migration and wound healing.

**ADME** — Abbreviation for Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion. The four stages of pharmacokinetics that determine how a drug moves through the body.

**Agonist** — A molecule that binds to a receptor and activates it, producing a biological response. Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist.

**Allometric Scaling** — A method of adjusting drug doses between species based on body surface area rather than body weight. Used to estimate human-equivalent doses from animal studies.

**AMPK (AMP-Activated Protein Kinase)** — A cellular energy sensor enzyme that is activated when cellular energy is low. MOTS-c activates AMPK. AMPK activation promotes fat oxidation, glucose uptake, and mitochondrial biogenesis.

**Anabolic** — Pertaining to the building up of complex molecules from simpler ones. Anabolic processes include muscle protein synthesis, bone formation, and tissue growth.

**Antagonist** — A molecule that binds to a receptor and blocks its activation, preventing the biological response. Somatostatin is a GH release antagonist.

**Autophagy** — A cellular process in which damaged organelles and proteins are degraded and recycled. Fasting and AMPK activation promote autophagy. Relevant to longevity peptide mechanisms.

**Bacteriostatic Water (BAC Water)** — Sterile water containing 0.9% benzyl alcohol as a preservative. The standard solvent for reconstituting lyophilized peptides. Allows multi-dose vial use for up to 28 days.

**Bioavailability** — The fraction of an administered dose that reaches systemic circulation in its active form. Subcutaneous peptide injection typically has 60-80% bioavailability. Oral peptide bioavailability is often below 1% without special formulation.

**Bioidentical** — Structurally identical to the molecule produced by the human body. Sermorelin is bioidentical to the first 29 amino acids of endogenous GHRH.

**BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor)** — A protein that supports the growth, differentiation, and survival of neurons. Semax and selank may increase BDNF expression.

**Catabolism** — The breakdown of complex molecules into simpler ones, releasing energy. Excessive cortisol promotes muscle catabolism.

**Certificate of Analysis (COA)** — A document from a third-party laboratory verifying the purity, potency, sterility, and composition of a peptide product.

**Compounding Pharmacy** — A pharmacy licensed to prepare customized medications based on individual prescriptions. 503A pharmacies compound for individual patients; 503B outsourcing facilities compound larger batches.

**Cytokine** — A small signaling protein involved in immune and inflammatory responses. Pro-inflammatory cytokines include TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, and IL-6. Anti-inflammatory peptides like BPC-157 and KPV modulate cytokine expression.

**DAC (Drug Affinity Complex)** — A chemical modification that enables a peptide to bind to serum albumin, dramatically extending its half-life. CJC-1295 with DAC has a half-life of approximately 8 days versus approximately 30 minutes without DAC.

**Desensitization (Tachyphylaxis)** — A reduction in receptor responsiveness following prolonged or repeated stimulation. Hexarelin is particularly prone to GHS-R1a desensitization. Many GH secretagogue protocols include cycling to mitigate desensitization.

**Diurnal** — Relating to a 24-hour cycle. Growth hormone secretion follows a diurnal pattern with the largest pulse occurring during deep sleep.

**DPP-4 (Dipeptidyl Peptidase-4)** — An enzyme that rapidly degrades native GLP-1 and GIP. GLP-1 agonists like semaglutide are engineered to resist DPP-4 degradation.

**Endogenous** — Originating within the body. Endogenous GH is the growth hormone your body produces naturally.

**Exogenous** — Originating outside the body. Exogenous peptide administration introduces peptide molecules from an external source.

**FAK-Paxillin Pathway** — A cellular signaling pathway involved in cell adhesion, migration, and tissue repair. BPC-157 is reported to activate this pathway, which may contribute to its wound-healing effects.

**Fibroblast** — A cell type responsible for producing collagen and other components of the extracellular matrix. GHK-Cu and BPC-157 stimulate fibroblast activity.

**GHS-R1a (Growth Hormone Secretagogue Receptor 1a)** — The receptor for ghrelin and synthetic growth hormone-releasing peptides (GHRPs). Also known as the ghrelin receptor. Expressed in the pituitary, hypothalamus, and other tissues.

**GHRH (Growth Hormone-Releasing Hormone)** — A 44-amino-acid peptide hormone produced by the hypothalamus that stimulates GH release from pituitary somatotrophs. Sermorelin, CJC-1295, and tesamorelin are GHRH analogs.

**GHRP (Growth Hormone-Releasing Peptide)** — Synthetic peptides that stimulate GH release via the GHS-R1a (ghrelin) receptor. Includes GHRP-2, GHRP-6, ipamorelin, and hexarelin.

**GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1)** — An incretin hormone produced by intestinal L-cells that stimulates insulin secretion, suppresses glucagon, slows gastric emptying, and reduces appetite. Semaglutide, tirzepatide, and liraglutide are GLP-1 receptor agonists.

**GIP (Glucose-Dependent Insulinotropic Polypeptide)** — An incretin hormone that enhances insulin secretion. Tirzepatide is a dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist.

**Half-Life** — The time required for the concentration of a substance in the body to decrease by 50%. Determines dosing frequency. Native GLP-1 has a half-life of approximately 2 minutes; semaglutide has a half-life of approximately 7 days.

**HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment of Insulin Resistance)** — A calculated index of insulin resistance using fasting glucose and fasting insulin: (glucose x insulin) / 405. Values below 1.5 indicate good insulin sensitivity.

**HPA Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal Axis)** — The neuroendocrine system governing cortisol production and the stress response. Some GHRPs (GHRP-2, GHRP-6) stimulate the HPA axis, transiently elevating cortisol.

**HPG Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal Axis)** — The neuroendocrine system governing testosterone and estrogen production. Kisspeptin is a master regulator of the HPG axis.

**IGF-1 (Insulin-Like Growth Factor 1)** — A peptide hormone produced primarily by the liver in response to GH stimulation. Mediates many of GH's anabolic and growth-promoting effects. Serum IGF-1 is the primary monitoring marker for GH secretagogue protocols.

**IGFBP (Insulin-Like Growth Factor Binding Protein)** — A family of proteins that bind IGF-1 and IGF-2 in circulation, modulating their bioavailability and activity. IGF-1 LR3 has reduced IGFBP binding, increasing its bioactive free fraction.

**In Vitro** — Experiments conducted outside a living organism, typically in cell cultures or test tubes. Latin for "in glass."

**In Vivo** — Experiments conducted in a living organism. Latin for "in the living."

**Intramuscular (IM)** — Administration by injection into muscle tissue. Some peptides (e.g., MGF for local muscle effects) are administered IM.

**Intranasal** — Administration via the nasal mucosa. Selank and semax are commonly administered intranasally.

**Lipidation** — Chemical modification of a peptide by attaching a fatty acid chain. Semaglutide uses C18 fatty acid lipidation to bind albumin, extending its half-life.

**Lyophilized** — Freeze-dried. Peptides are commonly supplied in lyophilized form for stability during storage. Must be reconstituted before use.

**MC4R (Melanocortin-4 Receptor)** — A receptor in the central nervous system involved in appetite regulation, energy homeostasis, and sexual function. PT-141/bremelanotide activates MC4R.

**Melanocortin** — A family of peptide hormones derived from proopiomelanocortin (POMC) that activate melanocortin receptors (MC1R through MC5R). Melanotan II is a synthetic melanocortin analog.

**mTOR (Mechanistic Target of Rapamycin)** — A kinase that serves as a master regulator of cell growth, protein synthesis, and autophagy. IGF-1 and insulin activate mTOR. The mTOR pathway is central to muscle hypertrophy.

**Myostatin (GDF-8)** — A protein that acts as a negative regulator of muscle growth. Follistatin binds and neutralizes myostatin, removing the brake on muscle hypertrophy.

**NAD+ (Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide)** — A coenzyme essential for cellular energy metabolism, DNA repair, and sirtuin activation. NAD+ levels decline with age. NMN and NR are precursors that boost NAD+ levels.

**Negative Feedback** — A regulatory mechanism in which the output of a system inhibits its own production. IGF-1 exerts negative feedback on GH secretion via the hypothalamus and pituitary.

**NF-kB (Nuclear Factor Kappa-B)** — A transcription factor that plays a central role in the inflammatory response. KPV and BPC-157 are reported to inhibit NF-kB activation.

**Off-Label Use** — The use of an FDA-approved drug for an indication, age group, dosage, or route of administration that is not specified in the approved labeling. Tesamorelin prescribed for non-HIV visceral fat reduction is off-label use.

**PEGylation** — The covalent attachment of polyethylene glycol (PEG) chains to a peptide or protein to increase its stability, reduce immunogenicity, and extend its half-life. PEG-MGF is PEGylated mechano growth factor.

**Pharmacodynamics** — The study of what a drug does to the body: its mechanism of action, therapeutic effects, and side effects.

**Pharmacokinetics** — The study of what the body does to a drug: absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME).

**Proteolysis** — The enzymatic breakdown of proteins and peptides into smaller fragments or amino acids. Proteolysis limits the oral bioavailability of most peptides.

**Pulsatility** — The pattern of hormone release in discrete bursts (pulses) rather than continuous secretion. Growth hormone is released in a pulsatile pattern, with the largest pulse during deep sleep. GHRH analogs and GHRPs promote pulsatile GH release, while CJC-1295 with DAC produces a more continuous (blunted pulsatility) pattern.

**Receptor** — A protein molecule on the surface of or within a cell that binds to a specific molecule (ligand) and initiates a cellular response.

**Satellite Cell** — A type of muscle stem cell that resides between the basal lamina and sarcolemma of muscle fibers. When activated by mechanical stress or growth factors (including MGF and IGF-1), satellite cells proliferate and fuse with existing muscle fibers to support repair and hypertrophy.

**Senescence (Cellular)** — A state in which cells permanently cease dividing but remain metabolically active and secrete pro-inflammatory factors (the senescence-associated secretory phenotype, or SASP). FOXO4-DRI targets senescent cells for apoptosis.

**Senolytic** — An agent that selectively kills senescent cells. FOXO4-DRI is a senolytic peptide.

**SARM (Selective Androgen Receptor Modulator)** — A class of non-steroidal compounds that selectively activate androgen receptors in muscle and bone with reduced activity in prostate and skin. Not peptides, but frequently discussed alongside them.

**Somatotroph** — A cell type in the anterior pituitary gland that produces and secretes growth hormone. GHRH and GHRPs act on somatotrophs to stimulate GH release.

**Somatostatin** — A peptide hormone produced by the hypothalamus (and other tissues) that inhibits GH release. Somatostatin acts as the "brake" in the GH regulatory system. GH secretagogue protocols often involve timing around somatostatin troughs to maximize GH pulse amplitude.

**Subcutaneous (SubQ)** — Administration by injection into the tissue layer between the skin and muscle. The most common route for peptide administration. Injection sites include abdominal fat, thigh, and upper arm.

**Tachyphylaxis** — Rapid decrease in the response to a drug after repeated doses over a short period. Hexarelin demonstrates significant tachyphylaxis of GH release. Also known as acute tolerance or desensitization.

**Telomerase** — An enzyme that adds telomeric DNA repeats to the ends of chromosomes, counteracting telomere shortening. Epithalon reportedly activates telomerase.

**Telomere** — Repetitive DNA sequences (TTAGGG in humans) at the ends of chromosomes that protect genomic integrity. Telomeres shorten with each cell division and are considered a biomarker of biological aging.

**TGF-beta (Transforming Growth Factor Beta)** — A superfamily of signaling proteins involved in cell growth, differentiation, and tissue repair. Myostatin (GDF-8) is a member of the TGF-beta superfamily.

**Titration** — The process of gradually adjusting a dose upward (or downward) to find the optimal therapeutic dose while minimizing side effects. GLP-1 agonists require slow upward titration to manage GI side effects.

**VEGF (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor)** — A signaling protein that promotes angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation). BPC-157 is reported to upregulate VEGF, which may contribute to its tissue repair effects.

**WADA (World Anti-Doping Agency)** — The international organization that maintains the prohibited substances list for competitive sport. Many peptides discussed in this textbook, including all GH secretagogues and IGF-1 variants, are prohibited by WADA.

### G.2 Peptide Contraindication Screening Checklist

**PEPTIDE CONTRAINDICATION SCREENING**

*This checklist is for the fitness coach's reference when discussing peptide-informed programming with clients. It does NOT replace medical screening by a licensed provider.*

**Absolute Contraindications (Refer to Physician Before Proceeding):**

[ ] Active cancer or malignancy of any type
[ ] History of cancer within the past 5 years (especially for GH secretagogues and IGF-1)
[ ] Pregnancy or planning to become pregnant
[ ] Currently breastfeeding/nursing
[ ] Active diabetic retinopathy (risk with GH-related peptides)
[ ] Uncontrolled diabetes (HbA1c > 9%)
[ ] Active pituitary tumor or history of pituitary adenoma
[ ] Known allergy to any peptide component
[ ] Severe renal impairment (eGFR < 30)
[ ] Severe hepatic impairment
[ ] Personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (contraindication for GLP-1 agonists)
[ ] Personal or family history of MEN2 syndrome (contraindication for GLP-1 agonists)
[ ] History of pancreatitis (relative contraindication for GLP-1 agonists)

**Relative Contraindications (Require Medical Clearance and Monitoring):**

[ ] Prediabetes or insulin resistance (monitor glucose with GH secretagogues)
[ ] History of carpal tunnel syndrome (GH-related peptides may exacerbate)
[ ] Edema or fluid retention conditions
[ ] History of intracranial hypertension
[ ] Hypothyroidism (GH secretagogues may unmask or exacerbate)
[ ] Scoliosis (in younger clients using GH-related peptides)
[ ] History of gastroparesis (GLP-1 agonists worsen gastric emptying)
[ ] History of gallbladder disease (GLP-1 agonists increase gallstone risk)
[ ] Currently taking blood thinners (injection-related bleeding)
[ ] History of eating disorder (GLP-1 agonist appetite suppression may complicate)
[ ] Age under 25 (GH axis still maturing; most peptides inappropriate)
[ ] Age over 65 (increased sensitivity to side effects; lower starting doses)
[ ] Competitive athlete subject to WADA testing (many peptides are prohibited)
