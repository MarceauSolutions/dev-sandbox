## 1. Statutory framework — what Florida law requires of you

### Primary governing statutes and rules

- **FS 489.105(4)** — Definition of "Qualifying Agent": person who possesses the requisite skill, knowledge, and experience, and has the responsibility, to supervise, direct, manage, and control the contracting activities of the business organization with which they are connected
- **FS 489.119** — Business organizations; qualifying agents; rules to be observed
- **FS 489.127** — Prohibitions; unlicensed contracting penalties
- **FS 489.128** — Contracts entered into by unlicensed contractors unenforceable
- **FS 489.129** — Disciplinary proceedings
- **FS 489.131** — Applicability
- **FAC 61G4-15.001 through .008** — Construction Industry Licensing Board administrative rules on qualifying agents

### Your core legal responsibilities as Primary Qualifying Agent

Per FS 489.1195, you are responsible for **all** of the following:

1. **Supervision** of the construction activities of Marceau Solutions
2. **Compliance** with the Florida Building Code and all applicable building, fire, electrical, plumbing, and mechanical codes
3. **Permits** — applying for, displaying, and closing out building permits issued in the name of the business organization
4. **Inspections** — being present for or arranging for proper inspections during work
5. **Project supervision** of all field activities, including those performed by employees or subcontractors
6. **Financial responsibility** — ensuring proper financial responsibility of the business organization
7. **Standards of practice** — adherence to all Florida construction industry standards
8. **Customer contracts** — ensuring all contracts comply with FL requirements (mandatory disclosures, notice of right to rescind, etc.)
9. **Public records compliance** — proper notice of commencement, certificates of completion, lien releases, etc.

### What "supervision" specifically means under Florida law

Per FAC 61G4-15.001 and case law:

- **Active and continuous supervision** of construction activities
- Not necessarily present at every job site, but having **meaningful involvement** in decisions
- Available to respond to inspectors, customers, and DBPR investigations
- Authority to direct or correct work performed
- Periodic on-site presence at jobs in progress
- Review and approval of contracts, change orders, and plans

**What DOES NOT count as supervision** (red flags that get qualifying business arrangements revoked):

- Lending your license name to someone else to operate without your real involvement
- Signing permits you have not reviewed
- Failing to address known code violations
- Inability to respond to inspection or DBPR communications
- Not knowing who your business's customers are or what work has been done

## 2. Long Island, NY → Florida supervision considerations

You are a non-resident qualifying agent. This is legally permitted in Florida but introduces specific risks.

### What FL DBPR scrutinizes for out-of-state QAs

DBPR has been increasingly aggressive about pursuing arrangements where the qualifying agent is geographically distant from the business and unable to provide genuine supervision. Recent enforcement actions have targeted:

- QAs who lend names for monthly fees without real involvement (criminal — FS 489.127)
- QAs who cannot demonstrate physical involvement during DBPR investigation
- Business organizations whose work product reflects clear lack of qualified supervision

For an out-of-state QA, DBPR may request **evidence of supervision** at the qualifying business application stage AND during subsequent investigations:

- Visit logs to Florida
- Documentation of communications with operating personnel (William Jr.)
- Permits signed by the QA
- Job sign-offs / approval records
- Customer complaint response records

### Realistic supervision framework for your situation

To maintain genuine supervision from Long Island, the operational discipline below is what must happen:

| Element | Requirement | Estimated time commitment |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-quote review** | William Jr. texts/emails every quote to you for review before sending to customer | 5-10 min/job, ~3-5 jobs/week initially |
| **Permit applications** | All Florida building permits applied for in YOUR name with your contractor license; reviewed by you before submission | 15-30 min/permit, varies |
| **Photo job review** | Before/after photos of every job texted to you for sign-off (creates audit trail) | 5 min/job |
| **Daily availability** | Phone reachable during William Jr.'s field hours (typical 8am-7pm ET = 8am-7pm Naples time) for live decisions | On-call basis |
| **Monthly Florida visits** | Minimum monthly trip to Florida, at least 2-3 days, with documented ride-alongs on jobs | 3-4 days/month travel + cost |
| **Decision/communication log** | Maintain a shared log of decisions, approvals, and supervision events | 5 min/week |
| **DBPR/inspector contact** | YOU respond to any DBPR or inspector communications, not William Jr. | As needed |
| **Annual CEUs** | Maintain 14 hours every 2 years for license renewal | 14 hours every 2 years |
| **License renewal** | $255 fee + CEUs, due 8/31 even-numbered years | $255 + time |

### Long Island → Florida travel cost analysis

| Travel pattern | Annual cost (flights + ground) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly trip (12/year) | $2,400 – $4,800 | Most defensible from supervision-adequacy standpoint |
| Quarterly trip (4/year) + as-needed | $1,200 – $2,400 | Likely insufficient if work volume scales beyond 2-3 jobs/week |
| Semi-annual (2/year) | $400 – $800 | Insufficient — DBPR likely to view as inadequate |
| Driving from Long Island | ~$300 fuel + 40+ hours each round trip | Time prohibitive for monthly cadence |

**Recommendation:** Budget monthly Florida trips. This is the minimum cadence that supports the supervision narrative AND keeps you connected to the business reality.

## 3. Personal liability exposure

This is where the financial risk becomes real for you personally.

### License-related liability

| Risk | Outcome | Financial impact |
|---|---|---|
| **DBPR discipline** (failure to supervise, code violation, complaint) | License suspension or revocation | License lost; income from the business pivots ended; reputation damage |
| **License revocation** | Cannot reapply for ~5 years; current business loses qualifier and cannot operate | Total loss of the HVAC business |
| **Administrative fines** | DBPR can impose fines up to $10,000+ per violation | Out of pocket |
| **Civil penalties** | Per FS 489.127 — unlicensed contracting can result in civil penalties up to $5,000 per occurrence | Out of pocket |
| **Criminal charges** for willful unlicensed contracting | First offense: first-degree misdemeanor. Second offense or amounts >$500: third-degree felony | Legal defense + potential conviction |

### Civil liability from customer issues

| Risk | Outcome | Financial impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer property damage** | Lawsuit, claim against general liability insurance | Insurance covers up to policy limits; you personally liable for excess |
| **Customer injury on job** | Lawsuit, claim against GL | Same — insurance to limits, personal liability for excess |
| **Refrigerant leak / environmental claim** | EPA violation, environmental claim | Insurance may not cover; EPA fines up to $25,000+/day |
| **Faulty installation causing fire / flood** | Major property loss claim | High-dollar, could exceed standard policy limits |
| **Customer fraud claim** (e.g., billing for unperformed work) | Civil suit + DBPR complaint | Insurance may exclude; reputation damage |
| **Workers comp claim if exemption improperly applied** | Out-of-pocket medical and indemnity costs | Could be tens of thousands |

### Personal asset exposure

The LLC provides some protection (Marceau Solutions LLC liabilities are generally limited to the LLC's assets), BUT:

- **Your license is YOUR personal asset**, not the LLC's. DBPR can discipline YOU even if the LLC is named in the complaint.
- **Personal guarantee on lease, equipment financing, or business credit** would expose your personal assets directly.
- **"Piercing the corporate veil"** — courts can disregard LLC protection if the LLC is found to be improperly operated, undercapitalized, or commingled with personal finances.
- **Professional liability** (errors and omissions) — your individual license holder status creates personal exposure beyond the LLC.

### What insurance does and does not cover

**General Liability ($300k BI / $50k PD minimum, recommend $1M):**
- Customer property damage from negligent work — COVERED
- Bodily injury to customers/third parties on job sites — COVERED
- Defense costs for covered claims — COVERED
- Intentional acts (fraud, etc.) — NOT COVERED
- Workers' injuries (that's workers comp) — NOT COVERED
- Damage to your own work product — NOT COVERED (separate inland marine policy)
- Pollution/environmental claims — NOT COVERED (typically requires endorsement)
- Professional liability (errors/omissions) — NOT COVERED

**Workers Compensation:**
- William Jr. as sole operator can file for exemption (saves premium)
- But if exemption is improperly filed or William hires help without WC coverage, you (as QA) are personally exposed to injury claims

**Surety Bond (~$10k bond, ~$200/yr premium):**
- Required for licensure in some FL municipalities
- Not insurance — pays the BOND value to a customer who wins a claim, and then bond company comes after YOU personally to recover

## 4. Worst-case scenarios

These are real situations that have happened to FL HVAC qualifying agents. Use them to gut-check whether the upside outweighs the risk.

### Scenario A: Customer files DBPR complaint for incomplete or substandard work

- William Jr. completes a job; customer is unhappy
- Customer files complaint with DBPR
- DBPR investigates → looks at your supervision evidence
- If supervision evidence is thin (you weren't around, no permits in your name, no quotes reviewed by you), DBPR escalates
- Possible outcomes: warning, fine, suspension, or revocation

**Mitigation:** Documented supervision (quotes reviewed, permits in your name, monthly visit logs, photo job approvals)

### Scenario B: Customer is injured on job site

- William Jr. or a helper accidentally damages something / injures customer
- Customer sues Marceau Solutions LLC AND you personally as license holder
- GL insurance defends but coverage may be inadequate
- Out-of-pocket exposure for excess + defense costs

**Mitigation:** $1M GL coverage minimum (not just $300k DBPR minimum), proper safety training for William, documented job procedures

### Scenario C: William Jr. performs work outside your supervision

- William starts taking jobs while you're in Long Island
- Skips quote-review step, doesn't loop you in
- DBPR discovers via complaint or audit
- DBPR concludes you're not supervising → license revocation

**Mitigation:** Hard operational rules — NO job proceeds without your review. Make the rule absolute.

### Scenario D: Code violation on installed work fails inspection

- Permitted job fails inspection (e.g., improper refrigerant line sizing, code violation)
- Inspector flags it; multiple failures accumulate
- DBPR sees pattern of code issues under your license
- Discipline triggered

**Mitigation:** Pre-installation review for any significant work; conservative job selection initially (service calls first, install work only when you can be present)

### Scenario E: Refrigerant violation (EPA Section 608)

- William Jr. handles refrigerant without proper EPA 608 cert OR violates handling protocols
- EPA fines and DBPR complaint
- License consequences

**Mitigation:** William Jr. completes EPA 608 Universal certification BEFORE any refrigerant work

### Scenario F: William Jr. cannot fulfill obligations

- Job goes sideways, William Jr. unable to complete or unwilling to fix
- You as QA must step in OR risk license discipline
- Customer demands resolution from licensed contractor (you)
- You're 1,200 miles away — must travel + remediate at your cost

**Mitigation:** Clear written agreement between you and William Jr. about responsibilities, escalation, and what happens if William cannot complete a job

## 5. Operational protections to put in place BEFORE first job

### A. Written Operating Agreement for Marceau Solutions LLC

The Operating Agreement should explicitly address:
- William Jr.'s role as sole owner/operator
- Your role as Qualifying Agent and the scope of your supervisory authority
- William Jr.'s obligation to follow your direction on contracting matters
- Your right to suspend operations if supervision becomes compromised
- Indemnification clause where William Jr. holds you harmless for his unsupervised acts (limited — won't override license discipline)
- Compensation structure if any (most family-arrangement QAs serve without separate fee, but document it either way)

### B. Documented supervision protocols

- Quote pre-approval (William texts you every quote; you reply approve or revise; saved as text history)
- Permit applications in YOUR name (you sign the permit application personally or via secure remote signing)
- Photo before-and-after for every job (William sends, you confirm)
- Job log with date, address, customer name, work performed, your approval status

### C. Insurance coverage above DBPR minimums

- General Liability: **$1,000,000** per occurrence, $2,000,000 aggregate (not the $300k DBPR minimum)
- Tools and equipment coverage if buying inventory (Inland Marine)
- Commercial Auto endorsement on William's SUV (required before any paid job)
- Pollution liability endorsement (refrigerant releases)
- Optional: Professional liability (E&O) — adds another $500-1,000/year but covers judgment-based claims

### D. Documented financial separation

- Dedicated business checking account for Marceau Solutions HVAC operations
- ALL revenue flows into business account
- ALL business expenses paid from business account
- Personal vs. business expenses NEVER commingled (defends against veil-piercing)

### E. Emergency exit clause

You should be able to revoke your QA status at any time. Florida law requires you to file DBPR notice to terminate the qualifying agent designation. The business then has 60 days to find a replacement QA or wind down contracting activities.

**Specific triggers that should automatically trigger your exit:**
- William Jr. takes a job without your pre-approval
- Pattern of customer complaints
- Failed inspections / pattern of code issues
- Insurance lapse
- William's W-2 job takes over and he cannot fulfill operational obligations
