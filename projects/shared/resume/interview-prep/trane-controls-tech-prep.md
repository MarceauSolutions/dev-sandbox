## 6. Technical Q&A — 15 likely questions, prepared answers

### Q1 — "Walk me through a PID control loop."

> A PID loop has three terms: proportional (response to current error), integral (response to accumulated error over time), and derivative (response to rate of change of error). Proportional gives you fast response but leaves steady-state offset. Integral eliminates offset by integrating error over time but introduces lag and can cause overshoot. Derivative dampens overshoot by anticipating where the error is going. In HVAC, you'd typically see PI control on chilled water valves, room temperature loops, and AHU discharge air temperature. Pure P or PI is often enough — D is sensitive to noisy measurements and often disabled in field tuning. I taught these labs at FGCU for three years.

### Q2 — "What's BACnet and why does it matter?"

> BACnet is the dominant building automation protocol — ASHRAE standard 135, used industry-wide for HVAC, lighting, fire, access. Two main flavors: BACnet/IP over Ethernet for backbone communication, and BACnet MS/TP over RS-485 for field-level devices. It defines standard object types — analog input, analog output, binary, schedule, trend log — so equipment from different manufacturers can interoperate. The reason it matters for a controls tech: you're constantly working in a mixed-manufacturer environment, and BACnet is what lets Trane Tracer talk to a Daikin chiller, a Honeywell VFD, and a Siemens valve actuator in the same system.

### Q3 — "Describe the basic refrigeration cycle."

> Four stages, four components. Compressor takes low-pressure low-temperature vapor and discharges high-pressure high-temperature superheated vapor. Condenser rejects heat to the outdoors and the refrigerant condenses to high-pressure subcooled liquid. Metering device — TXV, electronic expansion valve, or cap tube — drops the pressure and the refrigerant flashes to low-pressure low-temperature mix. Evaporator absorbs heat from the conditioned space and the refrigerant boils back to low-pressure vapor. Back to the compressor. Two reference states matter for diagnostics: superheat at the evaporator outlet (target 8-12°F for residential TXV) tells you the evaporator is fully wet and the compressor is protected from liquid slugging. Subcooling at the condenser outlet (target similar range) tells you the condenser is fully condensed and the metering device is fed properly.

### Q4 — "Tracer SC+ — what do you know about it?"

> Tracer SC+ is Trane's current-generation supervisory BAS controller. Built on open protocols — BACnet, LonTalk, Modbus — so it integrates Trane and non-Trane equipment. It handles scheduling, alarming, trend logging, energy reporting, web-based access for facility managers. I haven't programmed it in production, but the architecture maps directly to the SCADA and PLC head-end logic I worked with at Collier. I'd expect to come up the Tracer-specific learning curve fast because the underlying point-mapping and sequence-of-operations work is the same discipline.

### Q5 — "What's the difference between BACnet and LonWorks?"

> Both are BAS protocols, but BACnet has won the open-standards battle for the last 15+ years. BACnet is ASHRAE 135, manufacturer-neutral, supported by basically every BAS vendor. LonWorks is older, was historically Trane's preferred protocol on legacy equipment, and is still common in older Tracer installations. LonTalk runs on twisted pair like MS/TP and uses Neuron chips. The practical implication: walking into a building built before about 2015, you might find LonTalk on the field bus and you have to deal with it. Newer installs are almost universally BACnet.

### Q6 — "Tell me about a complex troubleshooting situation."

**[Use the AXI International or Collier example — your screening doc has both. Below is a polished template using AXI.]**

> At AXI International I supported commissioning of an automated fuel filtration system with EtherNet/IP communication between the PLC and inline magnetic conditioners. We had intermittent communication dropouts during normal operation — the PLC would lose comm with one of the field devices for a few seconds then reconnect, which threw nuisance alarms but didn't actually take the system down. The easy assumption was bad cable termination or noise on the IP segment. I worked through it systematically: verified the physical layer with a cable certifier, checked the PLC tag mappings, reviewed the device-level diagnostics on the EtherNet/IP module, and pulled the controller event log. Root cause turned out to be IP address conflict — a second device had been added to the network without updating the address scheme. Students wired, configured, and troubleshot VFD-driven motor control loops. At Collier, the lift station pumps were VFD-driven. I haven't done deep VFD tuning on a wide platform mix but the underlying concepts — speed reference, acceleration ramps, current limiting, regen handling — are the same across Allen-Bradley, ABB, Danfoss, whatever you have in the field.

### Q8 — "What ASHRAE standards do you know?"

> The three relevant ones for HVAC controls work are ASHRAE 15 (safety code for refrigeration systems — machinery room ventilation, refrigerant detection), ASHRAE 34 (refrigerant safety classification — A1, A2L, A3, the toxicity and flammability scheme), and ASHRAE 90.1 (energy standard for buildings — the controls envelope, economizer requirements, DCV thresholds, optimum start/stop). For BAS work specifically, ASHRAE Guideline 36 is becoming the reference — it's the high-performance sequences of operations document that a lot of new commercial work specifies.

### Q9 — "How would you commission a new air-handling unit?"

> High level: verify the install matches the submittal — equipment, dampers, sensors, control valves all where the drawings show. Walk the points list against the BAS configuration — every input and output named, ranged, calibrated. Then I'd run it through manual modes — energize each output one at a time, confirm the field device responds. Then occupancy modes — unoccupied, warmup, occupied — verify the sequence of operations matches the design intent. Loop tuning at the end: discharge air temperature, static pressure, mixed air dampers. Document everything — point-by-point checkout sheet, trend logs at startup, any deviations from spec for the engineer of record.

### Q10 — "Describe troubleshooting a BAS comm failure."

> Layered approach. Start at physical — is the cable terminated correctly, is the polarity right on the MS/TP bus, is anything physically damaged. Then check the device — power up, status LEDs, address dip-switches if applicable. Then the protocol layer — are MAC addresses unique, is the baud rate consistent across the segment, are there too many devices on one trunk (BACnet MS/TP is typically 32 devices max per segment without a repeater). Then the BAS side — are the device's points being polled, is the supervisory controller seeing the device as online. I'd typically start with the simplest hypothesis (physical) and work up the stack, but I'd also pull trend data first to see when the symptom started — sudden vs gradual onset narrows the causes.

### Q11 — "What's economizer logic?"

> Free cooling. When outside air is cooler than return air and below a setpoint — typically a dry-bulb or enthalpy threshold — the AHU modulates the outside air damper open and the return damper closed to cool the space without running mechanical cooling. Saves significant energy in shoulder seasons. Failure modes are stuck dampers, miscalibrated outside air temperature sensors, and economizer lockouts that aren't properly tuned. ASHRAE 90.1 mandates economizer capability for most commercial AHUs above a size threshold, and 36 has a clean SOO for it.

### Q12 — "What's your experience with chillers?"

> Limited direct experience — my refrigeration background is on the small commercial and residential side through HVAC service. I'm familiar with the architecture: centrifugal, screw, scroll, absorption. I understand the control loops at a high level — leaving chilled water temperature setpoint, condenser water reset, head pressure control, soft-start sequences. EPA 608 Type III specifically covers low-pressure systems including centrifugal chillers — I sat that section last week, didn't pass it, and it's on my retake June 21. I'd come in as someone who needs the chiller-specific field hours, but with the controls and refrigeration fundamentals in place.

### Q13 — "Do you have experience reading P&IDs and one-lines?"

> Yes — daily at Collier. P&IDs to map field instrumentation to the SCADA points, electrical one-lines to trace MCC feeds to the equipment I was working on. At FGCU I taught students to interpret similar diagrams. I'm comfortable with the standard symbology (ISA-5.1 for instrumentation, IEEE for electrical) and the documentation conventions — tag numbering, loop numbering, equipment numbering. For HVAC sequences of operations, the controls drawings — point lists, ladder, schedule of points — are the equivalent and the same skill carries over.

### Q14 — "How do you handle a difficult customer?"

> Listen first. Most "difficult customer" situations are actually communication mismatches — they expect one thing, I'm describing another. I slow down, ask what outcome they're trying to get to, and translate the technical findings into their language — usually dollars or uptime or risk. If they're frustrated about a delay or a recurring issue, I own it rather than deflect. From running Marceau Solutions and from three years teaching engineering students, I've learned that confidence about what's happening and clarity about next steps reduces friction more than any other variable.
