## 2. The Role

### Control Systems Hardware Designer

**Core Responsibilities:**
1. **Read and understand project plans and specifications** — both control system and instrumentation
2. **Create control panel electrical schematics & panel layouts** using AutoCAD Electrical
3. **Assemble submittal packages** using PDF indexed format
4. Design and create submittal packages for projects

**Translation:** You're the person who takes an engineer's specs/drawings for a water utility project and turns them into production-ready panel schematics, wiring diagrams, and organized documentation that gets submitted to the client for approval before fabrication begins.

### What a Submittal Package Contains
- Control panel electrical schematics (one-line diagrams, wiring diagrams)
- Panel layout drawings (physical arrangement of components)
- Bill of materials
- Data sheets for major components (PLCs, drives, instruments)
- Sequence of operations
- All organized in indexed PDF format

### Tech Stack to Know
| Tool | Your Experience |
|------|----------------|
| **AutoCAD Electrical** | Honest: SolidWorks primary, AutoCAD basics. Emphasize transferable skills + willingness to learn fast |
| **PLC platforms** | Allen-Bradley, Schneider likely. You have PLC + ladder logic from FGCU TA role |
| **SCADA platforms** | VTScada, Geo SCADA, CitectSCADA, Ignition — mention awareness |
| **UL 508A standards** | Know what this is: standard for industrial control panels (fire/shock safety). BCI is certified |
| **EtherNet/IP** | Direct experience from AXI International |

## 6. Likely Interview Questions & Answers

### Technical Questions

**Q: Walk me through your experience with control panel design.**
> "As a Controls Engineering TA at FGCU for three years, I designed and built control panels from scratch for our lab exercises. I'd start with the electrical schematics — power distribution, PLC wiring, sensor integration — then physically assemble the panels and create the documentation package. I built panels that included PLCs, temperature sensors, traffic light controllers, and PID control systems. Each one required a complete documentation package with wiring diagrams, component specs, and test procedures — very similar to the submittal packages you produce here at BCI."

**Q: What's your experience with AutoCAD Electrical?**
> "My primary CAD experience is in SolidWorks, where I've done both 3D modeling and 2D technical drawings including control system layouts and electrical schematics. I've used AutoCAD for basic work but haven't done extensive work specifically in AutoCAD Electrical. That said, I understand electrical schematic standards, component symbols, wire numbering conventions, and what a complete control system drawing set needs to contain. The transition to AutoCAD Electrical is really about learning the software interface — the engineering knowledge is already there. I'm committed to getting up to speed quickly, and honestly, for someone who's taught themselves Python, AI systems, and multiple engineering tools, learning a new CAD platform is straightforward."

**Q: What do you know about UL 508A?**
> "UL 508A is the standard for industrial control panels — it covers the safety requirements for panel construction, including things like proper wire sizing, short circuit current ratings, enclosure requirements, spacing, and overcurrent protection. I know BCI is a UL 508A certified panel shop, which means every panel you build has to meet those standards. My experience building control panels in the university lab gave me a foundation in these principles, though I'm sure there's more detail I'll learn about the specific UL requirements in a production environment."

**Q: Tell me about your experience reading project specifications.**
> "I've read and interpreted specs in multiple contexts. At AXI International, I worked from project specifications for industrial automation systems to design control system layouts. At Marmon, I interpreted electrical schematics and specs for military-grade cable production. At Insulet, I worked within FDA-regulated specifications for manufacturing equipment. And as a TA, I created specs for lab exercises and also taught students how to read and interpret technical drawings. I'm comfortable taking a spec set and translating it into actionable design work."

**Q: What PLC platforms have you worked with?**
> "I programmed PLCs using Arduino-based platforms for our controls lab — building state machines, traffic light controllers, temperature control systems with PID. I understand ladder logic, sequential function charts, and the fundamentals of PLC programming. At AXI, I supported troubleshooting on their PLC systems and worked with EtherNet/IP for data acquisition. I haven't worked extensively with specific industrial PLCs like Allen-Bradley or Schneider, but the programming concepts transfer directly. I'd need to learn the specific platform you use, but the logic and structure are the same."

**Q: What SCADA platforms are you familiar with?**
> "I'm aware of the major platforms — VTScada, Ignition, CitectSCADA, Wonderware. I know BCI is certified across several of these. My direct hands-on SCADA experience is limited, but I understand the architecture: field instruments and PLCs at the bottom, communication networks in the middle, and the SCADA HMI at the top for monitoring and control. For this hardware designer role, my understanding is the focus is more on the panel design and documentation side rather than SCADA programming, but I'm eager to learn the full picture."
