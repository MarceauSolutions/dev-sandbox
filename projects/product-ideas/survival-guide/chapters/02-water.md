# Chapter 2: Water — The Most Critical Survival Resource

> **The Rule of Threes**: You can survive 3 minutes without air, 3 hours without shelter in extreme conditions, 3 days without water, and 3 weeks without food. Water is your second-highest priority after immediate shelter from life-threatening exposure. Everything else — food, tools, long-term shelter — comes after you secure a reliable water supply.

> **Chapter Summary**: This chapter covers everything you need to know about water in a post-collapse scenario: how much you need, where to find it, how to make it safe, how to build systems to collect and store it, and how to handle water-related medical emergencies. Every instruction is written so that a person with zero prior knowledge can follow it step by step.

---

## Section 1: Understanding Water Needs

Water is not optional. It is the single most important resource you will secure after basic shelter. Your body uses water for temperature regulation (sweating), digestion, waste removal, joint lubrication, blood circulation, and brain function. When water intake drops below what your body expends, the cascade toward death begins within hours, not days.

### 1.1 Daily Water Requirements by Use

The commonly cited "1 gallon per person per day" is a survival minimum for drinking only. In reality, your total daily water needs are significantly higher when you account for all uses.

**Drinking Water**
- Minimum: 0.5 gallons (2 liters) per person per day in cool, sedentary conditions
- Standard: 1 gallon (4 liters) per person per day in moderate conditions with light activity
- Hot climate or heavy labor: 1.5 to 2 gallons (6 to 8 liters) per person per day
- Extreme heat with strenuous work: up to 3 gallons (12 liters) per person per day

**Cooking Water**
- Minimum: 0.5 gallons (2 liters) per person per day for basic meal preparation
- This includes water for boiling grains, beans, pasta, and rehydrating dried foods
- Soups and stews require an additional 0.25 to 0.5 gallons per batch
- Washing vegetables and food prep adds another 0.25 gallons

**Hygiene Water**
- Minimum survival hygiene: 0.5 gallons (2 liters) per person per day
- This covers hand washing before meals and after using the latrine, basic face washing, and wound care
- A full sponge bath uses 1 to 2 gallons
- Laundry (hand washing clothes): 3 to 5 gallons per small load
- Brushing teeth: 0.1 gallons per session (use a cup, do not let water run)

**Medical Water**
- Wound irrigation: 0.25 to 1 gallon per wound cleaning session
- Oral rehydration therapy for illness: 0.5 to 1 gallon per day on top of normal intake
- Fever management (cooling cloths, increased fluid intake): additional 0.5 gallons per day

**Total Realistic Daily Needs**
| Category | Minimum (gallons) | Comfortable (gallons) |
|----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Drinking | 0.5 | 1.0 |
| Cooking | 0.5 | 0.75 |
| Hygiene | 0.5 | 1.0 |
| Medical reserve | 0.0 | 0.25 |
| **Total per person** | **1.5** | **3.0** |

For planning purposes, use 2 gallons per person per day as your baseline target. This is the number you should design your collection, purification, and storage systems around.

### 1.2 Signs and Stages of Dehydration

Dehydration progresses through predictable stages. Recognizing early signs in yourself and others can prevent a medical emergency.

**Stage 1: Mild Dehydration (1-3% body weight lost as water)**
- Thirst (by the time you feel thirsty, you are already mildly dehydrated)
- Dry mouth and lips
- Slightly darker yellow urine
- Mild headache
- Slight decrease in urine output
- Fatigue that seems disproportionate to activity level
- Treatment: Drink water slowly, about 0.5 cups every 15 minutes until symptoms resolve. Do not gulp large amounts at once, as this can cause nausea.

**Stage 2: Moderate Dehydration (3-6% body weight lost)**
- Very dry mouth, sticky saliva
- Dark yellow or amber urine
- Significantly reduced urine output (urinating less than 3 times per day)
- Increased heart rate (pulse above 100 beats per minute at rest)
- Dizziness when standing up quickly
- Headache that worsens
- Irritability, difficulty concentrating
- Dry, cool skin that lacks elasticity (pinch the skin on the back of your hand — if it stays tented for more than 2 seconds, dehydration is moderate or worse)
- Sunken eyes
- Treatment: Oral rehydration solution (see Section 7). Sip continuously. Rest in shade. Cease all non-essential physical activity. This is a serious warning — if untreated, this stage progresses to severe dehydration within hours.

**Stage 3: Severe Dehydration (6-10% body weight lost)**
- No urine output, or extremely small amounts of very dark urine
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Rapid breathing
- Extreme dizziness, inability to stand
- Confusion, disorientation, delirium
- Fainting or loss of consciousness
- Very sunken eyes, no tears when crying
- Skin that is cool, mottled, or bluish at extremities
- In infants: sunken fontanelle (soft spot on top of head)
- Treatment: This is a life-threatening emergency. Administer oral rehydration solution if the person is conscious and can swallow. Give small sips every 1 to 2 minutes. Do not force liquids into an unconscious person — they can aspirate and drown. Place the person in the recovery position (on their side) if unconscious. Without intravenous fluids, which require medical supplies, severe dehydration has a high mortality rate.

**Stage 4: Fatal Dehydration (above 10% body weight lost)**
- Organ failure begins
- Seizures
- Loss of consciousness
- Death typically occurs between 10-15% water loss depending on baseline health

**The Urine Color Chart**
This is the simplest daily monitoring tool:
- Pale straw / light yellow: Well hydrated
- Yellow: Adequately hydrated
- Dark yellow: Mildly dehydrated — drink more water
- Amber / honey: Moderately dehydrated — prioritize water intake immediately
- Brown / cola-colored: Severely dehydrated — medical emergency
- Note: Some foods (beets, B-vitamins) can temporarily discolor urine. Consider overall symptoms, not just color.

### 1.3 Water Rationing Strategies

When water supply is limited and resupply is uncertain, rationing becomes necessary. Done correctly, rationing extends survival. Done incorrectly, it accelerates dehydration.

**Core Rationing Principles**

1. Never ration water below 1 quart (0.95 liters) per person per day. Below this amount, your body cannot maintain basic organ function regardless of activity level. It is better to drink what you have and use the energy to find more water than to sip tiny amounts and become too weak to move.

2. Drink what you have rather than "saving it." A common fatal mistake is carrying water while becoming dangerously dehydrated. Your body is the best water storage container. Water in your stomach keeps you alive. Water in your bottle does not.

3. Reduce water loss before reducing water intake:
   - Rest during the hottest parts of the day (10 AM to 4 PM)
   - Work and travel during early morning, late afternoon, and night
   - Stay in shade whenever possible
   - Breathe through your nose, not your mouth (mouth breathing increases moisture loss)
   - Wear loose, light-colored clothing that covers your skin (reduces sweat evaporation rate but protects from sunburn, which increases water needs)
   - Do not eat if you cannot drink — digestion requires water; eating without adequate water accelerates dehydration
   - Avoid alcohol, caffeine, and high-protein foods, which increase urination
   - Minimize talking (you lose moisture with every exhaled word)
   - Do not smoke (increases thirst and reduces circulation)

4. Ration by time, not by thirst:
   - Divide your available water into portions based on the hours until you expect to find more
   - Drink measured portions at regular intervals (every 2 to 4 hours)
   - Do not wait until you are thirsty — thirst is a lagging indicator

**Rationing Schedule Example**
If you have 2 gallons of water and estimate 4 days until resupply for 1 person:
- 2 gallons = 256 ounces
- 4 days = 96 hours
- 256 / 96 = approximately 2.7 ounces per hour while awake
- Divide into 6 drinking sessions per day (every 2.5 to 3 hours while awake)
- Each session: approximately 10 to 11 ounces (about 1.3 cups)
- This gives you 64 ounces (0.5 gallons) per day — enough to survive if activity is minimized
- Prioritize finding more water during this period; do not simply sit and wait

### 1.4 How Conditions Affect Water Needs

Your water requirement is not fixed. It changes dramatically based on conditions.

**Physical Activity**
- Sedentary (resting, sitting): baseline 0.5 gallons per day
- Light activity (walking at 2 mph, light camp chores): 0.75 to 1 gallon per day
- Moderate activity (hiking with a pack, digging, building): 1 to 1.5 gallons per day
- Heavy labor (chopping wood, running, carrying heavy loads): 1.5 to 2.5 gallons per day
- Extreme exertion (sustained heavy labor in heat): 2.5 to 3+ gallons per day
- Rule of thumb: for every hour of moderate to heavy exertion, drink an additional 16 to 32 ounces (0.5 to 1 liter) of water

**Temperature**
- Below 60 degrees F (15 C): baseline water needs
- 60 to 80 degrees F (15 to 27 C): add 25% to baseline
- 80 to 95 degrees F (27 to 35 C): add 50 to 75% to baseline
- Above 95 degrees F (35 C): double or triple baseline
- Cold weather warning: you still lose significant water through respiration in cold, dry air. The visible cloud of breath you exhale is water leaving your body. Cold also suppresses thirst sensation — you can become dehydrated in winter without feeling thirsty. Additionally, your body burns more calories (and water) to maintain core temperature in cold.

**Altitude**
- Above 5,000 feet (1,500 meters): add 25% to baseline
- Above 8,000 feet (2,400 meters): add 50% to baseline
- Above 12,000 feet (3,600 meters): add 75 to 100% to baseline
- At altitude, you breathe faster and lose more moisture through respiration. Air is drier. Altitude sickness includes nausea and vomiting, which further dehydrate you.

**Humidity**
- High humidity (above 75%): sweat evaporates slowly, which means your body's cooling is less efficient. You sweat more in an attempt to compensate. Water needs increase but not as dramatically as dry heat.
- Low humidity (below 30%): sweat evaporates very quickly, which cools you efficiently but means you may not notice how much water you are losing. In dry desert air, you can lose 1 to 2 liters per hour during exertion without visibly sweating.

**Illness**
- Fever: for every 1 degree F above normal (98.6 F), add approximately 8 ounces of water per day
- Vomiting: replace lost fluids ounce for ounce. Sip small amounts (1 tablespoon every 5 minutes) to avoid triggering more vomiting
- Diarrhea: this is the most dangerous — severe diarrhea can cause fatal dehydration within 24 hours, especially in children. Use oral rehydration solution (Section 7) immediately
- Burns: severe burns massively increase fluid loss through damaged skin. A person with extensive burns can need 3 to 5 times normal water intake
- Bleeding: blood loss reduces fluid volume. Increase water and salt intake after any significant bleeding

### 1.5 Water Needs for Vulnerable Populations

**Children (ages 1-12)**
- Children dehydrate faster than adults because they have a higher body surface area relative to volume
- Minimum: 0.5 to 0.75 gallons per day depending on age and size
- Children may not recognize or communicate thirst — offer water on a schedule
- Signs of dehydration in children: no tears when crying, dry diaper for 3+ hours in infants, sunken eyes, listlessness, irritability
- Infants under 6 months who are breastfeeding: the mother needs additional water (see below), but the infant gets all necessary water from breast milk. Do not give plain water to infants under 6 months — it can cause dangerous electrolyte imbalances
- Infants on formula: use only purified water for formula preparation. Contaminated water in formula is one of the leading causes of infant death in disaster situations

**Elderly (ages 65+)**
- Elderly individuals have a diminished thirst response — they literally do not feel thirsty even when dehydrated
- Kidney function declines with age, reducing the body's ability to conserve water
- Many elderly individuals take medications that increase urination (diuretics) — in a collapse scenario, these medications may run out, but the underlying conditions remain
- Minimum: 1 gallon per day, offered on a strict schedule
- Monitor urine color closely. Check on elderly group members frequently

**Pregnant Women**
- Water needs increase by approximately 25 to 50% during pregnancy
- Minimum: 1.25 gallons per day
- Dehydration during pregnancy can cause premature contractions, urinary tract infections, and low amniotic fluid, all of which threaten both mother and baby
- Iodine-based water purification should NOT be used by pregnant women (see Section 3 on chemical treatment)

**Nursing Mothers**
- Breast milk production requires significant additional water — approximately 25 to 32 extra ounces per day
- Minimum: 1.5 gallons per day
- If the mother becomes dehydrated, milk production decreases, threatening the infant
- Prioritize water allocation to nursing mothers — this is a force multiplier, as it keeps both mother and infant alive

**People with Medical Conditions**
- Diabetes: both Type 1 and Type 2 cause increased urination. Dehydration risk is significantly elevated
- Kidney disease: consult any available medical knowledge; some conditions require fluid restriction while others require increased intake
- Anyone on medications that cause fluid loss: diuretics, lithium, some blood pressure medications

## Section 2: Finding Water Sources

In a post-collapse environment, municipal water systems will fail within days to weeks. Pumping stations require electricity. Treatment plants require chemicals and trained operators. Distribution pipes will lose pressure, and when pressure is lost, contaminants enter through cracks and joints. Your survival depends on knowing where to find water in every environment and season.

### 2.1 Surface Water

Surface water is the most visible and accessible water source. It is also the most likely to be contaminated with biological pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites), chemical pollutants, and sediment. Always purify surface water before drinking.

**Rivers and Streams**
- Moving water is generally safer than still water because the current discourages bacterial growth and oxygenates the water
- Collect water from the fastest-flowing section — ideally in the middle of the stream, away from banks where animal waste and debris accumulate
- Upstream is better than downstream. Think about what might be upstream: a town dumping waste, agricultural runoff, a dead animal in the water
- Mountain streams above human habitation are the cleanest surface water you will find, but they can still harbor giardia and cryptosporidium from animal feces
- Clear water is not safe water. Giardia cysts are invisible to the naked eye. Always purify
- After heavy rain, surface water becomes much more contaminated as runoff washes soil, animal waste, and debris into waterways. Wait 24 to 48 hours after heavy rain if you can afford to

**Lakes and Ponds**
- Collect water from as far offshore as you can safely reach — shoreline water concentrates contaminants
- If you have a boat or can wade, collect from below the surface (about 6 to 12 inches down) to avoid surface scum, pollen, and floating debris
- Large, deep lakes are generally cleaner than small, shallow ponds
- Ponds with green algae blooms (particularly blue-green algae / cyanobacteria) should be avoided entirely. Cyanotoxins are not removed by boiling. They can cause liver failure and death
- Signs of a relatively healthy water body: presence of fish, frogs, aquatic insects, diverse plant life, no foul smell, no surface film or discoloration

**How to Assess Surface Water Safety (Before Purification)**
Even though you will purify all surface water, some sources are so contaminated that purification may be insufficient. Use this checklist:

1. Look at the color: Clear or slightly tinted = acceptable for treatment. Deeply colored, milky, or opaque = pre-filter before treating. Black or rainbow-sheened (petroleum) = avoid
2. Smell it: No odor or earthy odor = acceptable. Chemical smell (solvents, fuel, sulfur) = avoid. Sewage smell = avoid if possible, treat aggressively if no alternative
3. Check for flow: Moving water with a clear inlet and outlet = good. Stagnant water with no visible flow = higher risk
4. Look upstream/around: Nearby industry, agriculture, or dumps = chemical contamination risk. Dead animals in or near water = high biological contamination
5. Check for life: Aquatic insects, fish, frogs = water supports life, which is a positive sign. Complete absence of life in a water body = something is very wrong; avoid it

### 2.2 Groundwater

Groundwater is water that has percolated through soil and rock, which provides natural filtration. It is generally much cleaner than surface water but can still contain dissolved minerals, chemicals (from agricultural or industrial contamination), and occasionally bacteria.

**Natural Springs**
- Springs occur where the water table intersects the ground surface, and water flows out naturally
- Collect water as close to the point where it emerges from the ground as possible — this is the cleanest point
- A spring that flows year-round (perennial spring) is fed by a deep aquifer and is a highly valuable resource
- Springs that only flow seasonally (intermittent springs) may dry up when you need them most
- Even spring water should be purified, but it requires less aggressive treatment than surface water
- Signs of a spring: a wet area on a hillside, unexplained green vegetation in an otherwise dry area, a small flow of water emerging from a rock face or hillside
- Develop a spring by digging a small basin at the emergence point, lining it with rocks, and covering it to prevent contamination from surface debris and animals. Place a pipe or channel to direct the flow into a collection container

**Seeps**
- Seeps are areas where groundwater slowly oozes to the surface without a defined flow
- Seeps produce small quantities of water — often less than a gallon per hour
- To collect from a seep: dig a small hole (12 to 18 inches deep) at the wettest point. Line the hole with rocks or a container. Allow the hole to fill over several hours. Skim the clearest water from the top
- Seeps are often found at the base of hills, in valleys, and along rock formations where impermeable layers force water to the surface

**Hand-Dug Wells**
A hand-dug well is one of the most valuable water infrastructure projects you can undertake for long-term survival. (Full construction instructions in Section 4.)

- Wells access the water table directly, providing a reliable source below the level of surface contamination
- In most areas, the water table is between 10 and 50 feet below the surface. In arid regions, it may be much deeper
- Indicators of a shallow water table: lush vegetation in dry seasons, natural springs in the area, streams that flow year-round, history of wells in the area (check abandoned properties for existing wells)
- Site selection: at least 100 feet downhill and downstream from any latrine, animal pen, or waste area. At least 50 feet from any building foundation. On slightly elevated ground relative to immediate surroundings to prevent surface runoff from entering the well
- Never dig a well in a flood plain or at the bottom of a slope where surface water pools

**Driven Wells**
- A driven well uses a narrow metal pipe (1.25 to 2 inch diameter) with a pointed screen on the end that is driven into the ground with a hammer or maul
- This method works in sandy or soft soil where the water table is less than 25 feet deep
- Faster than digging — a driven well can be completed in one day in ideal conditions
- Requires specific materials: well point (screened drive tip), lengths of pipe with couplings, a drive cap to protect the pipe top while hammering, and a hand pump
- These materials should be scavenged from hardware stores, farm supply stores, or abandoned wells

### 2.3 Rainwater Harvesting

Rainwater is one of the purest water sources available. It contains no ground contaminants, no biological pathogens (when properly collected), and no dissolved minerals. It requires minimal treatment — usually just filtration to remove debris from the collection surface.

**Basic Collection System**
At minimum, you need three things: a collection surface, a way to channel the water, and a container to store it.

1. Collection surface: any clean, smooth, non-toxic surface. A roof is ideal. Metal roofing (galvanized steel, aluminum) is best. Asphalt shingles work but contribute some chemical contamination and grit. Avoid roofs treated with lead paint, creosote, or chemical preservatives. In a pinch, a tarp stretched between trees or poles works
2. Gutters and downspouts: channel water from the collection surface to your storage container. Improvised gutters can be made from split PVC pipe, bamboo halves, sheet metal bent into a channel, or even a series of angled boards
3. Storage container: any food-safe container. See Section 5 for detailed storage guidance

**Roof Catchment Calculation**
This formula tells you how much water you can collect from a given roof area:

**Gallons collected = Roof area (square feet) x Rainfall (inches) x 0.623**

The 0.623 factor converts square foot-inches to gallons. In practice, you lose about 10 to 25% to evaporation, spillage, and the first-flush diverter (see below), so multiply by 0.75 for a realistic estimate.

**Realistic formula: Gallons collected = Roof area (sq ft) x Rainfall (inches) x 0.623 x 0.75**

Example calculations:
- A 10 foot by 10 foot tarp (100 sq ft) in a 1-inch rainstorm: 100 x 1 x 0.623 x 0.75 = 46.7 gallons
- A small shed roof, 8 feet by 12 feet (96 sq ft) in a 0.5-inch rain: 96 x 0.5 x 0.623 x 0.75 = 22.4 gallons
- A full house roof, 30 feet by 40 feet (1,200 sq ft) in a 1-inch rain: 1,200 x 1 x 0.623 x 0.75 = 560.7 gallons
- A single 1-inch rainfall on a standard house roof can provide one person with water for over 9 months at 2 gallons per day

This demonstrates why rainwater harvesting should be a primary water strategy. Even in relatively dry climates receiving 15 to 20 inches of rain per year, a moderate collection surface provides enormous quantities of water.

**First-Flush Diverter**
The first water that runs off a roof carries bird droppings, dust, pollen, insect debris, and whatever else has accumulated on the surface. This first flush must be diverted away from your storage tank.

How to build a simple first-flush diverter:
1. Take a length of PVC pipe (3 to 4 inch diameter works well), about 3 to 4 feet long
2. Cap the bottom end with a PVC cap
3. Attach this pipe vertically to your downspout, positioned so that water flows into it before reaching your storage tank
4. Install a T-fitting or Y-fitting at the top so water goes down into the diverter pipe first
5. When the diverter pipe fills (first 1 to 2 gallons per 100 square feet of roof), water rises to the level of the T-fitting and begins flowing to your storage tank instead
6. Drill a tiny hole (1/16 inch) at the bottom of the diverter pipe so it slowly drains between rainstorms, resetting itself
7. The rule of thumb: divert the first 0.5 to 1 gallon per 100 square feet of roof area

**Rainwater Treatment**
- Filtered rainwater from a clean roof needs only minimal treatment
- At minimum: filter through a cloth or fine mesh screen to remove debris, then treat with 2 drops of household bleach (8.25% sodium hypochlorite) per gallon
- For higher confidence: filter and then boil
- Rainwater collected from the ground (tarps on the ground, puddles) should be treated like surface water — full purification required

### 2.4 Atmospheric Water

When no surface water or groundwater is accessible, you can extract water from the atmosphere. These methods produce small quantities and should be considered supplementary sources, not primary ones.

**Dew Collection**
- Dew forms when nighttime temperatures drop below the dew point (the temperature at which air becomes saturated and water condenses)
- Best conditions: clear nights, calm winds, high humidity, surfaces that radiate heat quickly (metal, stone)
- Method: before dawn, drag an absorbent cloth (cotton t-shirt, towel, cotton bandana) across grass, leaves, or any surface where dew has formed. Wring the cloth into a container. Repeat
- Expected yield: 0.25 to 1 quart per hour of collection in good conditions
- Alternative: spread clean tarps, plastic sheeting, or metal sheets on the ground in the evening. In the morning, tilt them to drain collected dew into a container
- Dew is essentially distilled water and is very clean, but treat it if collected from ground-level surfaces that may have animal contamination

**Fog Nets**
- In coastal or mountainous areas where fog is common, fog nets can collect significant water
- Construction: stretch a fine mesh material (window screen, shade cloth, or even multiple layers of mosquito netting) vertically across the path of prevailing fog/wind. Frame it between two poles
- Size: a 10 foot by 10 foot fog net can collect 1 to 5 gallons per day in good fog conditions
- Install a gutter or trough at the bottom of the net to channel collected water into a container
- The mesh must be fine enough to catch fog droplets but open enough for air to pass through — standard window screen mesh is approximately ideal
- Orient the net perpendicular to the prevailing wind direction

**Transpiration Bags**
- Living trees and plants pump water from the ground through their roots and release it through their leaves (transpiration). You can capture this water
- Method: place a clear plastic bag over a leafy branch. The bag should be large — 1 to 2 gallon capacity minimum. Seal the opening tightly around the branch with cordage, tape, or a rubber band. Position the bag so that one corner hangs lower than the rest to create a collection point
- Place a small stone in the lower corner to create a pocket where water accumulates
- Best results: use branches that receive full sun. Choose trees with broad, non-toxic leaves. Willows, maples, and birch work well. Do NOT use toxic species (oleander, poison oak, poison ivy, yew, hemlock)
- Expected yield: 0.25 to 1 quart per bag per day depending on tree species, temperature, and sunlight
- Use multiple bags on different branches for meaningful quantities
- Replace bags every 2 to 3 days or when the leaves inside begin to die (dead leaves stop transpiring)
- The water is distilled by the tree's biological process and is generally safe to drink without treatment, but filtering through a cloth is good practice

**Solar Still (Atmospheric)**
The ground-level solar still extracts moisture from soil and vegetation using solar energy. Full construction details are in Section 4, but the basic concept:
- Dig a hole, place a container in the center, cover with clear plastic, place a stone on the plastic above the container
- Sun heats the ground, moisture evaporates, condenses on the plastic, drips into the container
- Expected yield: 0.5 to 2 quarts per day depending on soil moisture and sun intensity
- This is an emergency-only water source due to low output and high effort

### 2.5 Urban Water Sources

In a post-collapse urban environment, enormous quantities of water remain trapped in buildings and infrastructure. Knowing where to look and how to access it safely can sustain you for weeks or months.

**Water Heaters (40 to 80 Gallon Reserve)**
Every residential water heater is a sealed tank holding 40 to 80 gallons of drinkable water. In an apartment building or neighborhood, this represents hundreds or thousands of gallons.

How to drain a water heater:
1. Turn off the gas or electricity to the heater (gas: turn the valve on the gas line to the perpendicular/off position; electric: flip the breaker)
2. Turn off the cold water inlet valve at the top of the heater (turn clockwise to close)
3. Open a hot water faucet somewhere in the building (upstairs is best) to break the vacuum and allow air into the system
4. Locate the drain valve at the bottom of the heater. It looks like a garden hose spigot
5. Attach a hose if available, or place a container beneath the valve
6. Open the drain valve (turn counterclockwise)
7. Water will flow out by gravity. The first quart may contain sediment — let this flush and then collect the clean water
8. A full 50-gallon heater takes 15 to 30 minutes to drain completely
9. If the water smells of sulfur or has a metallic taste, it is still safe to drink but should be filtered if possible
10. Treatment: this water was treated by the municipal system before the collapse and has been sealed in the tank. If it has been less than a few weeks since the water system failed, it is likely safe to drink as-is. After a few weeks, treat with 2 drops of bleach per gallon as a precaution

**Toilet Tanks (NOT Bowls)**
- The tank on the back of a toilet (the upper reservoir, not the bowl) holds 1.5 to 3.5 gallons of clean water
- This water is identical to tap water — it comes directly from the supply line
- Do NOT use water from the bowl — it is contaminated with bacteria
- Do NOT use water from tanks that contain colored cleaning tablets, bleach pucks, or other chemical inserts
- Simply lift the tank lid and scoop or pour the water into a container
- Treat with 2 drops of bleach per gallon before drinking as a precaution

**Draining Building Plumbing**
A building's pipes contain water throughout the entire plumbing system. In a multi-story building, this can be tens of gallons.

Method:
1. Start at the highest point in the building (top floor faucets)
2. Open all faucets on the top floor — this allows air into the system
3. Go to the lowest point in the building (basement or ground floor)
4. Open the lowest faucets and drain valves
5. Gravity pulls all water in the pipes down to the lowest open point
6. Collect the water as it flows out
7. Check under sinks for shut-off valves — the pipe between the shut-off valve and the faucet still holds water even if the main supply is off
8. Hot water lines often hold more water than cold lines because they connect to the water heater

**Swimming Pools**
- A standard residential swimming pool holds 10,000 to 30,000 gallons — an enormous water reserve
- Pool water contains chlorine and other treatment chemicals, but at concentrations that are not harmful for short-term drinking
- Concerns: pool water may also contain cyanuric acid (stabilizer), algaecide, and other chemicals. These are present in low concentrations and are not immediately dangerous, but long-term consumption is not ideal
- Treatment: filter the water through cloth to remove debris, then let it sit in an open container for 24 hours to allow excess chlorine to evaporate (off-gas). Then treat it as you would any surface water — filter and disinfect
- If the pool has turned green with algae: the water is still usable but requires more aggressive treatment. Filter through multiple layers, boil, and consider it a last resort
- Above-ground pools and hot tubs hold 500 to 5,000 gallons and are equally usable

**Water Features, Fountains, and Decorative Ponds**
- Smaller volumes but still useful
- Treat all decorative water as contaminated surface water — full purification required
- Fountains with copper components may have elevated copper levels — filter through activated charcoal if possible

### 2.6 Emergency Water Sources

**Ice and Snow**
- Never eat snow or ice directly — it lowers your core body temperature and accelerates hypothermia. The energy your body expends warming snow in your stomach costs more water than the snow provides
- Always melt snow or ice before consuming. Use a dark-colored container in sunlight, body heat (place a sealed container of snow inside your clothing), or fire
- Melt snow in stages: pack it loosely, add a little liquid water if you have any (this speeds melting dramatically), heat slowly
- Ice provides much more water per volume than snow. Fresh ice from a clean source is preferable to snow
- Old sea ice (multi-year ice that has weathered multiple seasons) has had most of its salt leach out and can provide drinkable water. It appears blue or clear, not gray or white. Taste a small piece — if it is not salty, it is safe. Fresh sea ice is salty and should not be consumed
- Energy cost: melting 1 gallon of snow requires approximately the same energy as boiling 0.5 gallons of water. This means it takes significant fuel. In a fuel-scarce winter scenario, this matters for planning

**Cactus — Myth vs. Reality**
- The popular survival myth that you can "drink from a cactus" is mostly false and can be dangerous
- Most cactus species contain oxalic acid and alkaloids that cause nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea — all of which accelerate dehydration
- The barrel cactus (Ferocactus) does contain a watery pulp, but consuming it causes nausea in most people
- The prickly pear cactus (Opuntia) fruits are edible and contain water, and the pads can be consumed after removing spines, but the water content is modest
- Bottom line: do not plan on cactus as a water source. If you are in a desert survival situation and find prickly pear fruits, they can supplement your water intake, but they are food, not a water source

**Vines**
- Certain tropical and temperate vines contain drinkable water
- Test method: cut a notch in the vine high up first, then sever the vine at the base. If you cut the bottom first, capillary action pulls the water up and out of reach
- Let the water drip into a container or your mouth
- Safe vine water is clear and odorless. If the liquid is milky, colored, or bitter, do not drink it — it may be toxic sap
- Grape vines in temperate climates produce safe, drinkable sap in spring

**Bamboo**
- Green bamboo sections (the internodes between joints) often contain trapped water
- Shake the bamboo and listen for sloshing — if you hear water, there is water inside
- Cut a notch in the bamboo just above a joint and tilt the section to pour water into a container
- Bamboo water from sealed sections is generally clean and safe to drink without treatment
- Young green bamboo is more likely to contain water than old dry bamboo

### 2.7 Natural Indicators of Nearby Water

When you cannot see water, nature provides signs of its presence.

**Animal Tracks**
- Animal trails that converge toward a single point often lead to water
- Follow tracks downhill — animals drink at the lowest accessible point
- Grazing animals (deer, elk, cattle) typically drink at dawn and dusk. Fresh tracks heading downhill in the morning suggest water is nearby
- Predators often hunt near water sources, so remain alert when following game trails

**Insects**
- Bees are a reliable water indicator. Bees need water and are rarely found more than 3 miles from a source. If you see bees in a dry area, water is relatively near. Watch their flight direction — they fly in a straight line (beeline) to and from water
- Mosquitoes indicate standing water within a few hundred yards
- Ants climbing a tree in a line may be heading toward a water-filled hollow
- Columns of flies and gnats often hover near water

**Vegetation**
- A line of green, lush vegetation in an otherwise dry landscape marks a subsurface water course. Dig there
- Cottonwood, willow, sycamore, and cattails grow only near water
- Cattails (Typha) are one of the most reliable water indicators — if you find cattails, water is at or just below the surface
- In arid environments, look for dense vegetation in draws, ravines, and valley floors

**Bird Behavior**
- Grain-eating birds (finches, pigeons, doves) fly toward water in the morning and away from it in the evening
- Raptors and meat-eating birds do not require drinking water as frequently and are not reliable indicators
- Flocks of birds circling and descending at dusk often indicate a water source

**Listening**
- In quiet environments, flowing water can be heard from a surprising distance — up to 0.5 miles in still conditions
- Stop, close your eyes, and listen. Cup your hands behind your ears to amplify sound
- The sound of frogs croaking at night indicates water within a few hundred yards

**Terrain Features**
- Water flows downhill and collects in low points. Follow valleys, draws, and ravines downhill
- The outside bend of a dry riverbed is the most likely place to find subsurface water — dig there
- At the base of cliffs and rock faces, look for seeps, springs, and wet patches
- In limestone terrain, look for sinkholes and cave entrances — limestone regions often have underground water systems
- Dry riverbeds may have water just a few feet below the surface. Dig in the lowest point, especially on the outside of a bend where the deeper channel cuts. Dig 2 to 4 feet deep and wait for water to seep in

### 2.8 Water Sources to Avoid

Some water is so contaminated that no field purification method will make it safe. Recognizing these sources can save your life.

**Stagnant Pools with No Inlet or Outlet**
- Standing water with no flow in or out accumulates bacteria, parasites, algal toxins, and chemical pollutants
- If you can see a scummy surface film, smell a foul odor, or see no aquatic life, avoid it
- Exception: if this is truly your only option, pre-filter through multiple layers of cloth, boil vigorously, and use chemical treatment as a backup. Accept the risk

**Water Near Industrial or Agricultural Sites**
- Chemical contamination (pesticides, herbicides, heavy metals, solvents, petroleum products) is not removed by boiling, basic filtration, or chemical treatment
- Only distillation or advanced activated charcoal filtration can remove chemical contaminants
- Stay upstream and uphill from factories, gas stations, auto repair shops, farms, and waste dumps
- If water has a chemical odor, rainbow sheen, or unusual color, do not use it

**Flood Water**
- Flood water is a toxic cocktail of sewage, chemicals, petroleum, animal waste, debris, and every contaminant from every surface it has touched
- Never drink flood water, even if treated. The concentration and variety of contaminants overwhelm standard purification methods
- After floodwaters recede, wait at least 2 weeks before using any surface water source in the affected area, and treat it aggressively

**Saltwater**
- Drinking saltwater accelerates dehydration and leads to death faster than drinking nothing at all
- Your kidneys must use more water to flush the excess salt than the saltwater provides
- Saltwater must be distilled (evaporated and recondensed) to remove salt. Boiling does not remove salt. Filtering does not remove salt. Chemical treatment does not remove salt. Only distillation or reverse osmosis (which requires pressure equipment) works
- Brackish water (a mixture of fresh and salt) can sometimes be tolerable in very small quantities as a last resort, but it should be distilled if possible

**Water Contaminated by Dead Animals**
- If you can see a dead animal in or near a water source, assume the entire downstream area is heavily contaminated with bacteria, including potential anthrax spores, botulism, and other dangerous pathogens
- Move upstream of the carcass by at least 200 yards and collect water there
- If you must use water from near a carcass, boil it for a full 5 minutes (not the standard 1 minute) and consider chemical treatment in addition to boiling

## Section 3: Water Purification Methods

No matter where you find water in a post-collapse world, assume it is contaminated until you have treated it. Even clear, cold, fast-flowing mountain streams can harbor invisible parasites. The single most common cause of death in survival situations (after trauma) is waterborne illness — specifically, the dehydration caused by diarrhea from drinking untreated water. This creates a vicious cycle: you drink contaminated water because you are thirsty, the contamination causes diarrhea, diarrhea dehydrates you further, and you drink more contaminated water.

Breaking this cycle requires understanding what is in the water and which treatment methods address which threats.

### 3.0 What Is in Untreated Water?

Water contaminants fall into four categories, and no single treatment method addresses all four:

**1. Bacteria** (size: 0.2 to 10 microns)
- Examples: E. coli, Salmonella, Vibrio cholerae (cholera), Campylobacter, Shigella
- Killed by: boiling, chemical treatment (chlorine, iodine), UV light
- Filtered by: filters rated 0.2 microns or smaller
- Health effects: diarrhea, vomiting, fever, potentially fatal dehydration

**2. Viruses** (size: 0.02 to 0.3 microns)
- Examples: Hepatitis A, Norovirus, Rotavirus, Poliovirus
- Killed by: boiling, chemical treatment, UV light
- NOT reliably filtered by most portable filters (too small) — this is a critical limitation
- Most common in water contaminated by human sewage
- In wilderness settings far from human habitation, viral contamination is less common but never impossible

**3. Protozoan Parasites** (size: 1 to 300 microns)
- Examples: Giardia lamblia (cyst: 8-12 microns), Cryptosporidium parvum (oocyst: 4-6 microns)
- Giardia: killed by boiling, partially resistant to chlorine (requires longer contact time), killed by iodine, killed by UV
- Cryptosporidium: killed by boiling, RESISTANT to chlorine and iodine at normal doses, killed by UV, filtered by 1-micron or smaller filters
- This means: chemical treatment alone does NOT reliably protect against Cryptosporidium. You need boiling, filtration, or UV in addition to chemicals
- Health effects: severe diarrhea lasting weeks, abdominal cramps, weight loss

**4. Chemical Contaminants**
- Examples: pesticides, herbicides, heavy metals (lead, mercury, arsenic), petroleum products, industrial solvents, pharmaceutical residues
- NOT removed by: boiling (actually concentrates them), chemical treatment, UV light
- Removed by: activated charcoal/carbon filtration (partial), distillation (most effective), reverse osmosis (requires pressure equipment)
- In a post-collapse scenario, chemical contamination is most concerning near industrial areas, farms, and cities
- If you suspect chemical contamination, distillation is your best option

**Treatment Method Quick Reference**

| Method | Bacteria | Viruses | Protozoa | Chemicals | Sediment |
|--------|----------|---------|----------|-----------|----------|
| Boiling | YES | YES | YES | NO (concentrates) | NO |
| Chlorine (bleach) | YES | YES | Partial (not Crypto) | NO | NO |
| Iodine | YES | YES | Partial (not Crypto) | NO | NO |
| Filtration (0.2 micron) | YES | NO | YES | NO | YES |
| Activated charcoal | Partial | NO | NO | Partial | YES |
| UV light | YES | YES | YES | NO | NO |
| Distillation | YES | YES | YES | YES | YES |
| SODIS (solar) | YES | YES | Partial | NO | NO |

**The key takeaway: no single method handles everything. The best approach combines filtration (removes sediment, bacteria, protozoa) with either boiling or chemical treatment (kills viruses). If chemical contamination is suspected, add distillation or charcoal filtration.**

### 3.1 Boiling — The Gold Standard

Boiling is the single most reliable field water purification method. It kills all biological pathogens — bacteria, viruses, and protozoan cysts — with certainty. No pathogen survives the temperature of boiling water. It requires no special equipment beyond a fire-safe container and a heat source.

**How It Works**
Heat denatures the proteins and disrupts the cell membranes of all microorganisms. At 212 degrees F (100 degrees C) at sea level, all waterborne pathogens are killed within seconds. The recommendation to boil for 1 minute provides an enormous safety margin.

**What It Kills**
- All bacteria: E. coli, cholera, Salmonella, etc.
- All viruses: Hepatitis A, Norovirus, Rotavirus, etc.
- All protozoan cysts: Giardia, Cryptosporidium, Amoeba
- Bacterial spores: most are killed, though some extremely heat-resistant spores (like Clostridium) may require prolonged boiling

**What It Does NOT Remove**
- Chemical contaminants (pesticides, heavy metals, solvents) — boiling actually concentrates these by evaporating some of the water
- Sediment and particulate matter — pre-filter cloudy water through cloth before boiling
- Dissolved minerals — not harmful at natural levels but affect taste
- Radioactive contamination — boiling does not address this

**Step-by-Step Instructions**

1. If the water is cloudy or contains visible particles, pre-filter it. Pour it through a clean cloth (a cotton t-shirt folded 4 times works), a coffee filter, or a bandana. This removes sediment that can shelter pathogens from heat and makes the treated water more palatable.

2. Pour the pre-filtered water into your container. Any heat-resistant container works: a metal pot, a metal cup, a tin can, a glass jar (be careful — rapid temperature changes can crack glass), or even a hollowed log or rock depression if you can get it hot enough (stone boiling — see below).

3. Place the container over your heat source. Build a fire with a good bed of coals for consistent heat. A camp stove with any fuel also works.

4. Bring the water to a full, rolling boil. A rolling boil means large bubbles are vigorously breaking the surface across the entire water surface, not just small bubbles forming on the bottom or sides (those appear well below boiling temperature).

5. Once a rolling boil is established, maintain it for:
   - 1 minute at elevations below 6,500 feet (2,000 meters)
   - 3 minutes at elevations above 6,500 feet (2,000 meters)
   - The longer time at altitude compensates for the lower boiling point of water at reduced atmospheric pressure. At 10,000 feet, water boils at approximately 194 degrees F (90 degrees C) instead of 212 degrees F (100 degrees C). Pathogens still die at this temperature, but the safety margin is reduced, so you boil longer.

6. Remove from heat and let cool. Do not add untreated water or ice to cool it — this recontaminates it.

7. Once cool, transfer to a clean storage container with a lid. Boiled water can taste flat because dissolved gases have been driven off. To improve taste, pour the water back and forth between two clean containers several times to re-aerate it.

**Stone Boiling (When You Have No Metal Container)**
If you have no metal container, you can boil water in any container that holds water — including a wooden bowl, a bark vessel, an animal skin, or a hole lined with clay or a tarp.

1. Build a fire and heat rocks in the fire until they are extremely hot (30 to 45 minutes in a strong fire). Use dense, dry rocks — never use river rocks or porous stones, which can contain trapped moisture that expands into steam and causes the rock to explode violently.
2. Fill your non-metal container with water.
3. Using wooden tongs, green wood sticks, or two flat pieces of wood like chopsticks, carefully lift a hot rock from the fire and place it into the water.
4. The water will immediately begin to sizzle and steam. The rock transfers its heat to the water rapidly.
5. Add more hot rocks as needed until the water reaches a full rolling boil.
6. Maintain the boil by replacing cooling rocks with freshly heated ones from the fire.
7. Remove the rocks once boiling time is complete.
8. Let the water cool, then carefully pour off the clean water, leaving any sediment and ash from the rocks at the bottom.

**Pros**
- Kills all biological pathogens with certainty
- Requires no special supplies or chemicals
- Works on any volume of water
- Method is universally understood — no dosage calculations or timing confusion

**Cons**
- Requires fuel (wood, gas, or other combustible) — in a long-term scenario, fuel is a finite resource
- Takes time: building fire, waiting for boil, waiting for cool-down
- Does not remove chemical contaminants
- Does not remove sediment (pre-filter first)
- Consumes a heat source that might also be needed for cooking and warmth
- Not practical for very large volumes (hundreds of gallons)

### 3.2 Chemical Treatment

Chemical treatment uses disinfectants to kill pathogens in water. It is lightweight, portable, and requires no fire. The three most practical chemical treatments in a post-collapse scenario are household bleach, iodine, and calcium hypochlorite.

#### 3.2.1 Household Bleach (Sodium Hypochlorite)

This is the most accessible chemical treatment because bleach is found in virtually every home, grocery store, and cleaning supply closet.

**Requirements**
- Unscented liquid household bleach containing 8.25% sodium hypochlorite (this is the current standard concentration in the US — check the label)
- If your bleach is an older formula at 6% concentration, use slightly more (see dosage table)
- Do NOT use scented bleach, color-safe bleach, bleach with added cleaners, or splash-less bleach — these contain additives that are toxic to drink

**Dosage**

For 8.25% sodium hypochlorite bleach:

| Water Clarity | Dosage per Gallon | Dosage per Liter |
|---------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Clear water | 2 drops | 0.5 drops (round to 1) |
| Cloudy, colored, or very cold water | 4 drops | 1 drop |

For 6% sodium hypochlorite bleach (older formula):
| Water Clarity | Dosage per Gallon | Dosage per Liter |
|---------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Clear water | 3 drops | 1 drop |
| Cloudy, colored, or very cold water | 6 drops | 2 drops |

One drop is approximately 0.05 milliliters. A standard medicine dropper produces drops of approximately this size.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**

1. If the water is cloudy, pre-filter through a cloth to remove sediment.
2. Measure your water into a clean container.
3. Add the appropriate number of drops of bleach (see dosage table above).
4. Stir or shake the water to mix the bleach thoroughly.
5. Let the water stand for 30 minutes. This contact time is essential — the chlorine needs time to kill pathogens.
6. After 30 minutes, smell the water. It should have a faint chlorine odor (like a swimming pool). If you cannot detect any chlorine smell, add another 2 drops per gallon (for 8.25% bleach) and wait another 15 minutes.
7. If after the second treatment there is still no chlorine smell, the water is too contaminated for bleach treatment alone. The organic matter in the water is consuming all the chlorine before it can disinfect. Pre-filter more aggressively or use a different treatment method.
8. If the chlorine taste is too strong, let the water sit uncovered for a few hours or pour it between containers to aerate it — excess chlorine will dissipate.

**What It Kills**: Bacteria (excellent), viruses (excellent), Giardia (effective with proper contact time — 30 minutes minimum, longer in cold water), some other protozoa.

**What It Does NOT Reliably Kill**: Cryptosporidium. This is the critical limitation of bleach. Cryptosporidium oocysts are highly resistant to chlorine at the concentrations achievable with field treatment. If Cryptosporidium is a concern (and in any livestock or wildlife area, it should be), combine bleach treatment with filtration through a filter rated at 1 micron or less, or boil instead.

**Shelf Life**: Liquid bleach degrades over time. Unopened, it retains full strength for approximately 6 to 12 months from manufacture date. After opening, effectiveness declines within 3 to 6 months. After 1 year, even unopened bleach may have lost enough strength to be unreliable.

This shelf life limitation is why calcium hypochlorite (see 3.2.3) is the superior long-term stockpile chemical — it stores for 10+ years.

**Pros**
- Available everywhere — every grocery and dollar store has bleach
- Extremely cheap
- Lightweight and portable (a small dropper bottle treats hundreds of gallons)
- Effective against bacteria and viruses
- Provides residual protection (the chlorine continues to protect the water for hours after treatment)

**Cons**
- Does not kill Cryptosporidium
- Degrades on shelf — limited useful life
- Requires careful dosing (too little is ineffective, too much is harmful)
- Does not remove chemical contaminants or sediment
- Unpleasant taste at higher doses
- Less effective in cold, cloudy, or high-organic-content water

#### 3.2.2 Iodine

Iodine is available as tablets (such as Potable Aqua), crystals, or tincture (liquid iodine solution from a first aid kit).

**Dosage for Iodine Tablets (typically tetraglycine hydroperiodide)**
- Follow the manufacturer's instructions on the bottle, but the standard dose is:
- 1 tablet per quart (liter) of clear water
- 2 tablets per quart of cloudy or cold water
- Contact time: 30 minutes for clear, warm water; 60 minutes or more for cold or cloudy water

**Dosage for 2% Tincture of Iodine (liquid)**
- 5 drops per quart (liter) of clear water
- 10 drops per quart of cloudy or cold water
- Contact time: same as tablets — 30 to 60 minutes

**Step-by-Step Instructions**
1. Pre-filter cloudy water through cloth.
2. Add the appropriate dose of iodine tablets or drops.
3. If using tablets, let them dissolve completely (crush them between two spoons if they are slow to dissolve in cold water).
4. Cap the container loosely and tip it to allow a small amount of treated water to wash over the cap threads and rim — untreated water trapped in the threads can recontaminate the treated water.
5. Wait the full contact time (30 minutes for warm, clear water; up to 4 hours for very cold or cloudy water).
6. Iodine-treated water has a distinctive, unpleasant taste. After the contact time, you can add vitamin C (ascorbic acid) powder or a vitamin C tablet to neutralize the iodine taste. This also neutralizes the iodine's disinfecting power, so only add the vitamin C AFTER the full contact time.

**What It Kills**: Bacteria (excellent), viruses (excellent), Giardia (effective with adequate contact time).

**What It Does NOT Reliably Kill**: Cryptosporidium. Like chlorine, iodine at field-treatable concentrations does not reliably kill Crypto oocysts.

**Critical Limitations**
- Do NOT use iodine for water treatment if you are pregnant or suspect pregnancy — iodine can harm fetal thyroid development
- Do NOT use long-term (more than 3 to 4 weeks continuously) — chronic iodine exposure can cause thyroid problems
- Do NOT use if you have a known thyroid condition or iodine allergy
- Not recommended for children under age 4 without medical guidance

**Shelf Life**: Iodine tablets, properly sealed, have a shelf life of 4 to 5 years. Once the bottle is opened and exposed to air and moisture, they degrade within months. Tincture of iodine has a shelf life of 2 to 3 years.

**Pros**
- Lightweight and portable (a small bottle treats hundreds of quarts)
- Faster than bleach in some conditions
- Available in pharmacies and outdoor/camping stores
- Effective against bacteria, viruses, and Giardia

**Cons**
- Does not kill Cryptosporidium
- Not safe for pregnant women, children under 4, or people with thyroid conditions
- Not for long-term use (weeks, not months)
- Unpleasant taste
- Degrades relatively quickly after opening
- More expensive than bleach

#### 3.2.3 Calcium Hypochlorite (Pool Shock)

Calcium hypochlorite is the granular or powdered form of chlorine used to treat swimming pools. For long-term survival water treatment, it is far superior to liquid bleach because of its shelf life: properly stored, calcium hypochlorite retains its potency for 10 years or more.

**Selecting the Right Product**
- Look for calcium hypochlorite with 68% to 78% available chlorine
- The label must say "calcium hypochlorite" — NOT sodium dichloro-s-triazinetrione (dichlor) or trichloro-s-triazinetrione (trichlor), which are different pool chemicals and are NOT safe for drinking water treatment
- The product should contain NO algaecides, clarifiers, or other additives — just calcium hypochlorite
- A 1-pound bag can treat approximately 10,000 gallons of water — making this the most cost-effective chemical treatment by far

**Making a Stock Solution**
You do not add granular calcium hypochlorite directly to drinking water. First, you make a concentrated stock solution, then you use that solution to treat your water.

Step 1: Make the stock solution
1. Add 1 level teaspoon (approximately 4 to 5 grams) of calcium hypochlorite to 1 gallon of water
2. Stir until dissolved. Undissolved granules will settle to the bottom — this is fine
3. This produces a stock solution of approximately 500 ppm (parts per million) chlorine — roughly equivalent to regular household bleach

Step 2: Treat your drinking water
1. Use the stock solution exactly as you would use liquid bleach (see Section 3.2.1 dosage table)
2. Add 2 drops of stock solution per gallon of clear water, or 4 drops per gallon of cloudy water
3. Wait 30 minutes, check for chlorine smell, proceed as with bleach

**Important Notes**
- The stock solution degrades like liquid bleach — make only what you will use within 1 to 2 weeks
- The dry granular calcium hypochlorite retains potency for a decade or more when stored in an airtight container in a cool, dark, dry place
- Store the dry chemical away from other chemicals, organic materials, and metal. It is a strong oxidizer and can cause fires or explosions if it contacts oils, solvents, or organic matter in concentrated form
- Handle with care — wear gloves if available. Avoid breathing the dust. If it contacts skin, wash immediately with water
- Keep the dry chemical absolutely dry — moisture causes it to degrade

**Pros**
- Extraordinary shelf life (10+ years for dry granular form)
- Incredibly cost-effective (1 pound treats 10,000 gallons)
- Lightweight and compact
- Same effectiveness as bleach against bacteria and viruses
- The best chemical water treatment for long-term stockpiling

**Cons**
- Does not kill Cryptosporidium (same limitation as all chlorine-based treatments)
- Requires the two-step process (stock solution then treatment)
- The dry chemical is a hazardous oxidizer — requires careful storage
- Must be the correct product (plain calcium hypochlorite, no additives)
- Not as universally available as household bleach

#### 3.2.4 Chlorine Dioxide

Chlorine dioxide (ClO2) is a more powerful disinfectant than regular chlorine. It is available as tablets (such as Aquamira, Katadyn Micropur MP1) and as a two-part liquid drop system.

**How It Works**
Chlorine dioxide is a different chemical from regular chlorine (sodium hypochlorite). It is an oxidizer that disrupts pathogen cell membranes. Importantly, it is more effective against Cryptosporidium than regular chlorine, though it requires longer contact time.

**Dosage (Tablets)**
- Follow manufacturer's instructions on the specific product
- Typical dose: 1 tablet per 1 quart (1 liter) of water
- Contact time: 30 minutes for bacteria and viruses, 4 hours for Cryptosporidium
- The 4-hour contact time for Crypto is a significant limitation — plan accordingly

**Dosage (Two-Part Liquid System — e.g., Aquamira)**
- Part A (chlorine dioxide) and Part B (phosphoric acid activator) are mixed together first
- Typical: 7 drops Part A + 7 drops Part B in the mixing cap. Wait 5 minutes for the mixture to turn yellow-green. Then add to 1 quart (1 liter) of water.
- Contact time: 15 to 30 minutes for bacteria and viruses, 4 hours for Cryptosporidium

**What It Kills**: Bacteria (excellent), viruses (excellent), Giardia (excellent), Cryptosporidium (effective, but requires 4 hours of contact time).

**Pros**
- More effective against Cryptosporidium than chlorine or iodine
- Pleasant taste (better than iodine, comparable to bleach)
- Safe for pregnant women and people with thyroid conditions
- Tablets have a 4 to 5 year shelf life
- Effective across a wider pH range than regular chlorine

**Cons**
- More expensive than bleach or calcium hypochlorite
- 4-hour wait time for Cryptosporidium protection is very long
- Less available than bleach (specialty outdoor and camping stores)
- Does not remove chemical contaminants or sediment

### 3.3 Filtration

Filtration physically removes contaminants by passing water through a material with pores small enough to trap pathogens and particles. The critical specification is the pore size, measured in microns.

**Micron Rating Guide**
- 1.0 micron: removes Giardia (8-12 micron cysts), Cryptosporidium (4-6 micron oocysts), most bacteria, and sediment. Does NOT reliably remove viruses (0.02-0.3 microns) or all bacteria species
- 0.2 micron (also written as 0.2 um): removes all bacteria, all protozoa, and all sediment. Still does NOT remove most viruses
- 0.02 micron: removes viruses in addition to bacteria and protozoa. Filters at this level are sometimes called "purifiers" rather than "filters"
- Activated charcoal/carbon: removes some chemical contaminants, chlorine, and improves taste. Does NOT reliably remove pathogens by itself (it is not a size-exclusion filter). Best used as a secondary stage after a micron filter or before chemical treatment

**The key principle: filtration alone (unless rated at 0.02 microns or below) does not remove viruses. In areas with potential human sewage contamination, combine filtration with boiling or chemical treatment.**

#### 3.3.1 Commercial Filters

If you can scavenge commercial water filters from homes, camping stores, or emergency supply caches, they provide excellent filtration with minimal effort.

**Gravity-Fed Filters (e.g., Berkey, ProOne, Doulton)**
- How they work: water is poured into an upper chamber, passes through ceramic or carbon filter elements by gravity, and drips into a lower chamber for collection
- Flow rate: 0.5 to 2 gallons per hour depending on filter type and number of elements
- Filter life: 1,000 to 6,000 gallons per element depending on source water quality
- Micron rating: typically 0.2 to 1.0 microns
- Excellent for base camp or home use — set it up and let gravity do the work
- Maintenance: scrub the outer surface of ceramic elements with a clean brush or ScotchBrite pad when flow rate slows. This removes the layer of trapped contaminants and restores flow

**Pump Filters (e.g., MSR, Katadyn)**
- How they work: a hand pump draws water through a filter element
- Flow rate: 1 to 2 liters per minute with steady pumping
- Filter life: 500 to 2,000 liters depending on model
- Micron rating: typically 0.2 to 0.3 microns
- Good for collecting water on the move from streams and lakes
- Maintenance: back-flush per manufacturer's instructions when flow rate decreases

**Straw-Type Filters (e.g., LifeStraw, Sawyer)**
- How they work: you suck water directly through a hollow-fiber membrane filter
- Flow rate: limited by how fast you can suck — roughly 0.5 liters per minute
- Filter life: 1,000 to 100,000 gallons depending on model (Sawyer Squeeze claims 100,000 gallons)
- Micron rating: typically 0.1 to 0.2 microns
- Extremely lightweight and portable — good for individual use
- Limitation: you can only drink from them in real-time (no storage); some models can be attached to bottles or hydration bladders for on-the-go use
- Maintenance: back-flush with clean water using the included syringe (Sawyer models) when flow rate decreases

#### 3.3.2 DIY Bio-Sand Filter

A bio-sand filter (BSF) is one of the most effective improvised water treatment systems you can build. It uses biological processes and physical filtration to remove pathogens and sediment. Over 200,000 bio-sand filters are in use worldwide in developing countries, and they have been extensively studied by the World Health Organization.

**How It Works**
Water is poured into the top of a container filled with carefully layered sand and gravel. As it passes through the sand, three processes purify it:
1. Physical filtration: sand grains trap particles, sediment, and larger organisms
2. Biological action: a living biofilm (called the "schmutzdecke" or "biological layer") develops in the top 1 to 2 inches of sand over 2 to 3 weeks. This biofilm contains beneficial microorganisms that consume and destroy pathogens
3. Adsorption: pathogens stick to the surface of sand grains and are eventually consumed by the biofilm organisms

**Effectiveness** (when properly built and matured)
- Bacteria: 90 to 99% removal
- Protozoa (Giardia, Cryptosporidium): 99 to 99.9% removal
- Viruses: 70 to 99% removal (the biological layer is key — an immature filter has low virus removal)
- Turbidity/sediment: 95%+ removal
- Note: a BSF should still be paired with chemical treatment (bleach) for maximum safety, especially during the first 2 to 3 weeks before the biological layer matures

**Full construction instructions are provided in Section 4.2.**

#### 3.3.3 DIY Charcoal Filter

Activated charcoal (also called activated carbon) is charcoal that has been treated to increase its surface area and adsorptive capacity. It removes chemical contaminants, improves taste, and reduces odor. It is NOT effective as a standalone pathogen filter — always combine it with another treatment method.

**Making Charcoal**
1. Build a hot fire using hardwood (oak, maple, hickory, etc.). Softwoods (pine, cedar) produce inferior charcoal
2. Let the fire burn down until you have a bed of glowing red coals
3. Deprive the coals of oxygen: bury them in a pit covered with dirt, place them in a sealed metal container, or smother them with green vegetation and dirt. The goal is to stop the combustion without adding water (which produces a less porous product)
4. Wait 4 to 8 hours for the coals to cool completely
5. Dig up or open the container. You should have black, lightweight charcoal pieces that break cleanly and feel very light for their size

**Activating Charcoal (field method)**
True commercial activation requires steam treatment at 1,000+ degrees C, which is difficult in the field. A field approximation:
1. Crush your charcoal into small pieces (pea-sized or smaller)
2. Soak the crushed charcoal in a 25% solution of calcium chloride (road de-icer) or lemon juice for 24 hours. The acid treatment opens up the pore structure
3. Rinse the charcoal thoroughly with clean water
4. Bake or dry it in a hot fire (but not to the point of re-igniting) until completely dry
5. This field-activated charcoal is less effective than commercial activated carbon but significantly better than plain charcoal

**Building a Charcoal Filter**
1. Take a container — a 2-liter bottle with the bottom cut off (turned upside down to create a funnel), a bucket with a hole in the bottom, or a length of PVC pipe capped at one end with a small drain hole
2. Place a layer of clean cloth or a coffee filter at the bottom (the outlet end) to prevent charcoal from washing through
3. Add a 3 to 4 inch layer of fine gravel or small stones
4. Add a 6 to 8 inch layer of crushed charcoal (smaller pieces = more surface area = better filtration)
5. Add another 2 to 3 inch layer of fine gravel on top
6. Add a 2 to 3 inch layer of clean sand on top (this acts as a pre-filter to protect the charcoal layer)
7. Place a cloth over the very top to distribute water evenly and prevent channeling

**Using the Charcoal Filter**
1. Pour water into the top slowly
2. Let it drip through by gravity
3. Collect the filtered water at the bottom
4. The first gallon through a new filter will be gray or black — discard it or re-filter it. The color is harmless charcoal fines washing out
5. Flow rate: approximately 0.25 to 0.5 gallons per hour for a standard bottle-sized filter
6. Replace the charcoal when the filter no longer improves taste or when flow rate drops dramatically — typically after 50 to 100 gallons

**What Charcoal Removes**: chlorine and chlorine taste, many organic chemicals, some pesticides and herbicides, some heavy metals, bad taste and odor.

**What Charcoal Does NOT Remove**: bacteria, viruses, protozoa, dissolved minerals, salt. It is NOT a biological purifier. Always follow charcoal filtration with boiling or chemical treatment.

#### 3.3.4 DIY Ceramic Filter

Ceramic filters have been used for centuries. A porous ceramic vessel allows water to seep through slowly while trapping bacteria and protozoa in the ceramic's microscopic pores.

**How to Make One from Terracotta**
If you have access to clay and can build a fire, you can make a ceramic filter:

1. Obtain natural clay (dig it from riverbanks, excavation sites, or pottery supply stores)
2. Mix the clay with a combustible material — sawdust, rice husks, or finely chopped dry grass — at a ratio of approximately 60% clay to 40% combustible material by volume. This combustible material burns away during firing, leaving microscopic pores in the ceramic
3. Knead the mixture thoroughly with water until it forms a workable dough with no lumps
4. Shape the dough into a pot or bowl form — about 8 to 10 inches in diameter and 3 to 4 inches deep. Make the walls approximately 0.75 to 1 inch thick. The shape should be like a flower pot without a drainage hole
5. Let the formed pot dry in the shade for 3 to 5 days (rapid drying in sun causes cracking)
6. Fire the pot in a kiln or a large bonfire. The firing temperature needs to reach at least 1,500 degrees F (815 degrees C). In a bonfire, this means a large, hot fire sustained for at least 3 to 4 hours with the pot buried in the center of the coals
7. After cooling, the pot should be hard, ring when tapped, and have a slightly rough, porous texture
8. Test for flow: fill the pot with water and place it on a support over a collection container. Water should seep through the walls at a rate of approximately 1 to 2 liters per hour. If water flows freely (too fast), the pores are too large. If no water comes through after several hours, the ceramic is too dense

**Using a Ceramic Filter**
1. Place the ceramic pot inside a larger bucket or pot, sitting on a support (sticks, a wire rack, or stones) so that filtered water drips into the collection container below
2. Fill the ceramic pot with water from your source
3. Water seeps through the ceramic walls, leaving bacteria and protozoa trapped in the ceramic
4. Collect the filtered water from the outer container
5. Clean the ceramic pot periodically by scrubbing the inside with a clean brush
6. Expected output: 3 to 5 gallons per day

**Effectiveness**: removes approximately 99% of bacteria and 99.9% of protozoa. Does NOT reliably remove viruses (the pores, while small, are not small enough). Follow with chemical treatment for complete protection.

#### 3.3.5 Cloth Filtration (Pre-Filtering Only)

Cloth filtration is a pre-treatment step, not a stand-alone purification method. A folded cloth cannot remove bacteria, viruses, or most protozoa. It can remove visible sediment, debris, insect larvae, and larger organic particles, which improves the effectiveness of subsequent treatment methods and makes the water more palatable.

**The Sari Cloth Method**
Research in Bangladesh showed that filtering water through folded sari cloth (a tightly woven cotton fabric) reduced cholera cases by approximately 48%. While 48% is far from adequate as the only treatment, it demonstrates that even simple cloth filtration provides meaningful pathogen reduction when combined with other methods.

**Folding Technique**
1. Take a piece of tightly woven cotton cloth — a cotton t-shirt, a bandana, a clean dishcloth, or a piece of muslin
2. Fold it to create at least 4 layers (more layers = finer filtration but slower flow)
3. Place it over the opening of your collection container and secure it with a string, rubber band, or by tucking the edges around the rim
4. Pour water through the cloth slowly
5. The cloth traps sediment, plant material, insect larvae, and larger organisms
6. Wring out and rinse the cloth frequently to maintain flow rate
7. Boil the cloth periodically to kill any pathogens trapped in it

**What Cloth Removes**: visible sediment, debris, insect larvae, some copepods (which carry cholera bacteria and Guinea worm larvae), plant material.

**What Cloth Does NOT Remove**: bacteria, viruses, protozoa, chemicals, dissolved contaminants. Always follow cloth filtration with boiling, chemical treatment, or filtration through a proper filter.

### 3.4 Solar Disinfection (SODIS)

SODIS uses ultraviolet radiation from the sun to kill pathogens in water. It is used by over 5 million people worldwide in developing countries where other treatment methods are unavailable. It costs nothing and requires only a clear plastic bottle and sunlight.

**How It Works**
UVA radiation (315-400 nanometers) from sunlight penetrates clear water and damages the DNA of microorganisms, preventing them from reproducing. The combination of UV radiation and the mild heating from the sun (temperatures above 122 degrees F / 50 degrees C significantly boost effectiveness) creates a synergistic disinfection effect.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**

1. Obtain a clear PET plastic bottle (PETE or PET #1 — the standard disposable water or soda bottle). Bottles up to 2 liters work. Larger bottles are too thick for UV to penetrate adequately. Glass bottles also work but are heavier and more fragile. Do NOT use PVC bottles (PVC #3) — they can leach harmful chemicals in sunlight.

2. If the water is cloudy or turbid, pre-filter it through cloth until it is reasonably clear. SODIS does not work well in turbid water because suspended particles block UV penetration. A simple test: fill the bottle and hold it up against a printed newspaper. If you can read the text through the water, it is clear enough for SODIS. If not, pre-filter more.

3. Fill the bottle about 3/4 full, cap it, and shake vigorously for 20 seconds. This aerates the water, increasing the dissolved oxygen content, which enhances the pathogen-killing effect.

4. Fill the bottle the rest of the way and cap it.

5. Place the bottle on a reflective or dark surface in full, direct sunlight. A corrugated metal roof is ideal — the reflectivity increases UV exposure and the heat boosts the thermal component. A sheet of aluminum foil or a white surface also works. Laying the bottle on its side (horizontally) provides better UV exposure than standing it upright.

6. Leave the bottle in sunlight for the required time:

| Conditions | Exposure Time |
|-----------|---------------|
| Full sun, clear sky | 6 hours |
| Partly cloudy (50% cloud cover) | 6 hours |
| Mostly cloudy (continuous clouds) | 2 full days (expose both days) |
| Overcast / rainy | SODIS is not effective. Use another method. |
| Water temperature reaches 122 F (50 C)+ | 1 hour (thermal boost) |

7. After the exposure period, the water is ready to drink. Leave it in the bottle for storage — recontamination occurs when you transfer it to a dirty container.

**What It Kills**: Bacteria (excellent after adequate exposure), viruses (effective), Giardia (effective), Cryptosporidium (partially effective — longer exposure needed, not fully reliable).

**What It Does NOT Remove**: chemical contaminants, sediment (pre-filter first), and may not fully inactivate Cryptosporidium.

**Pros**
- Free — no cost, no chemicals, no special equipment
- Simple — anyone can do it with a plastic bottle
- Scalable — use many bottles simultaneously
- No taste alteration

**Cons**
- Slow — 6 hours minimum, potentially 2 days
- Dependent on weather — useless in overcast/rainy conditions
- Small batch sizes (2 liters at a time per bottle)
- Not reliable against Cryptosporidium
- Requires clear water (pre-filter turbid water)
- UV-blocking bottles (colored, opaque, or deeply scratched) do not work

### 3.5 UV Treatment (Electronic)

Battery-powered UV devices like the SteriPEN use ultraviolet light (specifically UVC at 254 nanometers) to disinfect water quickly and effectively.

**How It Works**
The device emits UVC light, which is far more intense than natural sunlight UVA. This energy directly damages the DNA and RNA of pathogens, rendering them unable to reproduce and cause infection.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**
1. Pre-filter water to remove turbidity (UV requires clear water to be effective)
2. Fill a container with up to the volume rated for your device (typically 0.5 to 1 liter)
3. Insert the UV lamp into the water
4. Press the activation button
5. Stir the device gently in the water for the indicated cycle time (typically 90 seconds for 1 liter)
6. The device signals when the cycle is complete (usually an LED light)
7. Water is ready to drink

**What It Kills**: Bacteria (excellent), viruses (excellent), Giardia (excellent), Cryptosporidium (excellent — unlike chemical treatment, UV is highly effective against Crypto).

**What It Does NOT Remove**: chemical contaminants, sediment, dissolved minerals. UV does not physically remove anything — it sterilizes in place.

**Pros**
- Fast (90 seconds per liter)
- Effective against Cryptosporidium (unlike chemicals)
- Effective against all biological pathogens
- No taste alteration
- No chemicals needed

**Cons**
- Requires batteries (typically CR123A lithium or rechargeable via USB) — battery dependency is a critical limitation in a long-term collapse scenario
- The UV lamp has a limited lifespan (approximately 3,000 to 8,000 activations depending on model)
- Requires clear water — turbidity drastically reduces effectiveness
- Treats small volumes at a time (0.5 to 1 liter)
- Fragile electronic device — can break
- No residual protection — the water can be recontaminated immediately after treatment
- Expensive compared to other methods
- When batteries die and no replacements are available, the device is useless

**Battery Considerations for Long-Term Use**
- Stock up on batteries (CR123A lithium cells have a 10-year shelf life)
- Solar battery chargers can recharge rechargeable versions (USB-chargeable SteriPEN models)
- Calculate: at 3,000 activations treating 1 liter each, a single device can treat 3,000 liters (approximately 790 gallons) over its lifetime

### 3.6 Distillation

Distillation is the most thorough water purification method available. It removes biological pathogens AND chemical contaminants, including dissolved salts, heavy metals, pesticides, and radioactive particles. It is the only practical field method for making saltwater drinkable or for purifying chemically contaminated water.

**How It Works**
Water is heated to boiling. The steam (water vapor) rises and leaves behind everything else — pathogens are killed by the heat, chemicals remain in the boiling pot, salt stays behind, heavy metals do not evaporate. The steam is then cooled (condensed) back into liquid water, which is collected as pure distilled water.

**What It Removes**: everything. Bacteria, viruses, protozoa, salt, heavy metals, chemicals, pesticides, radioactive particles, sediment.

**Limitations**: distillation does not remove contaminants with a boiling point lower than water (some volatile organic compounds, or VOCs). However, these are relatively uncommon in natural water sources.

#### 3.6.1 Solar Still (Ground Level)

A solar still uses the sun's energy to evaporate water from moist soil or contaminated water, then condenses the vapor on a sheet of clear plastic.

**Materials**
- A sheet of clear plastic (4 feet by 4 feet minimum) — plastic sheeting, a clear trash bag, or a plastic tarp
- A collection container (cup, bowl, jar)
- A digging tool (shovel, stick, hands)
- A small stone or weight
- Optional: a drinking tube (surgical tubing, vinyl tubing, or even a hollow plant stem) so you can drink without opening the still

**Construction**
1. Choose a location with maximum sun exposure and moist soil (near a dry creek bed, in vegetation, or any area where the soil feels damp below the surface)
2. Dig a hole approximately 3 feet in diameter and 2 feet deep. Make the sides sloped, not vertical
3. If soil is dry, add moisture sources: pour contaminated or saltwater into the bottom, place cut vegetation (non-toxic) in the hole, or urinate in the hole (the still purifies it). The more moisture you add, the more output you get
4. Place your collection container in the center of the bottom of the hole
5. If you have a drinking tube, run it from the collection container up and out of the hole, securing the end outside for drinking access
6. Cover the hole with the clear plastic sheet
7. Seal the edges with rocks, dirt, or sand all the way around — the seal must be airtight for maximum efficiency
8. Place a small stone or weight on the center of the plastic, directly above the collection container. This creates a low point where condensation will drip
9. The weight should push the plastic down to form a cone shape, with the lowest point directly over the container, approximately 2 to 3 inches above the container's opening

**How It Produces Water**
- Sun heats the ground and moisture sources inside the sealed hole
- Water evaporates from the soil and any added liquid
- The vapor rises and condenses on the underside of the cooler plastic sheet
- Condensation runs down the plastic to the lowest point (where the stone is) and drips into the container
- The resulting water is distilled — pure and safe to drink

**Expected Yield**: 0.5 to 2 quarts (0.5 to 2 liters) per day, depending on soil moisture, sun intensity, and temperature. This is not a high-volume source.

**Tips to Increase Output**
- Build multiple stills
- Maximize the moisture in the hole (add vegetation, pour in dirty water)
- Ensure the plastic seal is airtight
- Choose the sunniest location possible
- Replace vegetation every 1 to 2 days as it dries out

#### 3.6.2 Stovetop Distillation Setup

For larger volumes or when you have a fire or stove, you can build a distillation apparatus that produces several gallons per day.

**Basic Setup**
You need: a pot with a lid, a length of metal or heat-resistant tubing (copper tubing is ideal), a collection container, and a way to cool the tube (cold water, wet cloths, or ambient air).

**Step-by-Step Construction**

1. Take a large pot and fill it with the water to be purified
2. Drill or punch a hole in the pot lid (or use a pressure cooker — the steam outlet is already there)
3. Insert one end of a copper or stainless steel tube into the hole in the lid. Seal around the tube with clay, dough, wet cloth, or silicone — steam must not escape around the tube
4. Run the tube out and downward from the pot lid. The tube should slope gently downward from the pot to the collection container
5. The tube needs to be cooled so that steam condenses inside it. Options:
   - Coil the tube through a bucket of cold water (this is the most efficient — it is a traditional "worm" condenser)
   - Wrap the tube in wet cloths and keep the cloths moist
   - Run the tube through a shady area in cool ambient temperatures
6. Position the outlet end of the tube over a clean collection container
7. Heat the pot to a steady boil
8. Steam exits through the tube, condenses as it cools, and drips out the end as pure distilled water
9. Keep the heat at a moderate, steady level — a violent boil can push contaminated water droplets (not just steam) into the tube

**Improvised Distillation Without Tubing**
If you have no tube:
1. Place a pot of water on a heat source
2. Invert a clean, concave lid (like a wok lid or domed pot lid) on top of the pot. Place it upside-down so the dome faces inward
3. Place ice, snow, or cold wet cloths on top of the inverted lid to keep it cool
4. As water boils, steam rises, hits the cool inverted lid, condenses, and runs down the dome shape to the center point
5. Place a collection container inside the pot, positioned to catch drips from the center of the inverted lid — or periodically tilt the lid to pour collected distilled water into a separate container
6. This is less efficient than a tube system but works with common kitchen items

**Expected Yield**: 0.5 to 1 gallon per hour with a strong heat source and good condensation.

**Pros**
- Removes ALL contaminants — biological, chemical, dissolved, and particulate
- The only practical field method for desalinating saltwater
- Works on any water source, no matter how contaminated

**Cons**
- Requires significant fuel (keeping water at a boil for hours)
- Relatively complex setup compared to other methods
- Slow for the amount of fuel consumed
- Produces flat-tasting water (re-aerate by pouring between containers)
- Does not remove VOCs with boiling points lower than water (uncommon concern)

### 3.7 Combination Approaches

No single purification method is perfect for all situations. The most reliable approach combines multiple methods to cover each other's weaknesses.

**Recommended Combination 1: Filtration + Boiling** (best for biological safety)
1. Pre-filter through cloth to remove large debris
2. Filter through a 0.2-micron commercial filter or a bio-sand filter to remove bacteria, protozoa, and sediment
3. Boil the filtered water for 1 minute (3 minutes above 6,500 feet)
4. This combination handles: all bacteria, all viruses, all protozoa, and all sediment

**Recommended Combination 2: Filtration + Chemical Treatment** (when fuel is scarce)
1. Pre-filter through cloth
2. Filter through a 0.2-micron filter or bio-sand filter
3. Treat with bleach (2 drops per gallon for clear water) and wait 30 minutes
4. This handles: bacteria, viruses, protozoa (including Cryptosporidium via filtration), and sediment

**Recommended Combination 3: Filtration + Charcoal + Chemical Treatment** (for suspected chemical contamination)
1. Pre-filter through cloth
2. Filter through a charcoal filter to remove chemical contaminants and improve taste
3. Filter through a bio-sand or commercial filter to remove biological pathogens
4. Treat with bleach for additional security
5. This provides the broadest protection achievable with field methods, short of distillation

**Recommended Combination 4: Distillation + Charcoal** (for severely contaminated water)
1. Distill the water (removes nearly everything)
2. Run the distilled water through a charcoal filter (catches any VOCs that may have carried over)
3. This is the most thorough purification possible in the field

**The Layered Defense Principle**
Think of water purification like armor — multiple layers provide better protection than a single thick layer. When in doubt, add another treatment step. The energy and time cost of over-treating water is trivial compared to the cost of a waterborne illness that can incapacitate or kill you.

## Section 4: DIY Water Systems — Detailed Build Instructions

The methods in Section 3 address immediate, short-term water treatment. This section covers building permanent or semi-permanent water infrastructure that provides reliable, ongoing water supply for a household or small community. These systems represent investments of labor and materials that pay dividends for months or years.

### 4.1 Rainwater Catchment System

A properly built rainwater catchment system can be your single most reliable long-term water source. Even in semi-arid regions receiving only 15 inches of rain per year, a house-sized roof can collect thousands of gallons annually.

**Overview of Components**
1. Collection surface (roof)
2. Gutters and downspouts
3. First-flush diverter
4. Pre-filter (leaf screen / debris filter)
5. Storage tank(s)
6. Overflow outlet
7. Tap or spigot for drawing water
8. Post-collection treatment (filtration and/or disinfection)

**Step-by-Step Build Instructions**

**Step 1: Assess Your Collection Surface**
- Measure the roof or collection surface: length x width = square footage
- For irregularly shaped roofs, break them into rectangles and triangles and sum the areas
- Note the roof material: metal is best (cleanest, smoothest), asphalt shingles are acceptable, wood shakes are acceptable but contribute more organic matter, thatch or sod roofs collect very little water and contribute heavy contamination
- A tarp stretched between poles can substitute for a roof: use a waterproof, non-toxic tarp (not treated with mildew chemicals). UV-resistant tarps last longer in sun

**Step 2: Install Gutters**
- Gutters run along the lower edge of the roof, catching water as it sheets off
- Commercial aluminum or vinyl gutters from a hardware store are ideal but any channel works
- Improvised gutters: split PVC pipe (cut a 4-inch pipe in half lengthwise), sheet metal bent into a V or U channel, bamboo split in half with internal nodes knocked out, wooden planks nailed into a V trough
- Slope the gutter toward the downspout: a drop of approximately 1/4 inch per foot of gutter length ensures water flows to the collection point and does not pool
- Attach gutters to the roof fascia or rafter tails with screws, wire, or rope
- The gutter opening should be positioned just below the roof edge so that water dripping off the roof falls into the gutter, not behind it

**Step 3: Install the Downspout**
- The downspout runs vertically from the low end of the gutter down to the first-flush diverter and storage tank
- Any pipe or tube works: PVC pipe, metal pipe, flexible hose, or bamboo
- 2 to 4 inch diameter is sufficient for most residential-scale systems
- Use elbows or curved sections to redirect the flow as needed

**Step 4: Build and Install the First-Flush Diverter**
(Detailed instructions were provided in Section 2.3. Expanded version below.)

The first-flush diverter captures the initial dirty water from the roof and prevents it from entering your storage tank.

Materials:
- A length of PVC pipe, 3 to 4 inch diameter, approximately 3 to 4 feet long
- A PVC cap for the bottom
- A PVC T-fitting that matches your downspout and diverter pipe
- PVC cement or waterproof sealant
- A small drill bit (1/16 inch)

Assembly:
1. Glue or press-fit the cap onto the bottom of the 3 to 4 foot pipe
2. Drill a 1/16 inch hole through the bottom cap. This tiny hole allows the diverter to drain slowly between rain events, resetting itself
3. Install the T-fitting at the top of the downspout where it meets the pipe leading to the storage tank
4. The T-fitting should direct water first into the vertical diverter pipe (by gravity — water falls down)
5. As the diverter pipe fills, the water level rises to the T-junction, and subsequent water is redirected horizontally (or into the next downward section) toward the storage tank
6. The diverter pipe volume should equal approximately 0.5 to 1 gallon per 100 square feet of roof area. For a 1,200 square foot roof, that is 6 to 12 gallons. A 4-inch diameter pipe that is 4 feet long holds approximately 2.6 gallons; you may need multiple diverter pipes or a larger diameter for big roofs

Without PVC (improvised version):
- Use any sealed container (a bucket, a jug) positioned below the downspout
- Arrange the plumbing so the first water fills the bucket
- Once the bucket is full, water overflows into a channel that leads to the storage tank
- Manually empty the bucket after each rain event to reset the system

**Step 5: Install a Pre-Filter / Leaf Screen**
- Before water enters the storage tank, pass it through a screen to catch leaves, insects, bird droppings, and other debris
- A simple window screen or hardware cloth (1/4 inch mesh) placed over the tank inlet works
- A more effective option: a "rain head" filter — a vertical pipe section with a fine mesh screen and a debris cleanout at the bottom
- Clean the screen after every significant rain event

**Step 6: Set Up the Storage Tank**
- See Section 5 for detailed storage container options and sizing
- Position the tank on a stable, level surface. A full 275-gallon IBC tote weighs over 2,200 pounds — it must be on solid ground, a concrete pad, or compacted gravel
- Elevate the tank if possible (on a platform, hillside, or raised pad) to allow gravity-fed distribution through a tap at the bottom
- All openings must be screened to prevent mosquitoes from breeding inside (mosquitoes breed in any standing water)
- The tank must be opaque or kept in the dark — light penetration promotes algae growth
- Install an overflow pipe near the top of the tank that directs excess water away from the foundation of the tank and any buildings

**Step 7: Install a Tap or Spigot**
- A standard garden hose spigot (hose bib) installed near the bottom of the tank allows you to draw water by gravity
- Install the tap 2 to 3 inches above the very bottom of the tank, not at the absolute bottom — this prevents sediment that settles to the bottom from being drawn into your supply
- If the tank is elevated at least 3 to 4 feet above the point of use, gravity provides adequate pressure for a slow, steady flow through a hose or pipe

**Step 8: Post-Collection Treatment**
- Rainwater collected through a properly built system with a first-flush diverter and pre-filter is relatively clean but should still be treated before drinking
- Minimum treatment: add 2 drops of bleach (8.25% sodium hypochlorite) per gallon when filling your daily-use container
- Better treatment: filter through a bio-sand filter or commercial filter, then add bleach
- Best treatment: filter through bio-sand filter, then through a charcoal filter, then add bleach

**Maintenance Schedule**
- After every rainstorm: clear debris from gutters and pre-filter screen
- Monthly: inspect the first-flush diverter drain hole (clear it if clogged), check the tank for algae growth, inspect all connections for leaks
- Every 6 months: clean the inside of the storage tank (drain it, scrub the walls, refill)
- Annually: inspect the collection surface (roof) for damage, replace degraded components

### 4.2 Bio-Sand Filter — Complete Construction Guide

The bio-sand filter (BSF) is one of the most proven, effective, and sustainable water treatment technologies available for non-industrial use. When properly built and maintained, it removes over 99% of protozoa, up to 99% of bacteria, and 70-99% of viruses.

**Design Principles**
- The filter uses layers of increasingly fine material from bottom to top: coarse gravel, fine gravel, coarse sand, fine sand
- Water enters the top, passes through the sand layers, and exits through a pipe at the bottom
- A biological layer (biofilm) develops in the top 1 to 2 inches of fine sand over 2 to 3 weeks of use. This biofilm is the most critical component — it is alive, made up of beneficial microorganisms that actively consume and destroy pathogens
- The filter must remain wet (the sand must always be submerged) to keep the biofilm alive. If the sand dries out, the biofilm dies and must re-establish over another 2 to 3 weeks

**Materials Needed**
- A container: a 5-gallon bucket (minimum — treats enough for 1 to 2 people), a 55-gallon drum (treats enough for a family or small group), or a concrete/masonry box for a community-scale filter
- PVC pipe or tubing for the outlet (0.5 to 1 inch diameter)
- Gravel: coarse (0.25 to 0.5 inch diameter) and fine (0.1 to 0.25 inch diameter)
- Sand: coarse (0.5 to 1 mm grain size) and fine (0.15 to 0.35 mm grain size). The fine sand is the most critical — too fine and it clogs immediately, too coarse and pathogens pass through
- A diffuser plate (a flat plate with small holes, or a plastic container lid with holes punched in it) to distribute incoming water evenly across the sand surface without disturbing the biofilm

**Where to Get Sand and Gravel**
- River beds and stream banks are the best sources — the water has already sorted material by size
- Hardware stores and building supply stores have bags of sand and gravel
- Dig sand from areas well above the waterline where it is clean and dry
- All sand and gravel must be washed before use to remove fine silt and organic matter. To wash: place material in a bucket, fill with water, stir vigorously, pour off the cloudy water, repeat until the rinse water runs clear. This may take 5 to 10 rinses

**Sizing the Sand Grains**
If you do not have a standard soil sieve, you can sort sand by size:
- Window screen mesh passes sand smaller than approximately 1.5 mm
- Standard window screen will catch gravel and coarse sand while allowing fine sand through
- For the ideal 0.15 to 0.35 mm fine sand: pass material through window screen (to remove large particles) then through a fine cloth like a t-shirt (the finest particles that pass through the cloth are too fine — use the material caught by the cloth but passed by the screen)

**Construction — 5-Gallon Bucket Version**

This builds a filter adequate for 1 to 3 people (producing approximately 8 to 15 gallons of filtered water per day).

1. Take a food-grade 5-gallon bucket with a lid
2. Drill a hole near the bottom of the bucket (approximately 1 inch above the very bottom) sized to fit your PVC pipe snugly
3. Insert a 12 to 18 inch length of PVC pipe through the hole. Inside the bucket, the pipe should curve upward so the interior end of the pipe reaches a height approximately 2 inches above the top of the sand layer. This upward curve creates a standing water level above the sand, which keeps the sand submerged and the biofilm alive. The pipe works as a simple siphon / standing-water-level control
4. Seal around the pipe where it passes through the bucket wall with silicone, epoxy, plumber's putty, or melted wax

5. Add the filter layers from bottom to top:

| Layer | Material | Depth | Purpose |
|-------|----------|-------|---------|
| 1 (bottom) | Coarse gravel (0.25-0.5 inch) | 2 inches | Supports fine layers, surrounds outlet pipe |
| 2 | Fine gravel (0.1-0.25 inch) | 2 inches | Transition layer, prevents fine sand from entering gravel |
| 3 | Coarse sand (0.5-1 mm) | 2 inches | Transition layer |
| 4 (top) | Fine sand (0.15-0.35 mm) | 8 to 10 inches | Primary filtration and biofilm layer |
| Standing water | Water above sand surface | 2 inches | Keeps biofilm alive, controlled by outlet pipe height |
| Air gap | Empty space above water | 4 to 6 inches | Room to pour in water |

6. Place the diffuser plate on top of the sand. This can be:
   - A plastic plate or lid with 15 to 20 small holes (1/8 inch) drilled in it
   - A piece of cloth laid flat on the sand surface
   - A flat rock or piece of wood with holes
   - The purpose is to spread incoming water across the entire sand surface evenly, preventing it from digging a channel through the sand and bypassing the biofilm

7. Cut a hole in the bucket lid to pour water through, or remove the lid for each use

**Operation**
1. Pour pre-filtered (cloth-filtered) water into the top of the filter, onto the diffuser plate
2. Water percolates through the sand layers and exits through the outlet pipe
3. Flow rate should be approximately 0.1 to 0.4 gallons per minute (a slow, steady drip to a thin stream)
4. If the flow rate is too fast (water rushes through): your sand is too coarse — replace the fine sand with finer material
5. If the flow rate is too slow (water barely drips): your sand is too fine or the filter is clogged. Carefully remove the top 0.5 to 1 inch of sand, wash it, and replace it (this is called "swirl and dump" maintenance)
6. Collect the filtered water in a clean container

**Biological Ripening Period**
- When first built, the bio-sand filter functions only as a physical filter. Biological treatment begins after beneficial microorganisms colonize the sand surface
- This "ripening" period takes 2 to 3 weeks of regular use (pour water through the filter at least once daily)
- During the ripening period, pathogen removal is lower — supplement with bleach treatment (2 drops per gallon)
- After ripening, the filter reaches full effectiveness
- Do NOT let the filter dry out after ripening — if the standing water level drops below the sand surface, the biofilm dries and dies. If this happens, you must wait another 2 to 3 weeks for re-establishment
- If you must leave the filter unused for more than a day or two, top it off with water and cover it

**Maintenance**
- When flow rate drops significantly, perform "swirl and dump": remove the diffuser plate, gently swirl the top 1 inch of sand with your hand in the standing water, carefully scoop out the cloudy water (without removing sand), and replace the diffuser. This removes accumulated debris from the top layer without destroying the deeper biofilm
- Never remove more than the top 1 inch of sand
- If you must replace sand, replace only the top inch with pre-washed fine sand and allow 1 to 2 weeks for the biofilm to re-establish in the new sand
- Clean the collection container regularly

**Construction — 55-Gallon Drum Version**

For a family or group (producing 30 to 60 gallons per day):

Follow the same design principles as the bucket version, scaled up:
- Coarse gravel layer: 4 inches
- Fine gravel layer: 3 inches
- Coarse sand layer: 3 inches
- Fine sand layer: 18 to 24 inches (the deeper the fine sand layer, the more effective the filtration)
- Standing water: 2 to 4 inches above sand
- Use a 1-inch PVC outlet pipe with the interior end reaching the appropriate standing water height
- Flow rate: 0.25 to 1 gallon per minute
- A 55-gallon drum BSF can serve 5 to 10 people with daily refilling

### 4.3 Hand-Dug Well

A hand-dug well provides direct access to groundwater — the most reliable, cleanest natural water source available. Building one is physically demanding but requires no specialized equipment.

**Site Selection**

Choosing the right location is critical for both water quality and yield:

1. At least 100 feet (30 meters) from any source of contamination: latrines, animal pens, waste pits, septic systems, fuel storage, cemeteries
2. At least 50 feet from buildings (to avoid undermining foundations)
3. Uphill from contamination sources (groundwater flows downhill)
4. In an area where the water table is likely shallow (indicators: lush vegetation, nearby springs or seeps, existing wells in the area, low-lying terrain)
5. On stable, non-rocky ground — digging through solid rock by hand is impractical
6. Accessible for daily use — you will be visiting this well multiple times per day

**Tools Needed**
- Shovel (round-point for digging, flat for shaping)
- Pick or mattock (for hard soil, clay, or small rocks)
- Bucket and rope (for removing excavated material and later for drawing water)
- Ladder or footholds carved into the well wall (for getting in and out as the well deepens)
- Lumber, stones, or bricks for casing the well walls (preventing cave-ins)
- Optional: a tripod with a pulley above the well to lift buckets of soil and water

**Digging Technique**

1. Mark out a circle 4 to 5 feet in diameter on the ground. This is wide enough to work in and narrow enough to case with available materials

2. Begin digging within the circle, removing soil evenly across the diameter. Place excavated material at least 10 feet from the well edge to prevent it from falling back in and to avoid overloading the soil near the well

3. Keep the walls as vertical as possible. In sandy or loose soil, you will need to install casing (lining) as you dig to prevent collapse

4. Dig in uniform layers — remove 12 to 18 inches of depth around the entire circumference before digging deeper. This keeps the bottom level and the walls stable

5. As you get deeper, use the bucket-and-rope system to remove soil. One person digs at the bottom, fills the bucket, and a second person at the surface hauls it up

6. Never work alone in a well. Cave-ins are the primary danger. Always have at least one person on the surface who can assist in an emergency

7. Below about 5 feet of depth, the danger of oxygen-depleted air becomes real (this is more of a concern in wet, organic-rich soil or near swamps). If you feel dizzy, short of breath, or notice a foul odor, get out immediately. You can test air quality by lowering a candle on a string — if the flame goes out, the oxygen level is dangerously low and the well must be ventilated before re-entering

8. Continue digging until you reach the water table. You will know you have reached it when the bottom of the well begins to seep water. Dig 3 to 5 feet below the first appearance of water to create a sump that fills with water during periods of lower water table

**Well Casing (Preventing Cave-Ins and Contamination)**

An unlined well will cave in. The casing serves two purposes: structural support and contamination prevention.

Options:
- Stone or brick: dry-stack stones or bricks to form a cylinder lining the well wall. This is the most durable option. Leave gaps in the lower section (below the water table) to allow water to enter. Mortar the upper section (above the water table) to prevent surface contamination from seeping in
- Concrete rings: if available, pre-cast concrete culvert rings provide excellent casing. Lower them into place as you dig
- Wood: logs, split timbers, or boards can be used as temporary casing. Wood will eventually rot below the waterline but lasts for months to a few years
- Barrels: stacking barrels (with the bottoms cut out) inside the well creates an improvised casing. Cut slots or holes in the lower barrels for water entry

**Preventing Surface Contamination**

1. Build an apron around the well opening: a 3 to 5 foot diameter pad of concrete, compacted clay, or stacked flat stones sloped away from the well. This prevents puddles from forming around the well, which would allow contaminated surface water to drain in

2. Raise the well casing 12 to 18 inches above ground level. This prevents surface runoff and debris from entering during rain

3. Build a cover over the well opening: a wooden lid, a concrete slab, or a hinged wooden door. This keeps out animals, children, debris, and insects

4. If using a bucket and rope to draw water, designate one bucket that is ONLY used for the well — never set it on the ground or use it for other purposes

5. Install a hand pump (see Section 4.4) if possible — this eliminates the contamination risk of open bucket-and-rope systems

**Well Yield**
- A hand-dug well in a good location typically provides 50 to 200 gallons per day
- Yield depends on the permeability of the soil/rock at the water table level and the depth of the sump below the water table
- Sandy soil yields more water than clay
- If the well fills slowly after drawing water, it is "low yield" — draw water in smaller batches and let it recharge

### 4.4 Hand Pumps

A hand pump eliminates the contamination risks of open-bucket systems and makes drawing water easier, especially from deeper wells.

**Pitcher Pump (Shallow Well)**
- Suitable for wells where the water level is 25 feet or less below the pump
- This is the classic "old-fashioned" hand pump seen on historic farm properties
- Works by suction: the pumping action creates a partial vacuum that draws water up

Installation:
1. Mount the pump body on top of the well casing
2. Connect a drop pipe (1.25 to 2 inch diameter PVC or galvanized steel) from the pump down into the well water
3. The drop pipe must extend into the water at least 2 feet below the lowest expected water level
4. Install a foot valve (a one-way check valve) at the bottom of the drop pipe, submerged in the well water. This valve holds water in the pipe between pumping sessions, maintaining the prime
5. Prime the pump by pouring water into the top and pumping. Once the suction draws water up, the pump is primed and will continue to work as long as the foot valve holds

Maintenance:
- If the pump loses its prime (no water comes out despite pumping), check the foot valve — it may be clogged with debris or the seal may have failed
- Leather cup seals (the moving seal inside the pump cylinder) wear out over time. They can be replaced with cut leather, rubber, or even tightly wrapped cloth
- Lubricate moving parts with food-safe grease periodically

**Deep Well Hand Pump**
- For wells where the water level is deeper than 25 feet (suction pumps cannot lift water from deeper because atmospheric pressure limits suction to approximately 25 to 33 feet)
- A deep well pump works by positive displacement: a piston at the bottom of the well (submerged in water) pushes water up through the pipe, rather than sucking it from the top
- The pump rod runs from the handle at the top, down through the drop pipe, to the cylinder at the bottom
- These are more complex to build from scratch but can be scavenged from rural properties, farm supply stores, or deep-well pump suppliers

Installation:
1. The pump cylinder (containing the piston) must be installed below the water level in the well
2. The drop pipe connects the cylinder to the pump head at the surface
3. Pump rods (thin metal rods connected end to end) run inside the drop pipe from the handle to the piston
4. Operating the handle moves the pump rods up and down, which moves the piston in the cylinder, which pushes water up the drop pipe

5. Deep well pumps can lift water from 100+ feet with adequate effort

**Improvised Hand Pump (Rope Pump)**
The rope pump is the simplest deep-well pump design and can be built entirely from scavenged or local materials.

How it works: a continuous loop of rope with small disc-shaped pistons (washers) attached at regular intervals runs down a pipe submerged in the well, passes through a guide at the bottom, and comes back up inside a rising main (another pipe). As the rope is pulled upward through the rising main, the pistons push water ahead of them up the pipe.

Materials:
- Rope (any durable rope — nylon, polypropylene, natural fiber)
- Small discs (washers, rubber stoppers, or leather circles cut slightly smaller than the pipe inner diameter) attached to the rope every 12 to 18 inches
- PVC pipe (2 to 3 inch diameter) for the rising main
- A wheel or pulley at the top (a bicycle wheel works well)
- A guide (a curved PVC elbow or smooth wooden guide) at the bottom where the rope turns

Construction:
1. Install a PVC pipe vertically in the well, with the bottom end submerged in the water and the top end above the well opening
2. Mount a bicycle wheel (or any smooth-turning wheel) at the top, directly above the pipe
3. Thread the rope into a continuous loop. On the side of the loop that runs upward through the PVC pipe, attach rubber disc pistons every 12 to 18 inches
4. The rope descends on the outside of the pipe, passes around a guide at the bottom (below water level), and enters the pipe. It travels up through the pipe (with the pistons pushing water) and exits at the top, where it goes over the wheel and back down the outside
5. Turn the wheel by hand. Water emerges from the top of the pipe as the pistons push it up

Output: approximately 0.5 to 2 gallons per minute depending on well depth and turning speed

### 4.5 Solar Still (Ground Level) — Detailed Build

(Expanded from Section 3.6.1 with additional detail.)

The ground-level solar still is a survival-level distillation system that requires minimal materials. It is best understood as an emergency water source or a supplement to other systems — it will not provide enough water to sustain a person alone.

**Optimal Conditions**
- Full sun (6+ hours of direct sunlight per day)
- Moist soil or added moisture sources
- Hot climate (higher temperatures = more evaporation = more output)
- Low wind (wind cools the plastic, reducing condensation efficiency)

**Materials**
- Clear plastic sheet, at minimum 4 x 4 feet (6 x 6 is better). Thinner plastic transmits more sunlight. Wrinkled or scratched plastic reduces efficiency — use the smoothest, clearest plastic you have
- A collection container: any cup, bowl, or jar. Wider opening catches more drips. A dark-colored container absorbs heat and may slightly increase evaporation around it
- A digging tool
- A small stone or weight
- Optional: 6 feet of flexible tubing for a drinking straw (avoids opening the still to retrieve water, which releases moisture and resets the condensation cycle)
- Optional: green vegetation, contaminated water, or saltwater to add moisture

**Construction Steps**

1. Select a site with maximum sun exposure. South-facing slopes (in the northern hemisphere) receive the most sun. Avoid shade from trees or structures.

2. Dig a bowl-shaped hole:
   - Diameter: 3 to 4 feet across (larger = more collection surface area = more water)
   - Depth: 18 to 24 inches
   - Shape: bowl-like with sloped sides, not a flat-bottomed trench
   - Dig into the darkest, most moist soil available

3. If the soil is dry, add moisture sources to the bottom and sides of the hole:
   - Pour in contaminated water, saltwater, or urine (the still distills it)
   - Cut fresh green vegetation (non-toxic — avoid poisonous plants) and spread it inside
   - The more moisture you add, the more the still produces

4. Place the collection container in the exact center of the bottom of the hole. Ensure it is stable and will not tip over.

5. If using a drinking tube, place one end in the collection container and run the other end up and out of the hole, along the ground surface. Ensure the tube is long enough to reach comfortably when lying next to the still.

6. Cover the hole with the clear plastic sheet. Pull it taut across the opening.

7. Seal the edges completely with soil, sand, or rocks all the way around. The seal must be airtight — any gap lets moist air escape, dramatically reducing output.

8. Place a small stone (egg-sized, approximately 2 to 3 ounces) on the center of the plastic, directly above the collection container. The plastic should sag to form a cone shape with the lowest point 1 to 3 inches above the container opening.

9. The stone must be centered precisely. If it is off-center, condensation will drip into the soil instead of the container.

**How It Works (Detailed)**
- Sunlight passes through the clear plastic and heats the dark soil and any moisture inside
- Water evaporates from the soil, vegetation, and any added liquid. The air inside the sealed hole becomes saturated with water vapor
- The plastic sheet is cooler than the air inside (it radiates heat to the sky). Water vapor condenses on the underside of the plastic as tiny droplets
- These droplets coalesce and run downhill along the plastic surface, following the cone shape to the lowest point (where the stone is)
- At the lowest point, droplets collect until they are heavy enough to drip, falling into the collection container below
- The process continues as long as there is moisture to evaporate and sun to provide energy

**Expected Yield**
- Arid soil, no added moisture: 0.25 to 0.5 quarts per day (barely worth the effort)
- Moist soil: 0.5 to 1 quart per day
- Added vegetation and/or contaminated water: 1 to 2 quarts per day
- Multiple stills are recommended — build 4 to 6 for meaningful output

### 4.6 Gravity-Fed Distribution System

If your water source (spring, rainwater tank, or well with an elevated holding tank) is at a higher elevation than your dwelling, you can create a gravity-fed distribution system that delivers water without pumping.

**The Basic Principle**
Water flows downhill. If the source is higher than the destination, water will flow through a pipe from source to destination powered by gravity alone. The pressure at the destination depends on the vertical drop (called "head"): every 2.31 feet of vertical drop provides 1 PSI (pound per square inch) of pressure.

**Planning**
1. Measure the vertical elevation difference between your water source and your dwelling. You need at least 10 to 15 feet of vertical drop for useful pressure (4 to 7 PSI)
2. Measure the horizontal distance the pipe must travel. Longer runs experience more friction loss in the pipe, reducing pressure
3. Choose your pipe: 0.75 to 1 inch diameter PVC or polyethylene (garden hose) for a single household. Larger diameter for community systems. Larger pipe = less friction loss but more material

**Installation**
1. Connect the pipe to the source (a tank outlet, spring box, or elevated reservoir). Install a shut-off valve at the source so you can stop flow for maintenance
2. Run the pipe along the ground surface or bury it 12 to 18 inches deep (burying protects from freezing, UV degradation, and physical damage)
3. The pipe should slope continuously downhill from source to destination. Avoid upward sections if possible — air can collect at high points and create airlocks that block flow. If you must go over a hill, install an air-release valve at the high point (a simple T-fitting with a capped vertical pipe that you can open periodically to release trapped air)
4. At the destination, install a spigot (tap) or connect to a simple distribution point. You can add a float valve (like in a toilet tank) to automatically shut off flow when a local storage container is full

**Pressure Estimates**
| Vertical Drop (feet) | Approximate PSI | Flow Performance |
|----------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 5 | 2.2 | Weak trickle from a tap |
| 10 | 4.3 | Slow but usable flow |
| 20 | 8.7 | Moderate flow, usable for basic needs |
| 50 | 21.6 | Strong flow, comparable to low municipal pressure |
| 100 | 43.3 | High pressure, suitable for irrigation |

**Important Notes**
- Screen the inlet at the source to prevent debris from entering the pipe
- Install a shut-off valve at both ends (source and destination)
- Drain the system before hard freezing weather to prevent pipe bursts — water expands when it freezes and will crack PVC pipe
- Inspect the pipe periodically for leaks, especially at joints
- If using garden hose, connect sections with standard hose fittings. If using PVC, use primer and cement on all joints for a watertight seal

## Section 5: Water Storage

Finding and purifying water means nothing if you cannot store it safely for later use. Proper storage protects your treated water from recontamination, provides a buffer during dry spells or emergencies, and allows you to build up reserves during periods of abundance (rainy season, spring snowmelt) for use during scarcity.

### 5.1 Container Types

Not all containers are suitable for water storage. The container material must be food-safe (no toxic chemicals leaching into the water), durable, and sealable.

**Food-Grade Plastic — HDPE #2 (Best General-Purpose Option)**
- High-density polyethylene (HDPE), marked with the recycling symbol #2 on the bottom
- This is the material used for commercial water jugs, milk jugs, laundry detergent bottles (if thoroughly cleaned), and purpose-built water storage drums
- HDPE does not leach chemicals into water under normal conditions
- Opaque (usually blue or white) HDPE blocks light, preventing algae growth
- Available in sizes from 1 gallon jugs to 55-gallon drums to 275-gallon IBC (Intermediate Bulk Container) totes
- Lightweight relative to capacity

What to avoid:
- Do NOT use containers that previously held non-food substances (chemicals, motor oil, paint, pesticides). Even thorough washing cannot guarantee removal of all residues
- Do NOT use PVC plastic (#3) — it can leach vinyl chloride and phthalates
- Do NOT use polycarbonate (#7) — older versions contain BPA (bisphenol A)
- Do NOT use any container that has a chemical odor even after washing

How to identify food-grade containers: look for the HDPE #2 recycling mark, the "NSF" certification mark, or the fork-and-glass food-safe symbol. When in doubt, use containers that originally held food or beverages.

**Glass**
- Completely inert — leaches nothing into water
- Easy to sterilize (boil or wash with bleach solution)
- Transparent, so you can assess water clarity
- Drawbacks: heavy, fragile, breaks when frozen (water expands ~9% when freezing, which will shatter glass if full), usually only available in small sizes (quarts to gallon jugs)
- Best use: short-term storage, medical water, and situations where chemical purity is critical

**Stainless Steel**
- Inert and durable
- Does not leach chemicals
- Available as canteens, thermoses, and larger commercial vessels
- Drawbacks: heavy, expensive, opaque (cannot see water level or clarity), can develop rust if the stainless coating is damaged
- Best use: personal daily-carry water containers, cooking pots that double as water storage

**Concrete and Masonry Cisterns**
- For large-scale storage (hundreds to thousands of gallons)
- Concrete cisterns are extremely durable, long-lasting, and can be built from local materials (cement, sand, gravel, rebar)
- Must be sealed on the interior to prevent water from leaching through and to prevent minerals from leaching into the water. Apply a food-safe concrete sealer or plaster the interior with multiple coats of cement morite (a smooth cement finish)
- New concrete raises the pH of stored water initially. Flush the cistern with water several times before using it for drinking water, or let it cure for 30 days before first use
- Excellent for underground installation (stable temperature, protected from sunlight)
- Drawbacks: permanent installation, labor-intensive to build, cannot be moved

**Improvised Containers**
In a survival scenario, you use what you have:
- Clean bathtubs: 40 to 80 gallons. Plug the drain, fill before an anticipated water outage, cover with plastic sheeting
- Trash cans (new, never used for trash): 30 to 45 gallons. Line with a heavy-duty trash bag if the can is not food-grade
- Waterbed mattresses: 200+ gallons. Note: waterbed water is often treated with anti-algae chemicals. Use only as non-drinking water (hygiene, irrigation) or distill before drinking
- Lined holes in the ground: dig a pit, line it with heavy plastic sheeting (pond liner), fill with treated water, cover with a tarp to prevent contamination and evaporation. This works for large-volume non-drinking storage

### 5.2 How Much to Store

**Minimum Standard**: 1 gallon per person per day for 14 days (2 weeks)
- For a single person: 14 gallons minimum
- For a family of 4: 56 gallons minimum

**Recommended Standard**: 2 gallons per person per day for 30 days (1 month)
- For a single person: 60 gallons
- For a family of 4: 240 gallons (approximately four 55-gallon drums, or one IBC tote)

**Long-Term Planning**: 2 gallons per person per day for 90 days (3 months)
- For a single person: 180 gallons
- For a family of 4: 720 gallons
- At this scale, you need a combination of stored water AND active collection systems (rainwater catchment, well, etc.)

**Weight Considerations**
- Water weighs 8.34 pounds per gallon
- A full 55-gallon drum weighs approximately 459 pounds — this cannot be moved without wheels, a dolly, or multiple people
- A full IBC tote (275 gallons) weighs approximately 2,294 pounds — it goes where you put it and stays there
- Plan your storage location before filling containers. Fill in place

### 5.3 Shelf Life and Rotation

**Treated Municipal Water**
- Commercially bottled water has an indefinite shelf life if stored properly (cool, dark, sealed). The expiration date on bottled water is for the plastic container, not the water itself — over many years, chemicals from the plastic may leach at very low levels
- Tap water treated with bleach (2 drops per gallon of 8.25% sodium hypochlorite) and stored in food-grade containers in a cool, dark location: safe for 6 to 12 months
- After 6 to 12 months, re-treat with bleach and the water is good for another 6 to 12 months

**Untreated Water**
- Untreated water stored in sealed containers can grow bacteria and algae within days to weeks
- Always treat water before storing it for any significant period

**Rotation Schedule**
For stored water you are maintaining as an emergency reserve:
1. Every 6 months: open a sample container, inspect the water visually (should be clear, no sediment, no slime), smell it (should have no odor or a faint chlorine smell), and optionally taste it
2. If the water looks, smells, and tastes fine, add 2 drops of bleach per gallon to refresh the chlorine, reseal, and return to storage
3. If the water is discolored, slimy, or smells off: dump it, clean the container thoroughly (scrub with a bleach solution of 1 tablespoon bleach per gallon of water, rinse, air dry), refill with fresh water, treat, and reseal

### 5.4 Preventing Contamination in Stored Water

Even properly treated water can become contaminated during storage. These practices prevent recontamination:

1. **Always seal containers**: use tight-fitting lids, screw caps, or bungs. If using a barrel with a removable lid, weigh down the lid and wrap the seal with tape or cloth to prevent dust, insects, and debris from entering

2. **Keep containers opaque or in the dark**: light promotes algae growth. Blue or dark-colored containers are better than clear. If using clear containers (glass, clear plastic), store them in a dark space or cover with an opaque cloth

3. **Keep containers off the ground**: elevate on pallets, boards, or shelving. This prevents moisture from accumulating underneath (which promotes mold), protects from ground-level flooding, and prevents chemicals from the concrete floor from leaching through the plastic (a real concern with some thin plastics on concrete)

4. **Never use a dirty cup or hand to scoop water from a container**: use a clean ladle or pour from the tap/spigot. Every time you reach into a container, you introduce bacteria from your skin

5. **Designate containers for clean water only**: do not use a clean water container to carry dirty water, even temporarily. Once contaminated, a container requires thorough cleaning before reuse

6. **Keep containers away from chemicals**: do not store water next to gasoline, pesticides, paint, or solvents. Fumes can permeate some plastics

7. **Maintain residual chlorine**: water stored with a small amount of bleach (2 drops per gallon) maintains a residual chlorine level that continues to kill any bacteria that enter. If you are storing for long periods, this residual chlorine provides ongoing protection. Re-treat every 6 months to maintain it

### 5.5 Large-Scale Storage

**IBC Totes (Intermediate Bulk Containers)**
- Capacity: 275 gallons (standard) or 330 gallons
- Construction: HDPE plastic tank in a galvanized steel cage on a pallet base
- Available at: farm supply stores, industrial supply companies, chemical distributors (buy food-grade, not previously used for chemicals), and online
- Pros: large capacity, stackable (when full, the cage supports the weight of another tote), pallet base allows transport with a pallet jack or forklift, built-in valve at the bottom
- Cons: very heavy when full, not easily moved, the cage can rust, some totes previously held non-food chemicals (verify history), vulnerable to UV degradation (the plastic becomes brittle in sustained sunlight — keep in shade or wrap with a tarp)
- Preparation: flush a new or used food-grade tote with a bleach solution (1 tablespoon bleach per gallon of water), drain, and rinse before filling with your water supply

**Underground Cisterns**
- Advantages: constant cool temperature (underground stays around 50 to 60 degrees F year-round in temperate climates), protected from freezing, out of sight, large capacity
- Construction: dig a pit, line with concrete (poured or block), waterproof the interior, install an access hatch and an overflow pipe
- Size: a 6 foot x 6 foot x 6 foot underground cistern holds approximately 1,600 gallons
- Install a pipe from your rainwater catchment system to feed the cistern
- Install a hand pump or a pipe with a valve to draw water out
- Ensure the access hatch is sealed to prevent contamination and is secure enough to prevent children from falling in

**Pond Lining**
- For non-drinking water storage (irrigation, livestock, firefighting, hygiene), a lined pond provides enormous capacity
- Dig a depression or use natural low terrain
- Line with heavy-duty pond liner (EPDM rubber, PVC pond liner, or heavy polyethylene sheeting)
- Capacity depends entirely on size — a 10 x 10 x 3 foot lined pond holds approximately 2,244 gallons
- This water must be purified before drinking
- Cover or screen against mosquito breeding if possible

### 5.6 Winter Considerations

Water's behavior in freezing temperatures creates unique storage challenges.

**Preventing Freezing**
- Water freezes at 32 degrees F (0 degrees C). When it freezes, it expands approximately 9%. This expansion generates enormous force — enough to burst metal pipes, crack plastic containers, and shatter glass
- Never fill a container 100% if it may freeze. Leave at least 10% empty space (headroom) for expansion
- Insulate containers: wrap with blankets, straw bales, foam insulation, or bury underground (below the frost line, which varies by region — 12 inches in the southern US, up to 60+ inches in northern states and Canada)
- Store containers inside a structure if possible — even an unheated garage or barn stays warmer than outdoors
- A large volume of water resists freezing longer than a small volume. A 55-gallon drum in a cold garage may take days to freeze solid, while a 1-gallon jug freezes overnight
- Moving water resists freezing better than still water. In a gravity-fed system, maintaining a slow trickle through pipes during cold snaps prevents freezing (this wastes water, so use it only when necessary and recapture the trickle)

**Insulation Methods**
- Straw bales stacked around containers provide excellent insulation (R-value approximately 1.5 per inch of straw)
- Rigid foam insulation board (polystyrene or polyisocyanurate) wrapped around containers
- Earth berming: pile soil around and over containers. Below 12 to 18 inches of soil, the temperature is moderated by the ground's thermal mass
- Snow itself is an insulator (R-value approximately 1 per inch). A container buried under 2 to 3 feet of snow is protected from extreme surface temperatures
- Build an insulated housing: a small, enclosed shed or box around your water storage, with thick walls of straw, foam, or double-wall construction with dead air space

**If Water Freezes**
- Frozen water in a container is not ruined — simply melt it and use it
- Do not try to speed melting by applying a flame to a plastic container — the plastic will melt before the ice does
- Move the container into a warm space, or place it in sunlight, or chip ice out and melt in a pot
- Check containers for cracks after freezing — a container that expanded may have hairline cracks that leak when the ice melts

## Section 6: Water in Different Environments

Your water strategy depends heavily on your environment. The approaches that work in a temperate forest are useless in a desert, and the techniques that sustain you on a coast fail in the mountains. This section provides environment-specific guidance.

### 6.1 Arid and Desert Environments

Water is the defining constraint of desert survival. Every decision — when to move, how far to travel, where to shelter — revolves around water.

**General Principles**
- Minimize water loss before attempting to increase water intake (rest in shade, minimize exertion, travel at night, breathe through your nose, wear loose covering clothing)
- You can lose 1 to 2 liters of water per hour through sweat during exertion in desert heat without realizing it (sweat evaporates immediately in dry air)
- In hot desert conditions, you may need 2 to 3 gallons per day — far more than the standard 1 gallon baseline

**Finding Water**

1. **Dry riverbeds (arroyos, wadis)**: these are your best bet. Water often exists just below the surface. Dig in the outside bend of the channel (where the deeper water carved) — start 2 to 4 feet deep. Wait 30 minutes to several hours for water to seep into the hole. The water will be muddy — pre-filter through cloth

2. **Solar stills**: build multiple ground-level solar stills in areas of moist soil (near dry riverbeds, in areas with some vegetation). Add any available moisture sources: cut cactus pads (carefully), urine, crushed vegetation. Expect 0.5 to 2 quarts per still per day

3. **Dew collection**: desert dew forms in surprising quantities during clear nights, especially on metal surfaces. Spread tarps, metal sheets, or vehicle hoods in the evening. Collect condensation before sunrise with absorbent cloth

4. **Rock basins and tinajas**: natural rock depressions often collect and hold rainwater for weeks or months after rain events. Look in slot canyons, at the base of cliff faces, and in rocky terrain. These pools are often hidden from view — look for bird activity, insect swarms, and green moss/algae on rock faces (indicating persistent moisture)

5. **Desert plants**: date palms indicate groundwater. Mesquite trees can have roots reaching 100+ feet to the water table — where mesquite grows, water is deep but present. Cattails, willows, and cottonwoods indicate shallow water. Digging near the base of these trees (within the root zone, 10 to 20 feet from the trunk) may reach water

6. **Animal behavior**: follow converging animal tracks at dawn and dusk. In the desert, every animal visits water regularly. Doves and pigeons fly directly to water in the morning

**Water Conservation Rules in Desert**
- Do NOT ration below 1 quart per day — your body cannot function below this
- Eat minimally if water is scarce — digesting food requires water
- Do NOT sit on hot ground (heat radiates up and increases sweat) — sit on an insulating layer (pack, cloth, vegetation)
- Do NOT remove clothing in an attempt to cool down — clothing reduces direct sun on skin, which reduces the sweat rate needed for cooling
- Keep your mouth closed; breathe through your nose
- Place a pebble in your mouth to stimulate saliva and reduce the sensation of thirst (this does not replace water — it is a psychological aid)

### 6.2 Coastal Environments

Living near the ocean provides unlimited water that you cannot drink. The core challenge is converting saltwater to freshwater.

**Desalination Methods**

1. **Solar still (elevated/coastal version)**: construct a still using saltwater as the input. Instead of relying on ground moisture, pour saltwater into a dark, shallow pan or tray inside the sealed still. Sun heats the saltwater, water evaporates (leaving salt behind), condenses on the plastic/glass cover, and drips into a collection container as fresh water. This is essentially the same as a ground-level solar still but with a dedicated saltwater input.

   Output: similar to the ground still (0.5 to 2 quarts per day per still), but potentially more if you use a large dark surface to absorb heat (a black tray or dark rocks in the still).

2. **Boiling distillation**: the most efficient method for larger quantities. Set up the stovetop distillation system described in Section 3.6.2 with saltwater in the boiling pot. The steam condenses as pure freshwater. A vigorous setup can produce 0.5 to 1 gallon per hour.

3. **Beach well**: dig a hole in the sand above the high-tide line, at least 50 to 100 feet from the water's edge. In some coastal areas, freshwater "lenses" — layers of freshwater floating on top of denser saltwater — exist in the sand. The water from a beach well may be fresh, brackish, or salty. Taste it before committing to treatment. If it is brackish (slightly salty), distillation can make it drinkable. If it is fresh, standard purification (boiling or chemical) is sufficient.

   Important: digging too deep can tap into the saltwater layer below the freshwater lens, contaminating your well.

4. **Rainwater catchment**: on a coast, this is your primary freshwater strategy. Storms and rainfall are often more frequent in coastal areas. Build a catchment system (Section 4.1) as a top priority.

**Avoiding Saltwater Contamination of Freshwater**
- Store freshwater and saltwater collection/processing equipment separately — never cross-contaminate containers
- When distilling saltwater, clean the boiling pot regularly — salt buildup (scale) reduces efficiency and can contaminate the steam if liquid saltwater splashes into the condensation tube
- Taste-test all well water and collected water before treating it as freshwater

### 6.3 Cold and Winter Environments

Cold environments present a paradox: water may be everywhere (as snow and ice), but accessing it in liquid form requires energy (fuel for melting). Dehydration is a major and underestimated winter risk.

**Melting Snow and Ice**

1. Never eat snow directly. It takes more energy (body heat) to melt snow in your stomach than the water provides, accelerating hypothermia. Even in non-emergency situations, eating snow cools your core temperature and forces your body to burn additional calories to rewarm.

2. Ice is far more efficient than snow: 1 cubic foot of ice yields approximately 7.5 gallons of water, while 1 cubic foot of loose snow yields only 0.75 to 1.5 gallons (snow is 85-95% air)

3. Melting procedure:
   - Use a dark-colored container in direct sunlight — even on cold days, solar heating can melt snow/ice in a dark pot
   - Over a fire or stove: put a small amount of liquid water in the bottom of the pot first (if you have any), then add snow gradually. Heating a pot full of dry snow without liquid at the bottom scorches the pot and wastes heat because snow is such a poor heat conductor
   - Pack snow tightly before melting — this reduces the air content and speeds melting
   - Add snow in stages: melt a small batch, add more snow to the resulting water, melt, repeat. This is much more fuel-efficient than trying to melt a full pot of snow at once

4. Energy cost: melting 1 gallon of snow (compacted) requires approximately 10,000 to 15,000 BTUs of heat — roughly equivalent to burning 1 pound of dry wood. Plan your fuel needs accordingly. In a fuel-scarce winter survival situation, this is a significant constraint.

**Ice as a Water Source**
- Clear lake or river ice: usually safe if the water body was clean. Melt and treat (boil) as a precaution
- Old sea ice (blue/clear, multi-year): has had most salt leach out. Taste a small chip — if not salty, melt and use. Fresh sea ice (gray/white, first-year) is still salty and must be melted and distilled
- Icicles from eaves and rock faces: essentially frozen rainwater or snowmelt. Clean and safe after melting
- Ice from frozen puddles or ditches: treat like surface water — may contain contaminants

**Ice Fishing Holes as Water Sources**
- An open hole in lake ice provides direct access to liquid water
- The water below thick ice has been sealed from surface contamination and is often very clean
- Treat it as surface water (boil or chemically treat) as a precaution, especially near shore or in areas with inflow from agricultural land
- Keep the hole from refreezing by covering it with an insulated lid and clearing new ice regularly

**Preventing Dehydration in Cold**
- Cold air is extremely dry — you lose significant moisture with every breath (the visible cloud you exhale is your body's water)
- Thirst sensation is suppressed in cold — you can become severely dehydrated without feeling thirsty
- Alcohol constricts blood vessels and impairs thermoregulation — avoid it
- Caffeine is a mild diuretic but the fluid in tea or coffee typically outweighs the diuretic effect. Warm drinks are fine
- Make a conscious effort to drink regularly, even when you do not feel thirsty. Set a schedule: drink at every rest break, every 1 to 2 hours minimum

### 6.4 Urban Environments

A post-collapse urban environment contains enormous water reserves trapped in infrastructure, but accessing them requires systematic knowledge.

**Systematically Draining a Building's Plumbing**

When municipal water pressure fails, the water remaining in a building's pipes, fixtures, and appliances does not disappear — it sits there until someone removes it.

Complete procedure for a multi-story building:

1. Start at the top floor. Open all faucets (hot and cold) and all shower valves. This introduces air into the system from the highest point.

2. If the building has a roof-mounted water tank (common in older urban buildings), check it first — it may hold 5,000 to 10,000 gallons. Access the tank through the roof hatch. These tanks are typically gravity-fed, so opening a valve at the bottom drains the tank.

3. On each floor, working top to bottom:
   - Open all faucets (kitchen, bathroom, laundry)
   - Flush all toilets to empty the tanks (collect the tank water, not the bowl water)
   - Check under sinks for shut-off valves. The pipe between the wall valve and the faucet holds water — open the faucet with the wall valve closed, then open the wall valve to release the water in that section
   - Look for water filtration systems (refrigerator filters, under-sink filters) — these hold 0.25 to 1 gallon each

4. Go to the basement or lowest level. Open the lowest faucets and drain valves. All the water from the upper floors that gravity can reach will flow here.

5. Locate the water heater (typically in the basement or a utility closet). Follow the procedure in Section 2.5 to drain it — 40 to 80 gallons.

6. Locate the boiler or hydronic heating system if present. Building boilers hold 50 to 200+ gallons. Drain through the boiler drain valve. This water may contain rust and heating system additives — filter through charcoal and boil before drinking.

7. Check the building's fire suppression system (sprinkler system). The pipes are full of water under pressure. Locating the main drain valve for the sprinkler system can release hundreds of gallons. This water is treated but may be stagnant and contain rust — treat aggressively.

**Pool Water Treatment for Drinking**

Pool water contains chlorine (which is good — it disinfects), but also cyanuric acid (stabilizer), algaecides (often copper-based), and potentially other chemicals.

Treatment procedure:
1. Scoop water from below the surface to avoid floating debris
2. Filter through cloth to remove visible particles
3. Let the water sit in an open container for 24 hours in a well-ventilated area. This allows excess chlorine and volatile chemicals to off-gas
4. Filter through a charcoal filter to remove remaining chemicals (cyanuric acid, algaecide residues)
5. If you have no charcoal filter, the water is still usable for short-term consumption. The chemical levels in a properly maintained pool are low enough that weeks of consumption are unlikely to cause acute harm
6. For long-term use, distillation is the safest option (removes all pool chemicals)

**Other Urban Water Sources**
- Fire hydrants: contain water but require a hydrant wrench to open. The water is treated municipal water and is safe (if the system was recently pressurized). Check hardware stores or fire stations for hydrant wrenches
- Vending machines: soft drink and water vending machines may contain dozens of sealed bottles
- Office water coolers: the bottles contain 3 to 5 gallons each
- Gyms, restaurants, and hotels: commercial water heaters (80 to 120+ gallons), ice machines (water reservoir), soda fountain systems (water reservoir and CO2 tanks)

### 6.5 Forest and Jungle Environments

Forested environments generally offer the most abundant water, but accessing it may require specific techniques.

**Vine Water**
- Many tropical and some temperate vines contain drinkable water
- Selection: choose vines that are green (alive), at least 1 inch in diameter, and not milky when cut. Milky sap may be toxic latex
- Technique: use a machete or knife to make a deep notch HIGH on the vine first. Then sever the vine completely at ground level. Water will drain from the upper cut — gravity pulls the water column down. If you cut the bottom first, surface tension and capillary action hold the water in the vine and it will not flow
- Hold the cut end over your mouth or a container. Drips can yield 0.5 to 2 cups per vine section
- Safe vine water is clear, odorless, and tasteless or slightly sweet. Discard any that is milky, colored, bitter, or stinging

**Bamboo Water**
- Mature green bamboo stores water in the sealed chambers (internodes) between each joint (node)
- Test method: shake the bamboo stalk — if you hear sloshing, there is water inside
- Access: cut a notch (V-shape) into the bamboo just above a node (joint). Tilt the section to pour water out
- Alternatively, cut the bamboo at a node, creating an open cup. Tilt to pour from adjacent sealed sections
- Bamboo water from sealed sections is generally clean and safe without treatment. Once a section is opened, treat it like surface water if not consumed immediately
- Young, green bamboo is most likely to contain water. Old, dry, or brown bamboo is empty

**Tree Transpiration Bags**
- Detailed instructions in Section 2.4
- In a forest, this is highly practical because trees are abundant
- Best species: broad-leafed deciduous trees with large leaf surfaces (maple, birch, oak, willow)
- Avoid: conifers (low water output), and any toxic species (oleander, poison ivy/oak, yew, rhododendron, mountain laurel)
- Place bags in the morning, collect in the evening. Replace bags every 2 to 3 days
- A single large bag on a healthy tree in full sun may produce 0.5 to 1.5 quarts per day
- Use 5 to 10 bags simultaneously for meaningful water production

**Collecting Rainwater in Forest**
- Use the natural funneling of large leaves. Banana leaves, elephant ear leaves, and other broad tropical leaves can channel rain into a container
- Tie a tarp between trees at an angle to create a catchment surface
- Look for natural rain catchers: hollows in tree trunks, rock depressions, the upturned bases of fallen trees
- After rain, check crotches of trees (where branches meet the trunk) — water collects there

**Streams and Rivers**
- Forest streams are among the cleanest surface water sources, especially at higher elevations above human activity
- Collect from the fastest-flowing, clearest section
- Even in pristine-looking forests, always purify. Animal feces in the watershed introduce Giardia, Cryptosporidium, and bacteria
- In dense jungle, look for streams by following the sound of running water, walking downhill, and watching for convergent animal trails

## Section 7: Water-Related Health

Waterborne illness is one of the leading killers in survival and disaster scenarios worldwide. Understanding the common threats, their symptoms, and basic treatment can save lives — both your own and those of others in your group.

### 7.1 Common Waterborne Diseases

#### Giardia (Giardiasis)

**What it is**: A protozoan parasite (Giardia lamblia) that lives in the intestines of infected humans and animals. Its cysts (dormant, protective form) are shed in feces and contaminate water sources.

**How you get it**: Drinking water contaminated with Giardia cysts. Extremely common in wilderness water sources — even clear, cold mountain streams can harbor it from animal feces upstream.

**Symptoms** (appear 1 to 3 weeks after exposure):
- Watery, foul-smelling diarrhea (often greasy or floating)
- Abdominal cramps and bloating
- Nausea
- Gas (flatulence) — often severe
- Fatigue and malaise
- Weight loss (with prolonged infection)
- Symptoms can last 2 to 6 weeks and may recur

**Treatment without medicine**:
- Stay hydrated — oral rehydration solution (see 7.2) is critical
- The body can clear Giardia without medication in many cases, but it takes weeks
- Rest and maintain nutrition
- If available: metronidazole (Flagyl) is the standard pharmaceutical treatment — 250 mg three times daily for 5 to 7 days

**Prevention**: filter water through a 1-micron or finer filter, boil, or treat with iodine (chlorine requires extended contact time and is not fully reliable against Giardia at standard doses).

#### Cryptosporidium (Cryptosporidiosis)

**What it is**: A protozoan parasite (Cryptosporidium parvum) similar to Giardia but more resistant to chemical treatment. Common in water contaminated by livestock (cattle are a major reservoir).

**How you get it**: Drinking water contaminated with Cryptosporidium oocysts.

**Symptoms** (appear 2 to 10 days after exposure):
- Profuse, watery diarrhea
- Stomach cramps
- Nausea and vomiting
- Low-grade fever
- Dehydration (can be severe and rapid)
- Symptoms typically last 1 to 3 weeks

**Treatment without medicine**:
- Hydration is the primary treatment — oral rehydration solution is essential
- There is no reliably effective antibiotic for Cryptosporidium. The immune system must fight it off
- In healthy adults, it is usually self-limiting (resolves on its own in 1 to 3 weeks)
- In children, elderly, and immunocompromised individuals, it can be fatal due to dehydration

**Prevention**: boil water (most effective), filter through 1-micron or finer filter, or use UV treatment. Chlorine and iodine at standard field doses are NOT reliable against Cryptosporidium.

#### Cholera

**What it is**: A bacterial infection (Vibrio cholerae) that causes severe, life-threatening diarrhea. Cholera is historically one of the deadliest waterborne diseases and thrives in areas where sanitation infrastructure has collapsed.

**How you get it**: Drinking water or eating food contaminated with cholera bacteria, typically from human fecal matter entering the water supply.

**Symptoms** (appear hours to 5 days after exposure):
- Sudden onset of profuse, watery diarrhea (often described as "rice water stools" — pale, cloudy liquid)
- Vomiting
- Rapid dehydration — a person can lose 10 to 20 liters of fluid per day through diarrhea
- Leg cramps (from electrolyte loss)
- Rapid heart rate
- Low blood pressure
- Sunken eyes, dry skin
- In severe cases: shock and death within hours if untreated

**Treatment without medicine**:
- Oral rehydration solution is the primary, life-saving treatment. Cholera kills through dehydration, and replacing fluids can reduce the fatality rate from 50% to less than 1%
- Administer ORS continuously — as fast as the person is losing fluid
- If the person cannot keep fluids down (vomiting), give smaller sips more frequently (1 tablespoon every 1 to 2 minutes)
- If available: doxycycline (300 mg single dose), azithromycin (1 gram single dose), or ciprofloxacin (1 gram single dose) can shorten the duration and reduce fluid loss

**Prevention**: boiling, chlorine treatment, or iodine all effectively kill cholera bacteria. Proper sanitation (latrines at least 100 feet from water sources) is the most important preventive measure.

#### E. coli (Escherichia coli) Infections

**What it is**: A group of bacteria, some strains of which (particularly E. coli O157:H7) cause serious illness. Found in fecal-contaminated water and food.

**Symptoms** (appear 1 to 10 days after exposure):
- Severe abdominal cramps
- Watery diarrhea that may become bloody
- Nausea and vomiting
- Low-grade fever
- In severe cases (especially in children): hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS), which can cause kidney failure

**Treatment**:
- Hydration (oral rehydration solution)
- Do NOT use anti-diarrheal medications (like Imodium) for suspected E. coli — they can worsen the condition by preventing the body from expelling the bacteria
- Most cases resolve in 5 to 10 days without antibiotics
- Antibiotics are generally NOT recommended for E. coli O157:H7 — they may increase the risk of HUS

**Prevention**: any standard water treatment method (boiling, chlorine, iodine, filtration) effectively eliminates E. coli.

#### Hepatitis A

**What it is**: A viral infection of the liver spread through fecal contamination of water and food. Unlike bacterial infections, hepatitis A can cause prolonged illness lasting weeks to months.

**Symptoms** (appear 2 to 6 weeks after exposure):
- Fatigue (often profound — lasting weeks)
- Nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite
- Abdominal pain (particularly in the upper right, over the liver)
- Dark urine (cola-colored)
- Clay-colored stools
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin and whites of eyes)
- Joint pain
- Low-grade fever
- Recovery takes 2 weeks to 3 months. There is no specific treatment

**Treatment**:
- Rest — the body must fight the virus on its own
- Hydration and nutrition
- Avoid alcohol and liver-toxic substances (acetaminophen/Tylenol) — the liver is already compromised
- Most healthy adults recover completely, though recovery is slow

**Prevention**: boiling and chemical treatment (chlorine, iodine) both kill hepatitis A virus. Filtration alone (unless rated for viruses at 0.02 microns) is NOT sufficient.

### 7.2 Water Treatment for Medical Use

In a post-collapse scenario, you may need purified water for medical purposes: wound irrigation, mixing medications, and preparing oral rehydration solutions. Medical-use water must be the cleanest water available.

**Wound Irrigation**
- Wound irrigation (flushing a wound with clean water) is one of the most important infection prevention measures. Dirt, debris, and bacteria in an open wound lead to infection, which in a scenario without antibiotics can be fatal
- Use water that has been boiled and cooled, or water that has been filtered through a 0.2-micron filter and chemically treated
- The volume matters more than the pressure: use at least 250 milliliters (approximately 1 cup) per inch of wound length. For a 3-inch cut, use at least 3 cups
- Deliver the water in a gentle stream. A clean plastic bottle with a small hole in the cap creates a usable irrigation stream. Alternatively, fill a clean plastic bag, poke a small hole, and squeeze
- Do NOT use untreated water for wound irrigation — you are introducing potential pathogens directly into an open wound
- After irrigating, pat the wound dry with the cleanest available cloth and cover with a clean bandage

**Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) — The WHO Formula**

Oral rehydration solution is arguably the single most important medical intervention in a survival scenario. It treats dehydration from diarrhea, vomiting, fever, heat exhaustion, and blood loss. The World Health Organization estimates that ORS saves millions of lives per year.

**Standard WHO Formula**:
- 1 liter (approximately 1 quart) of clean, treated water
- 6 level teaspoons of sugar (approximately 30 grams / 1 ounce)
- 1/2 level teaspoon of salt (approximately 2.5 grams)

Stir until both sugar and salt are completely dissolved.

**Step-by-Step Preparation**:
1. Start with 1 liter of the cleanest water available (boiled and cooled, or filtered and chemically treated)
2. Measure 6 level teaspoons of sugar. "Level" means scraped flat across the top of the teaspoon — not heaping. If you do not have measuring spoons, approximate: 6 level teaspoons is roughly 2 tablespoons, or the amount that fits in 3 standard sugar packets
3. Measure 1/2 level teaspoon of salt. This is a small amount — approximately the size of a pea's worth of salt, or one pinch between your thumb and two fingers
4. Add both to the water and stir until fully dissolved
5. Taste the solution — it should taste like tears. If it is saltier than tears, add a small amount of water. If it is sweeter, the sugar ratio is fine (a bit extra sugar is not harmful)

**Administering ORS**:
- For mild to moderate dehydration in adults: drink 200 to 400 milliliters (approximately 1 to 2 cups) after each episode of diarrhea or vomiting, plus regular sips between episodes
- For children: 50 to 100 milliliters (approximately 2 to 4 ounces) after each episode of diarrhea
- For infants: 1 to 2 teaspoons every 1 to 2 minutes
- Do NOT give large volumes at once to someone who is vomiting — it will come right back up. Give 1 tablespoon every 1 to 2 minutes instead
- Continue until urine output returns to normal and symptoms improve
- A severely dehydrated person may need 2 to 4 liters of ORS in the first 4 hours

**If Sugar and Salt Are Not Available**:
- Rice water (the starchy water left from boiling rice) with a pinch of salt provides a basic rehydration fluid
- Coconut water is a natural electrolyte solution and works as an emergency ORS
- Watered-down fruit juice with a pinch of salt provides sugar and electrolytes
- Even plain clean water, while not as effective as ORS, is far better than nothing

**Enhanced ORS (when available)**:
- Add 1/2 cup of orange juice or 1/4 teaspoon of potassium chloride (salt substitute/NoSalt) for potassium
- Add 1/2 teaspoon of baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) to help counteract acidosis from dehydration
- These additions improve the formula but are not required — the basic sugar-salt-water solution saves lives

### 7.3 When and How to Use Oral Rehydration

**Use ORS When**:
- Anyone has diarrhea (3 or more loose stools per day)
- Anyone is vomiting repeatedly
- Anyone has a fever above 101 degrees F (38.3 degrees C) with sweating
- Anyone shows signs of moderate dehydration (dark urine, dry mouth, dizziness, rapid pulse)
- Anyone has suffered heat exhaustion or heat stroke
- After significant blood loss (as a supplement to other care)
- When someone has been unable to drink adequate water due to illness

**Do NOT wait for severe dehydration to start ORS.** Begin at the first sign of fluid loss. Early intervention prevents the cascade toward severe dehydration and potential death.

**Monitoring Response to ORS**:
- Urine output increases (a good sign — the kidneys are getting enough fluid to function)
- Urine color lightens from dark amber toward pale yellow
- Heart rate slows toward normal (below 100 at rest)
- Dizziness and confusion improve
- Skin turgor improves (the pinch test on the back of the hand returns to normal in less than 2 seconds)
- Tears return when crying (especially important to monitor in children)

**When ORS Is Not Enough**:
- If a person is unconscious and cannot swallow — they need intravenous fluids (if available)
- If vomiting is so severe that no fluid stays down despite giving small sips every 1 to 2 minutes — try rectal rehydration as a last resort (administer ORS rectally using a tube or bulb syringe; the colon absorbs fluid)
- If diarrhea continues for more than 5 days with no improvement despite ORS — the infection may require antibiotics if available
- If blood appears in the stool — this suggests a more serious infection (dysentery) requiring antibiotics

### 7.4 Hygiene Water — Minimum Sanitation Requirements

The primary killer in most survival situations is not starvation or exposure — it is infectious disease spread by poor hygiene. Waterborne and fecal-oral diseases thrive when sanitation collapses. Maintaining basic hygiene requires dedicated water allocation, even when water is scarce.

**Minimum Hygiene Water Allocation**
- Hand washing: 0.25 gallons per person per day (6 handwashes per day at approximately 150 milliliters each)
- When to wash hands: before eating, before preparing food, after using the latrine, after handling waste, after caring for a sick person, after handling raw meat
- Hand washing technique: wet hands, apply soap (or ash — wood ash is an effective soap substitute that kills bacteria through alkalinity), scrub all surfaces of both hands including between fingers and under nails for at least 20 seconds, rinse, air dry or dry with a clean cloth

**Sanitizing Eating and Cooking Utensils**
- Wash with hot soapy water (or water with wood ash) after every use
- If water is extremely scarce: scrub utensils with sand or soil, then rinse with a small amount of clean water, then expose to direct sunlight for drying (UV helps kill remaining bacteria)
- Shared eating utensils spread disease — each person should have dedicated utensils if possible

**Laundry**
- Clothing worn close to the skin (underwear, socks, shirts) should be washed at least weekly to prevent skin infections, fungal infections, and parasite infestations
- Minimum laundry method: soak in hot water with soap or ash for 30 minutes, agitate by hand, rinse, and dry in direct sunlight (UV kills many pathogens and parasites)
- Water use: approximately 3 to 5 gallons per small load of hand laundry

**Bathing**
- A full-body wash at least once per week prevents skin infections, fungal infections (especially in warm, humid climates), and improves morale
- A sponge bath uses only 1 to 2 gallons of water — use a cloth, a basin of warm water, and soap or ash
- Focus on the high-priority areas: groin, armpits, feet, face, and hands. These areas accumulate the most bacteria and are most prone to infection

**Latrine Placement and Water Protection**
- Build latrines at least 100 feet (30 meters) from any water source
- Position latrines downhill and downstream from your water source
- Dig latrines at least 200 feet from where your group eats and sleeps
- Cover deposits with soil or ash after each use (reduces fly breeding, which is a major disease transmission vector)
- Wash hands after every latrine visit — this single practice prevents more disease than any other hygiene measure

**When Water Is Too Scarce for Hygiene**
If water is so scarce that you must choose between drinking and hygiene:
1. Drinking always takes priority — without adequate hydration, hygiene is irrelevant
2. Use waterless alternatives: ash for hand cleaning, sand for utensil scrubbing, sunlight for UV disinfection of surfaces
3. Alcohol-based hand sanitizer (60%+ ethanol) kills most bacteria without water — scavenge it from pharmacies, stores, and bathrooms
4. Even a tiny amount of water used for handwashing after latrine use provides disproportionate disease prevention benefit — prioritize this over bathing and laundry

## Chapter Summary and Quick Reference

### The Water Hierarchy of Priorities

1. **Immediate**: secure any available water — even if untreated, dehydration kills faster than most waterborne diseases. If you have no water and no way to treat it, drink what you find (except saltwater, flood water, or chemically contaminated water) and deal with the potential illness when you have the resources to do so.

2. **Short-term (hours to days)**: establish a purification method. Boiling is the simplest and most reliable. Chemical treatment (bleach) is the most portable.

3. **Medium-term (days to weeks)**: build a rainwater catchment system and a bio-sand filter. These two systems together provide a sustainable, reliable water supply.

4. **Long-term (weeks to months)**: dig a well if groundwater is accessible. Build a larger rainwater storage system. Establish redundant purification methods (filter + chemical + boiling capability).

### Quick-Reference Dosage Chart

| Treatment | Dosage | Contact Time | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------------|-------|
| Boiling | Rolling boil | 1 min (3 min above 6,500 ft) | Gold standard — kills everything biological |
| Bleach (8.25%) | 2 drops/gallon clear, 4 drops/gallon cloudy | 30 minutes | Does NOT kill Cryptosporidium |
| Iodine tablets | 1 tab/quart clear, 2 tabs/quart cloudy | 30-60 minutes | Not for pregnant women or long-term use |
| Calcium hypochlorite | 1 tsp per gallon = stock solution, then use like bleach | 30 minutes | 10+ year shelf life dry |
| Chlorine dioxide | Per manufacturer's instructions | 30 min (4 hrs for Crypto) | Most effective chemical against Crypto |
| SODIS | Fill PET bottle, lay in sun | 6 hours full sun, 2 days cloudy | Free, simple, but slow and weather-dependent |

This formula saves lives. Memorize it. Teach it to everyone in your group.
