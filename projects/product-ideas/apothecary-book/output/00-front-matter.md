## Sourcing Quality Herbs and Ingredients

### Where to Source

1. **Your own garden** — The freshest, most potent source. Start with easy-to-grow herbs: chamomile, lemon balm, peppermint, calendula, echinacea, lavender.

2. **Local herb farms and farmers' markets** — Fresh, locally grown herbs are often superior to dried imports. Ask about growing practices (organic, pesticide-free).

3. **Reputable online suppliers** — For herbs you can't grow yourself. Look for:
   - Organic certification (USDA Organic)
   - Third-party testing for contaminants (heavy metals, pesticides, microbial)
   - Clear country of origin labeling
   - Batch or lot numbers for traceability
   - Latin botanical names (not just common names)

4. **Wild harvesting (foraging)** — Free and potent, but requires knowledge:
   - **Positive identification is mandatory** — Never consume a plant you cannot identify with 100% certainty
   - Avoid areas sprayed with pesticides, herbicides, or near roads (exhaust contamination)
   - Never harvest endangered species
   - Follow the "rule of thirds": take no more than 1/3 of any plant stand
   - Harvest at peak potency: leaves before flowering, roots in fall, flowers when just opened
   - Get proper training or use multiple field guides for identification

### Quality Indicators

**Good signs:**
- Vibrant color (dried herbs should retain their natural color)
- Strong, characteristic aroma
- Clean, free of debris, insects, or mold
- Clearly labeled with botanical name, harvest date, and origin
- Stored in sealed, opaque containers

**Red flags:**
- Faded, brown, or dusty appearance
- Weak or no aroma
- Musty, moldy, or "off" smell
- No botanical name or origin listed
- Bulk bins exposed to light and air for extended periods

## Storage and Preservation

### Drying Fresh Herbs

**Air drying (simplest):**
1. Harvest herbs in the morning after dew has evaporated
2. Bundle stems together with twine (bundles of 4-6 stems)
3. Hang upside down in a warm, dry, well-ventilated area out of direct sunlight
4. Allow 1-3 weeks to dry completely
5. Herbs are dry when stems snap cleanly (not bend)

**Dehydrator drying (fastest):**
1. Spread herbs in a single layer on dehydrator trays
2. Set temperature to 95-110F (35-43C) for delicate leaves and flowers
3. Set temperature to 125-135F (52-57C) for roots and bark
4. Check every 2-4 hours; most herbs dry in 4-12 hours

**Oven drying (emergency method):**
1. Set oven to lowest temperature (usually 170F/77C)
2. Spread herbs on parchment-lined baking sheet
3. Prop oven door open slightly for air circulation
4. Check every 30 minutes; risk of overheating and losing volatile oils

### Storage Best Practices

| What | Store In | Location | Shelf Life |
|------|----------|----------|------------|
| Dried herbs (whole) | Glass jars, sealed | Cool, dark, dry | 1-3 years |
| Dried herbs (powdered) | Glass jars, sealed | Cool, dark, dry | 6-12 months |
| Tinctures | Amber glass bottles | Cool, dark | 3-5+ years |
| Infused oils | Dark glass bottles | Cool, dark | 1 year |
| Salves/balms | Glass jars or tins | Cool location | 1-2 years |
| Syrups | Glass jars, refrigerated | Refrigerator | 2-3 months |
| Fermented foods | Glass jars | Refrigerator | 6-12 months |
| Essential oils | Dark glass, sealed | Cool, dark | 2-5 years |
| Dried roots/bark | Glass jars, sealed | Cool, dark, dry | 2-4 years |

**Golden Rules of Storage:**
1. **Label everything** — Date, herb name (common AND botanical), preparation method, alcohol percentage (for tinctures)
2. **Dark glass only** — UV light degrades active compounds
3. **Keep dry** — Moisture causes mold; dried herbs must stay below 14% moisture content
4. **Cool but not frozen** — 60-70F (15-21C) is ideal; freezing can damage some preparations
5. **Check regularly** — Discard anything with mold, off-odors, or significant color loss
6. **Rotate stock** — Use oldest preparations first (FIFO: First In, First Out)

# Chapter 2: Mastering Preparation Methods

Every remedy in this book uses one or more of these core preparation techniques. Master these fundamentals and you can prepare any remedy confidently.

## Herbal Teas and Infusions

**What it is:** Hot water extraction of delicate plant parts (leaves, flowers, soft stems).

**Best for:** Chamomile, peppermint, lemon balm, lavender, nettle, hibiscus, rosehip.

**Method:**
1. Boil fresh, filtered water
2. Place 1-2 teaspoons dried herb (or 1 tablespoon fresh) per cup in a tea infuser, French press, or directly in a mug
3. Pour hot water over herbs
4. Cover (this traps volatile oils that would otherwise evaporate)
5. Steep 5-15 minutes (5 min for mild, 10-15 min for medicinal strength)
6. Strain and drink

**Pro tip:** A "nourishing infusion" (Susun Weed's method) uses 1 oz (28g) dried herb per quart of boiling water, steeped 4-8 hours or overnight. This extracts significantly more minerals and compounds than a standard tea.

## Decoctions

**What it is:** Simmered extraction of tough plant parts (roots, bark, seeds, dried berries).

**Best for:** Ginger root, turmeric root, dandelion root, valerian root, licorice root, cinnamon bark, elderberries.

**Method:**
1. Place 1-2 teaspoons of herb per cup of cold water in a saucepan
2. Bring to a boil
3. Reduce heat, cover, and simmer for 15-30 minutes (longer for very hard materials like bark)
4. Strain and drink

**Why different from tea?** Tough plant materials need sustained heat to break down cell walls and release active compounds. Simply pouring hot water over roots won't extract enough medicine.

## Tinctures

**What it is:** Alcohol extraction of herbs. The most concentrated and longest-lasting preparation method.

**Best for:** Any herb, but especially those with alcohol-soluble compounds (alkaloids, resins, volatile oils).

**Folk Method (Simpler's Method):**
1. Chop herb material (fresh: fill jar 2/3; dried: fill jar 1/3 to 1/2)
2. Cover completely with alcohol (80-proof vodka for most herbs; 190-proof grain alcohol for resins like myrrh or propolis)
3. Seal tightly and label with herb name, alcohol type, and date
4. Store in a cool, dark place for 4-6 weeks
5. Shake jar daily (or as often as you remember)
6. Strain through cheesecloth, squeezing to extract all liquid
7. Bottle in amber glass dropper bottles
8. Label with herb name, date, and alcohol percentage

**Weight-to-Volume Method:**
- Fresh herbs: 1:2 ratio (1g herb : 2mL alcohol), using 95% alcohol
- Dried herbs: 1:5 ratio (1g herb : 5mL alcohol), using 40-65% alcohol

**Standard dosage:** 30-60 drops (1-2 dropperfuls), 1-3 times daily, taken in a small amount of water.

**Shelf life:** 3-5+ years when stored properly.

**Glycerites (alcohol-free alternative):**
Replace alcohol with food-grade vegetable glycerin. Sweeter taste, suitable for children. Less potent extraction and shorter shelf life (1-2 years).

## Syrups

**What it is:** Sweet herbal preparation made by combining a strong decoction or infusion with honey or sugar.

**Best for:** Cough remedies, immune support, children's preparations.

**Method:**
1. Make a strong decoction (double the herb amount, simmer until liquid reduces by half)
2. Strain thoroughly
3. Measure the liquid
4. Add an equal volume of raw honey (1:1 ratio) while still warm (but below 110F to preserve enzymes)
5. Stir until fully combined
6. Bottle in glass jars

**Shelf life:** 2-3 months refrigerated. Adding 20% brandy or vodka extends shelf life to 6-12 months.

## Infused Oils

**What it is:** Carrier oil infused with the fat-soluble compounds of herbs. The base for salves, balms, and massage oils.

**Best for:** Calendula, comfrey, St. John's Wort, arnica, plantain, lavender, rosemary.

**Solar Infusion Method (Traditional):**
1. Fill a clean, dry glass jar 1/2 to 2/3 full with dried herb (MUST be dried — fresh herbs contain water that causes mold)
2. Cover completely with carrier oil (olive oil, sweet almond oil, or jojoba oil)
3. Ensure herbs are fully submerged (exposed herbs will mold)
4. Cap tightly and place in a sunny windowsill
5. Let infuse for 4-6 weeks, shaking gently every few days
6. Strain through cheesecloth into clean dark glass bottles

**Stovetop Method (Faster):**
1. Place herbs and oil in a double boiler (or glass bowl over simmering water)
2. Heat on the lowest setting for 2-4 hours, maintaining temperature below 110F
3. Strain and bottle

**Shelf life:** 1 year in dark glass, stored cool.

## Salves and Balms

**What it is:** Semi-solid topical preparations made by combining infused oil with beeswax.

**Best for:** Wound healing, skin conditions, muscle pain, chest rubs.

**Basic Salve Recipe:**
1. Gently warm 1 cup infused herbal oil in a double boiler
2. Add 1 oz (28g) beeswax, stirring until melted
3. Test consistency: dip a spoon, let it cool — if too soft, add more wax; if too hard, add more oil
4. Optional: add 10-20 drops essential oil (while still liquid but cooling)
5. Pour into tins or jars immediately (it sets fast)
6. Let cool completely before capping

**Ratios:**
- Soft balm: 1 cup oil : 0.75 oz beeswax
- Medium salve: 1 cup oil : 1 oz beeswax
- Firm salve: 1 cup oil : 1.25 oz beeswax
- Lip balm: 1 cup oil : 1.5 oz beeswax

**Shelf life:** 1-2 years.

## Poultices and Compresses

**What it is:** Direct application of herb material to the skin for localized treatment.

**Poultice (direct herb application):**
1. Mash fresh herbs into a paste (or mix dried herb powder with enough hot water to form a paste)
2. Apply directly to affected area
3. Cover with cloth or gauze to hold in place
4. Leave on 20 minutes to several hours
5. Repeat 2-3 times daily

**Compress (cloth soaked in herbal liquid):**
1. Make a strong tea or decoction
2. Soak a clean cloth in the warm (or cold) liquid
3. Wring out slightly and apply to affected area
4. Hold in place; re-soak when it cools (for warm compress) or warms (for cold compress)
5. Apply for 15-30 minutes, 2-3 times daily

## Fermented Preparations

**What it is:** Using beneficial bacteria and/or yeast to transform foods into probiotic-rich, easily digestible remedies.

**Best for:** Gut health, immune support, nutrient enhancement.

**Key principles:**
- Cleanliness matters (but you don't need sterility — beneficial bacteria naturally dominate in proper conditions)
- Salt creates an environment that favors beneficial lactobacilli over harmful bacteria
- Temperature affects fermentation speed (65-75F is ideal for most ferments)
- Submerge all vegetables below the brine line (exposed food molds)
- Fermentation produces CO2 — use airlock lids or "burp" jars daily

**Basic lacto-fermentation:** 2% salt by weight of vegetables. Pack tightly in jars, submerge below brine, ferment at room temperature for 3-14 days.

## Vinegar Preparations (Acetums)

**What it is:** Herbs extracted in vinegar. Milder than tinctures, suitable for those avoiding alcohol.

**Method:**
1. Fill a jar 1/3 to 1/2 with dried herbs (or 2/3 with fresh)
2. Cover with raw apple cider vinegar (with the mother)
3. Use a plastic lid (vinegar corrodes metal)
4. Steep 2-4 weeks, shaking daily
5. Strain and bottle

**Oxymels:** Combine herbal vinegar with honey (1:1 ratio) for a sweet-tart preparation excellent for respiratory conditions and immune support.

## Essential Oil Safety

Essential oils are extremely concentrated — a single drop of peppermint essential oil equals approximately 28 cups of peppermint tea. Handle with respect:

- **Never ingest** essential oils unless specifically trained and supervised
- **Always dilute** before skin application (1-2% dilution = 6-12 drops per ounce of carrier oil)
- **Patch test** new oils on inner forearm 24 hours before broader use
- **Keep away from eyes, ears, and mucous membranes**
- **Keep away from children and pets** (cats are especially sensitive to essential oils)
- **Pregnant women** should avoid most essential oils, especially in the first trimester
- **Some oils are phototoxic** (bergamot, lemon, lime, grapefruit) — avoid sun exposure after application
