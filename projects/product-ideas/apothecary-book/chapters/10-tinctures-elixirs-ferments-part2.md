# Chapter 10, Part 2: Elixirs & Bitters

---

## Understanding Elixirs and Bitters

### What Are They?

**Elixirs** are sweetened herbal preparations, usually combining tinctures with honey, syrup, or glycerin to make them more palatable. Historically, the word "elixir" carried connotations of a cure-all or life-extending potion.

**Bitters** are intensely bitter herbal preparations designed to stimulate the digestive system. When bitter compounds contact the tongue, they activate T2R bitter taste receptors, which trigger a cascade: increased saliva production, gastric acid secretion, bile release, and enhanced enzyme production. This is not folk belief -- the bitter taste receptor mechanism is well-established in physiology.

**Herbal Liqueurs** are alcohol-based preparations sweetened and flavored for sipping, often blurring the line between medicine and beverage.

## 1. Swedish Bitters (Traditional Maria Treben Formula)

**Category:** Digestive bitter, general tonic

### Ingredients (Small Swedish Bitters -- 11 herbs)
- 10g aloe vera powder (or wormwood powder as substitute)
- 5g myrrh (Commiphora myrrha)
- 0.2g saffron
- 10g senna leaves
- 10g camphor (natural, NOT synthetic)
- 10g rhubarb root
- 10g zedoary root (Curcuma zedoaria)
- 10g manna (Fraxinus ornus sap)
- 10g theriac venetian (a complex compound containing angelica, valerian, cinnamon, cardamom)
- 5g carline thistle root
- 10g angelica root

**Solvent:**
- 1.5 liters 38-45% alcohol (vodka, fruit brandy, or grain alcohol diluted to 40%)

### Preparation
1. Combine all dried herbs in a large glass container (at least 2-liter capacity)
2. Pour 1.5 liters of alcohol over the herbs
3. Seal with a tight-fitting lid
4. Place in a warm location (near a window or on a counter -- NOT in direct sunlight)
5. **Shake or stir daily** for 14 days minimum (traditional recipes call for up to 6 weeks)
6. After 14-42 days, strain through a fine cloth or coffee filter
7. Bottle in dark glass bottles
8. **Do not discard the herb residue** -- it can be topped up with fresh alcohol 2-3 more times for subsequent batches (each subsequent batch will be slightly weaker)

### Storage
- Dark glass bottles, cool dark place
- Shelf life: Indefinite when properly stored (the alcohol preserves it)
- Traditional claim: potency increases with age

### Traditional Uses
- Digestive stimulant before and after meals
- Bloating, gas, and indigestion
- Appetite stimulation
- Constipation relief (senna component)
- Headache (topical application to temples)
- Wound care (topical compress)
- General health tonic
- Maria Treben's "Old Manuscript" attributed 46 different healing properties to Swedish Bitters

### Scientific Evidence
- **Limited but plausible for digestive effects.** A 2015 study published in the Journal of Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine found that bitters both prevent and relieve indigestion.
- The digestive mechanism is physiologically sound: bitter compounds stimulate bitter taste receptors (T2R), increasing saliva, gastric juice, and bile production -- all containing digestive enzymes that aid fat and food breakdown.
- Individual ingredients have varying levels of evidence (myrrh has anti-inflammatory data, senna is a proven laxative, aloe has laxative effects).
- Phenol and tannin content provides documented antioxidant activity.
- **Limitation:** No randomized controlled trials on the complete Swedish Bitters formulation. Evidence is largely anecdotal or extrapolated from individual ingredient studies.

### Dosage
- **Digestive support:** 1 tablespoon diluted in water or herbal tea, before or after meals
- **General tonic:** 1 teaspoon in water, 1-3 times daily
- **Topical:** Apply to cotton pad and place on affected area

### Safety & Contraindications
- Contains SENNA, a stimulant laxative -- do not use daily long-term (risk of electrolyte imbalance, laxative dependence, and potassium depletion)
- Contains CAMPHOR -- toxic in large doses (seizures, liver damage). Do not exceed recommended doses.
- Contains ALOE latex -- a potent laxative; contraindicated in pregnancy, inflammatory bowel disease, and intestinal obstruction
- Not safe during pregnancy or breastfeeding
- Not safe for children
- May interact with heart medications (potassium depletion from laxative herbs can affect digoxin)
- Avoid with kidney disease
- May interact with blood thinners
- The alcohol content makes this unsuitable for people in recovery from alcohol addiction

## 2. Digestive Bitters (Simple Formula)

**Category:** Digestive support

### Ingredients
- 2 tablespoons dried gentian root (the "king of bitters")
- 1 tablespoon dried dandelion root
- 1 tablespoon dried burdock root
- 1 tablespoon dried orange peel (dried, organic)
- 1 teaspoon dried ginger root
- 1 teaspoon fennel seeds
- 2 cups 80-proof vodka

### Preparation
1. Combine all dried herbs in a pint mason jar
2. Cover with vodka, ensuring herbs are submerged
3. Seal, label, and store in a cool dark place for 4-6 weeks
4. Shake every other day
5. Strain through fine cheesecloth
6. Bottle in dark glass dropper bottles or small bitters bottles

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Take before meals to stimulate digestion
- Bloating, gas, and slow digestion
- Appetite stimulation (for people with poor appetite)
- Nausea relief
- Liver and gallbladder support

### Scientific Evidence
- **Well-supported mechanism.** The bitter taste receptor pathway (T2R receptors) is scientifically established. Bitter compounds on the tongue trigger a reflex arc that increases digestive secretions throughout the GI tract.
- Gentian contains amarogentin, one of the most bitter naturally occurring substances, and is officially recognized in European pharmacopeias as a digestive stimulant.
- Dandelion root has demonstrated choleretic (bile-stimulating) effects in animal studies.
- Ginger has strong evidence for anti-nausea effects (multiple RCTs).
- **Limitation:** While the mechanism is sound, clinical trials on specific bitters formulations are limited.

### Dosage
- **Before meals:** 1/4 to 1/2 teaspoon (or 15-30 drops) in a small amount of water, 15-30 minutes before eating
- **For acute indigestion:** Same dose as needed
- **Important:** The bitters MUST contact the tongue to activate the bitter reflex. Do not take in capsules -- that defeats the purpose.

### Safety & Contraindications
- Generally very safe when taken as directed
- Avoid with gastric or duodenal ulcers (stimulating acid production can worsen ulcers)
- Avoid with GERD/acid reflux (may increase stomach acid)
- Avoid with gallstones (bile stimulation could trigger a gallbladder attack)
- Not recommended during pregnancy (some bitter herbs are emmenagogues)
- Gentian may interact with blood pressure medications

## 3. Angostura-Style Aromatic Bitters

**Category:** Digestive, culinary/cocktail bitters

### Ingredients
- 2 tablespoons gentian root
- 1 tablespoon cinchona bark (source of quinine -- the classic bitter)
- 1 tablespoon dried orange peel
- 1 teaspoon whole cloves
- 1 teaspoon cardamom pods (cracked)
- 1 cinnamon stick (broken)
- 1/2 teaspoon allspice berries
- 1/4 teaspoon nutmeg (freshly grated)
- 1 star anise
- 2 cups 151-proof grain alcohol

### Preparation
1. Lightly toast whole spices in a dry pan for 2-3 minutes until fragrant (do NOT burn)
2. Combine all botanicals in a pint mason jar
3. Cover with high-proof grain alcohol
4. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 2-3 weeks
5. Shake daily
6. Strain through fine cheesecloth, then through a coffee filter for clarity
7. Sweeten lightly with 1-2 tablespoons simple syrup or honey if desired
8. Bottle in small dark glass bottles with dropper tops

### Storage
- Dark glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: Indefinite (high alcohol content)

### Traditional Uses
- Digestive aid (a few dashes in soda water after a heavy meal)
- Nausea and upset stomach remedy
- Cocktail ingredient (the original purpose of cocktail bitters was medicinal)
- Hiccup remedy (folk tradition)
- Appetite stimulant

### Scientific Evidence
- **Supported for digestive stimulation** via the T2R bitter taste receptor mechanism (see Digestive Bitters above).
- Cinchona bark (quinine) has well-established antimalarial and anti-inflammatory properties, though the amounts in bitters are far below therapeutic doses.
- Individual spice components (cloves, cinnamon, cardamom) have documented antioxidant and antimicrobial properties.

### Dosage
- **Digestive:** 1-5 dashes (approximately 1/4 teaspoon) in water or soda water before or after meals
- **In cocktails:** 2-4 dashes per drink
- These are concentrated -- a little goes a long way

### Safety & Contraindications
- Very safe in the small quantities typically used (dashes, not ounces)
- Cinchona bark can cause cinchonism (tinnitus, headache, nausea) in large quantities -- not a concern at bitters doses
- Avoid cinchona bark if you have a quinine allergy
- Not recommended for daily use in large quantities during pregnancy

## 4. Adaptogenic Energy Elixir

**Category:** Adaptogen, energy, stress resilience

### Ingredients

**Base tinctures (make each separately first, or purchase):**
- 30 mL ashwagandha root tincture
- 30 mL rhodiola root tincture
- 20 mL eleuthero (Siberian ginseng) root tincture
- 15 mL schisandra berry tincture
- 15 mL licorice root tincture

**Elixir base:**
- 100 mL raw honey (warmed to liquid)
- 50 mL filtered water

### Preparation
1. Prepare each tincture separately using the standard method (1:5 dried herb in 50% alcohol, macerated for 6 weeks) or purchase from a reputable source
2. In a clean glass measuring cup, combine all five tinctures
3. Warm honey gently (do not exceed 110F/43C to preserve enzymes)
4. Add warm honey and water to the tincture blend
5. Stir thoroughly until homogeneous
6. Bottle in dark glass with a tight seal
7. Label with all ingredients, date, and dosage

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place or refrigerate
- Shelf life: 1-2 years (honey preserves but water content shortens life)
- Shake well before each use (honey may settle)

### Traditional Uses
- Daily energy and stamina without stimulant effects
- Stress resilience and adrenal support
- Mental clarity and focus
- Recovery from burnout or prolonged stress
- Athletic performance support
- Immune support during high-stress periods

### Scientific Evidence
- **Well-supported for the individual adaptogens.** Evidence from RCTs confirms ashwagandha and rhodiola exert multi-dimensional adaptogenic effects, improving psychophysical health and supporting stress resilience through regulation of the HPA axis, neurotransmission, and immune and hormonal pathways.
- Eleuthero (Eleutherococcus senticosus) has decades of research from Russian studies showing improved work capacity and stress resistance, though study quality varies.
- Schisandra is classified as an adaptogen and hepatoprotectant in Russian and Chinese pharmacopeias.
- Licorice root (glycyrrhizin) supports adrenal function and extends the half-life of cortisol, which can be beneficial in adrenal fatigue but harmful with chronic use.
- **The combination** has traditional support (adaptogenic blends are common in TCM and Ayurveda) but no clinical trials on this specific formulation.

### Dosage
- **Daily tonic:** 1-2 teaspoons (5-10 mL) in the morning, mixed in warm water, tea, or coffee
- **Before stressful events:** 1 teaspoon 30-60 minutes beforehand
- Take in the morning or early afternoon (may interfere with sleep)
- Cycle: 6 weeks on, 1-2 weeks off

### Safety & Contraindications
- Individual adaptogen contraindications apply (see ashwagandha and rhodiola entries in Part 1)
- **Licorice root:** Do NOT use for more than 6 weeks continuously. Glycyrrhizin can cause potassium depletion, sodium retention, edema, and hypertension. Use deglycyrrhizinated licorice (DGL) if long-term use is desired.
- Not recommended during pregnancy or breastfeeding
- May interact with blood pressure medications, blood thinners, diabetes medications, thyroid medications, and immunosuppressants
- Avoid late in the day (stimulating effects of rhodiola and eleuthero)

## 6. Calming Nervine Elixir

**Category:** Sleep support, anxiety relief

### Ingredients

**Base tinctures:**
- 30 mL passionflower tincture
- 25 mL valerian root tincture
- 20 mL skullcap tincture
- 15 mL California poppy tincture
- 15 mL lemon balm tincture
- 10 mL hops tincture (Humulus lupulus)

**Elixir base:**
- 80 mL raw honey
- 50 mL glycerin (vegetable)

### Preparation
1. Prepare each tincture separately (or purchase)
2. Combine all tinctures in a clean glass container
3. Add honey (warmed to liquid) and glycerin
4. Stir thoroughly until combined
5. Bottle in dark glass
6. Label with all herbs, date, and "FOR EVENING USE ONLY" warning

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place or refrigerate
- Shelf life: 1-2 years
- Shake well before use

### Traditional Uses
- Insomnia and difficulty falling asleep
- Anxiety and nervous tension
- Restlessness and racing mind
- Muscle tension before bed
- Stress-related sleep difficulties

### Scientific Evidence
- **Moderately supported for the combination.** Passionflower + valerian + hops is one of the best-studied herbal sleep combinations, with several clinical trials showing reduced sleep latency and improved sleep quality.
- Passionflower alone was comparable to oxazepam for anxiety in one RCT.
- Valerian may improve sleep quality with regular use over 2-4 weeks.
- Lemon balm (Melissa officinalis) has demonstrated anxiolytic effects in several small studies, working via GABA-transaminase inhibition.
- California poppy adds mild sedative and analgesic effects via GABA-A receptor binding.
- **The combination is traditional and widely used** but the specific formulation has not been studied in clinical trials.

### Dosage
- **Sleep:** 2-4 teaspoons (10-20 mL) in warm water or chamomile tea, 30-60 minutes before bed
- **Evening anxiety:** 1-2 teaspoons as needed in the evening
- **Do NOT use during the day** if you need to drive or operate machinery

### Safety & Contraindications
- **SEDATING -- do not drive or operate machinery after taking**
- Do NOT combine with prescription sleep aids, benzodiazepines, barbiturates, antihistamines, or alcohol
- Do NOT combine with antidepressants (especially MAO inhibitors -- passionflower concern)
- Not safe during pregnancy or breastfeeding
- Valerian may cause paradoxical stimulation in some people
- Hops should be avoided in estrogen-sensitive conditions (mildly estrogenic)
- May cause morning grogginess -- reduce dose if this occurs
- Taper off gradually after regular use (valerian withdrawal possible)

## 7. Elderberry & Honey Elixir (Elderberry Syrup)

**Category:** Immune support, cold and flu

### Ingredients
- 1 cup dried elderberries (Sambucus nigra)
- 3 cups filtered water
- 2 tablespoons fresh ginger (grated)
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 5 whole cloves
- 1 cup raw honey
- Optional: 1/2 cup brandy or vodka (for extended shelf life)

### Preparation
1. Combine elderberries, water, ginger, cinnamon, and cloves in a saucepan
2. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a simmer
3. Simmer uncovered for 45-60 minutes until liquid is reduced by approximately half
4. Remove from heat and allow to cool until comfortable to touch (below 110F/43C)
5. Strain through fine mesh strainer, pressing berries to extract all juice
6. Discard spent berries and spices
7. Stir in raw honey until fully dissolved (adding honey while too hot destroys beneficial enzymes)
8. Optional: Add 1/2 cup brandy or vodka for preservation (extends shelf life significantly)
9. Bottle in clean glass jars or bottles

### Storage
- **Without alcohol:** Refrigerate immediately. Use within 2-3 months.
- **With alcohol:** Refrigerate. Use within 6-12 months.
- Always use a clean spoon to dispense

### Traditional Uses
- Cold and flu prevention and treatment
- Immune support during winter
- Sore throat soother
- General winter tonic
- Children's immune support (non-alcohol version, children over 1 year only due to honey)

### Scientific Evidence
- See Elderberry Tincture entry in Part 1 for detailed evidence
- The syrup/elixir form is the most commonly studied preparation
- Honey itself has documented antimicrobial and cough-suppressing properties (multiple RCTs show honey is superior to dextromethorphan for childhood cough)

### Dosage
- **Prevention:** Adults: 1 tablespoon daily. Children (over 1): 1 teaspoon daily.
- **Acute illness:** Adults: 1 tablespoon every 3-4 hours. Children: 1 teaspoon every 3-4 hours.
- **Duration:** Up to 5 days during acute illness

### Safety & Contraindications
- **NEVER give honey to infants under 1 year** (risk of infant botulism)
- Raw elderberries are TOXIC -- they must be cooked
- See elderberry tincture entry for full contraindication list
- Alcohol-containing version is not suitable for children, pregnant women, or people in recovery

## 8. Fire Cider Elixir

**Category:** Immune stimulant, circulatory stimulant, digestive tonic

### Ingredients
- 1/2 cup fresh horseradish root (grated)
- 1/2 cup fresh ginger root (grated)
- 1 medium onion (chopped)
- 10 cloves garlic (crushed)
- 2 fresh cayenne peppers (chopped) or 1 teaspoon cayenne powder
- 1 lemon (zest and juice)
- 2 tablespoons fresh rosemary
- 2 tablespoons fresh thyme
- 1 tablespoon turmeric root (grated) or 1 teaspoon ground turmeric
- 1/4 teaspoon black pepper
- Raw apple cider vinegar with mother (enough to fill jar)
- Raw honey to taste (typically 1/4 to 1/2 cup)

### Preparation
1. Grate horseradish and ginger, chop onion, crush garlic, chop peppers
2. Place all ingredients EXCEPT vinegar and honey in a quart mason jar, filling about 2/3 full
3. Pour raw apple cider vinegar over everything until jar is completely full and all ingredients are submerged
4. **Important:** If using a metal lid, place parchment paper or plastic wrap between the lid and jar (vinegar corrodes metal)
5. Label with date and contents
6. Store in a warm spot (back of the stove, sunny windowsill) for 4-6 weeks
7. Shake daily
8. After 4-6 weeks, strain through fine mesh, pressing solids firmly to extract all liquid
9. Add honey to taste, stirring until dissolved
10. Bottle in glass jars

### Storage
- Glass jar or bottle, refrigerate
- Shelf life: 6-12 months refrigerated
- The flavor intensifies and mellows with age

### Traditional Uses
- Immune booster at onset of cold or flu
- Circulatory stimulant (warming)
- Digestive tonic
- Sinus congestion and mucus relief
- General winter tonic
- Popularized by herbalist Rosemary Gladstar in the 1970s

### Scientific Evidence
- **Individual ingredients are well-studied.** Garlic (allicin) has documented antimicrobial and immune-supporting properties. Ginger has strong anti-nausea and anti-inflammatory evidence. Turmeric (curcumin) is a proven anti-inflammatory. Cayenne (capsaicin) improves circulation and has analgesic properties. Apple cider vinegar has modest evidence for blood sugar regulation.
- **The specific combination** has no clinical trial evidence.
- Fire cider is best understood as a "food as medicine" preparation -- it provides concentrated doses of culinary plants with known health properties.
- The acetic acid in vinegar extracts some plant compounds but is less efficient than alcohol.

### Dosage
- **Prevention:** 1-2 tablespoons daily, straight or in water
- **Acute illness:** 1 tablespoon every 3-4 hours
- **Culinary use:** Use as salad dressing, marinade, or shot
- **Dilute if too intense** -- can be mixed with water, juice, or honey

### Safety & Contraindications
- **Very spicy** -- can cause intense burning in the mouth, throat, and stomach. Start with small amounts.
- Avoid on an empty stomach if you have acid reflux, ulcers, or gastritis
- The horseradish and cayenne may trigger acid reflux in susceptible people
- Not suitable for young children (too spicy)
- Garlic may interact with blood thinners
- Capsaicin can irritate hemorrhoids
- Avoid vinegar if you have stomach ulcers
- Not recommended in large quantities during pregnancy (emmenagogue herbs present)

## 9. Immune-Boosting Elderberry-Astragalus Elixir

**Category:** Deep immune tonic

### Ingredients
- 1/2 cup dried elderberries
- 1/4 cup dried astragalus root slices
- 2 tablespoons dried echinacea root
- 1 tablespoon dried rosehips
- 1 tablespoon dried ginger
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 3 cups water
- 1 cup raw honey
- 1/4 cup brandy (optional preservative)

### Preparation
1. Combine elderberries, astragalus, echinacea, rosehips, ginger, and cinnamon in a saucepan with water
2. Bring to a boil, reduce heat, and simmer for 45-60 minutes until reduced by half
3. Remove from heat, cool to below 110F/43C
4. Strain through fine mesh, pressing herbs to extract liquid
5. Stir in honey and brandy (if using)
6. Bottle in clean glass

### Storage
- Refrigerate. Shelf life: 2-3 months without alcohol, 6-12 months with brandy.

### Traditional Uses
- Deep immune tonic for chronic immune weakness
- Prevention during cold and flu season
- Recovery from prolonged illness
- Support for people prone to frequent infections

### Scientific Evidence
- Combines herbs with individual evidence (see elderberry, astragalus, and echinacea entries in Part 1)
- Astragalus is considered an immune tonic for prevention (not acute illness per TCM)
- Rosehips add significant vitamin C (well-established for immune function)
- The specific combination is not clinically studied

### Dosage
- **Prevention:** 1 tablespoon daily during cold/flu season
- **Acute illness:** 1 tablespoon 3-4 times daily
- **Not for long-term continuous use** due to echinacea (cycle 8 weeks on, 2 weeks off)

### Safety & Contraindications
- See individual herb entries for astragalus, elderberry, and echinacea
- Do NOT use astragalus during acute illness (TCM principle)
- Avoid with autoimmune conditions
- Not safe for children under 1 (honey)
- Alcohol version not suitable for pregnant women or children

## 10. Herbal Digestive Liqueur (Chartreuse-Style)

**Category:** Digestive, after-dinner liqueur

### Ingredients
- 2 tablespoons dried angelica root
- 1 tablespoon dried lemon balm
- 1 tablespoon dried hyssop
- 1 tablespoon dried peppermint
- 1 teaspoon dried thyme
- 1 teaspoon dried marjoram
- 1/2 teaspoon whole cloves
- 1/2 teaspoon coriander seeds
- 1 small piece of mace
- Zest of 1 lemon
- 3 cups 100-proof vodka or brandy
- 1 cup simple syrup (1:1 sugar:water, cooled) or honey syrup

### Preparation
1. Combine all herbs and spices in a quart mason jar
2. Cover with vodka or brandy
3. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 3-4 weeks
4. Shake every other day
5. Strain through fine cheesecloth, then through a coffee filter for clarity
6. Add simple syrup or honey syrup to taste (start with 1/2 cup, add more as desired)
7. Bottle in attractive glass bottles
8. Allow to age for at least 2 additional weeks before drinking (the flavor mellows and rounds out)

### Storage
- Glass bottle, cool dark place
- Shelf life: Indefinite (high alcohol and sugar content preserve it)
- Flavor improves with age over months to years

### Traditional Uses
- After-dinner digestive (digestif)
- Digestive comfort after heavy meals
- The tradition of herbal liqueurs as digestive aids spans centuries in European monastic traditions (Chartreuse, Benedictine)
- Carminative effects (reduces gas and bloating)
- The ritual of sipping a small glass after dinner aids digestion through multiple mechanisms: bitter/aromatic stimulation, relaxation, and mindful pause

### Scientific Evidence
- Peppermint is well-studied for IBS and digestive comfort (multiple RCTs).
- Angelica root is recognized in European pharmacopeias as a digestive stimulant.
- Aromatic herbs (thyme, marjoram, hyssop) contain volatile oils with carminative (gas-reducing) properties.
- Lemon balm has demonstrated smooth muscle relaxant effects in the GI tract.
- **The liqueur tradition** is supported by the physiological mechanism of bitter/aromatic digestive stimulation, though the specific recipe is not clinically studied.

### Dosage
- **After dinner:** 1-2 oz (30-60 mL) sipped slowly
- **As needed for digestive discomfort:** 1 oz in warm water

### Safety & Contraindications
- Contains alcohol -- all standard alcohol cautions apply
- Not suitable for people in recovery from alcohol addiction
- Not recommended during pregnancy
- Angelica root may increase photosensitivity
- Small amounts used as digestif have minimal risks for healthy adults

## 12. Throat Coat Elixir

**Category:** Respiratory support, sore throat

### Ingredients
- 30 mL marshmallow root tincture (Althaea officinalis)
- 20 mL licorice root tincture
- 20 mL slippery elm tincture (Ulmus rubra)
- 15 mL echinacea tincture
- 15 mL sage tincture
- 100 mL raw honey
- 30 mL fresh lemon juice

### Preparation
1. Combine all tinctures in a clean glass container
2. Add honey (warmed to liquid)
3. Add fresh lemon juice
4. Stir thoroughly
5. Bottle in dark glass

### Storage
- Refrigerate after making (lemon juice shortens shelf life)
- Shelf life: 3-6 months refrigerated
- Shake before use

### Traditional Uses
- Sore throat relief
- Cough suppression
- Laryngitis and voice recovery
- Dry, irritated throat
- Mucous membrane soothing

### Scientific Evidence
- Marshmallow root contains mucilage that physically coats and soothes irritated mucous membranes -- this is a direct mechanical effect, well-established.
- Licorice root (glycyrrhizin) has demonstrated anti-inflammatory and demulcent properties. A 2009 study found licorice gargle reduced sore throat after surgery.
- Slippery elm is recognized by the FDA as a safe and effective oral demulcent.
- Honey has strong evidence as a cough suppressant (multiple RCTs, superior to dextromethorphan in children).
- Sage has demonstrated anti-inflammatory properties for pharyngitis in at least one RCT.

### Dosage
- **Sore throat:** 1-2 teaspoons every 2-3 hours, swallowed slowly to coat the throat
- **Cough:** 1 teaspoon as needed, up to 6 times daily
- **Voice recovery:** 1 teaspoon 4-6 times daily, especially before and after voice use

### Safety & Contraindications
- **Licorice root:** Do not use for more than 2 weeks continuously in large amounts (hypertension, potassium depletion risk). Use DGL licorice for long-term formulas.
- Slippery elm may slow absorption of other medications -- take 2 hours apart from prescriptions.
- Not for infants under 1 (honey)
- Licorice may interact with blood pressure medications, potassium-depleting diuretics, and corticosteroids
