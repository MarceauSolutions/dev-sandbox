## PART 2: GUT HEALING REMEDIES

The intestinal lining is a single layer of epithelial cells -- the largest interface between the body's interior and the external environment. When this barrier is compromised (often called "increased intestinal permeability" or colloquially "leaky gut"), incompletely digested food particles, bacterial toxins, and other substances can pass into the bloodstream, triggering immune reactions and systemic inflammation. While "leaky gut syndrome" as a diagnosis remains debated in conventional medicine, the underlying concept of intestinal permeability is well-established in peer-reviewed research, and many conventional gastroenterologists now acknowledge its role in conditions ranging from inflammatory bowel disease to autoimmune disorders.

The remedies in this section focus on restoring and maintaining the integrity of the gut lining, supporting the gut microbiome, and reducing gastrointestinal inflammation.

### 2.1 Bone Broth for Gut Healing

**Historical Background:**
Bone broth is arguably the most ancient prepared food still in regular use. Every culture with access to animal bones developed some form of long-simmered bone broth -- Chinese medicine has used it for thousands of years to support digestion and the kidneys, Jewish penicillin (chicken soup) is proverbial, and South American, African, and European cuisines all feature long-cooked bone broths. The modern "bone broth movement" of the 2010s-2020s has refocused attention on its gut-healing properties.

**Key Components:**
- **Gelatin/Collagen:** Long simmering extracts collagen from bones and connective tissue, which hydrolyzes into gelatin. Gelatin provides the amino acids glycine, proline, and glutamine -- all critical for gut lining repair.
- **Glycine:** Supports bile acid conjugation, is anti-inflammatory, and directly supports mucosal barrier integrity.
- **Glutamine:** The primary fuel source for intestinal epithelial cells (enterocytes). See entry 2.2 for more on glutamine.
- **Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs):** Including hyaluronic acid and chondroitin sulfate, extracted from cartilage. Support mucosal lining.
- **Minerals:** Calcium, magnesium, phosphorus, and trace minerals leach from bones during long cooking (though in smaller amounts than often claimed).

**Recipe -- Classic Gut-Healing Bone Broth:**

*Ingredients:*
- 2-3 lbs mixed bones (beef marrow bones, knuckle bones, and/or oxtail; chicken feet and backs; or a mix)
- Chicken feet are particularly high in collagen (include 2-4 if available)
- 2 tablespoons apple cider vinegar (helps extract minerals from bones)
- 1 onion, quartered (skin on is fine)
- 2-3 carrots, roughly chopped
- 2-3 celery stalks with leaves
- 4-5 garlic cloves, smashed
- 1 tablespoon whole peppercorns
- 2 bay leaves
- Fresh herbs as desired (thyme, parsley stems, rosemary)
- Filtered water to cover (approximately 12-16 cups)
- 1 tablespoon sea salt (adjust to taste)

*Method:*
1. **Optional but recommended:** Roast bones at 400F for 30-40 minutes for deeper flavor (not required for medicinal purpose)
2. Place bones in a large stockpot or slow cooker
3. Add apple cider vinegar and enough cold water to cover bones by 2 inches
4. Let sit for 30 minutes (allows acid to begin mineral extraction before heating)
5. Bring to a boil, then immediately reduce to the lowest simmer possible
6. Skim any foam/scum that rises in the first 30 minutes
7. Add vegetables, garlic, herbs, and peppercorns
8. **Simmer time:**
   - Chicken bones: 12-24 hours
   - Beef/pork bones: 24-48 hours
   - Fish bones: 4-8 hours (do not oversimmer -- becomes bitter)
9. Add salt in the last hour of cooking
10. Strain through a fine-mesh strainer or cheesecloth
11. Cool and refrigerate. Good broth will gel when cold (like Jell-O) -- this indicates high gelatin content. If it does not gel, the next batch needs more collagen-rich bones (chicken feet, pig feet, knuckle bones) or longer cooking.
12. Keeps 5 days refrigerated, 6 months frozen

*Gut-Healing Protocol:*
- Drink 8-16 oz warm bone broth daily, ideally on an empty stomach or before meals
- For acute gut healing: 2-3 cups daily for 2-4 weeks
- Can be used as the base for soups, for cooking grains, or simply sipped
- For those doing an elimination diet or gut-healing protocol, bone broth is often the foundational food

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Glycine has demonstrated anti-inflammatory effects in the gut in multiple studies. A 2017 review in *Amino Acids* documented glycine's protective effects on intestinal epithelial cells.
- Glutamine supplementation improves intestinal permeability (see entry 2.2 for detailed evidence).
- Gelatin has been shown to protect the gastric mucosal lining in animal studies.
- A 2019 study found that collagen peptides improved symptoms in patients with inflammatory bowel disease.
- **Honest assessment:** While the individual components (glycine, glutamine, gelatin) have evidence supporting gut healing, bone broth itself has not been studied in controlled clinical trials. The studies are on isolated components, not the whole food. The broth may also contain variable amounts of heavy metals (lead in particular has been found in bone broth, especially from conventionally raised animals). The clinical significance of this is debated, but sourcing bones from pasture-raised animals is prudent.

**Safety Warnings:**
- Generally very safe as a food.
- **Lead content:** A 2013 study in *Medical Hypotheses* found that bone broth (especially chicken) contained several times more lead than the water alone. Use bones from organic/pasture-raised animals when possible.
- **Histamine:** Long-cooked foods are high in histamine. People with histamine intolerance may react to bone broth with headaches, flushing, digestive upset, or anxiety. Shorter cooking times (4-8 hours) produce less histamine.
- **Glutamate sensitivity:** The high glutamine/glutamate content may cause reactions in people sensitive to MSG (they are chemically related).
- **Sodium:** Commercial bone broths can be very high in sodium. Homemade allows control.
- **Oxalate:** If adding greens to broth, this can increase oxalate content (relevant for kidney stone formers).

### 2.2 L-Glutamine for Gut Lining Repair

**Background:**
L-glutamine is the most abundant amino acid in the human body and the primary fuel source for enterocytes (intestinal epithelial cells). During periods of stress, illness, or intense exercise, the body's demand for glutamine exceeds what muscles can produce, making it conditionally essential. Supplemental glutamine has become a cornerstone of integrative gut-healing protocols.

**How It Works:**
- Provides direct fuel to intestinal cells for repair and regeneration
- Supports the rapid cell turnover of the gut lining (intestinal cells replace themselves every 3-5 days)
- Supports tight junction protein expression (the "seals" between intestinal cells that prevent permeability)
- Modulates intestinal immune function
- Reduces inflammatory cytokine signaling in the gut

**Protocol:**
1. **Form:** L-glutamine powder (unflavored) is most cost-effective and allows flexible dosing. Capsules available but require many capsules for therapeutic doses.
2. **Gut-healing dose:** 5-10 grams, 2-3x daily (15-30 grams total daily)
3. **Maintenance dose:** 5 grams daily
4. **Timing:** Best taken on an empty stomach (dissolve in room-temperature water -- heat can degrade glutamine)
5. **Duration:** 8-12 weeks for gut healing, then taper to maintenance
6. **Start lower (5g/day) and increase** over 1-2 weeks to assess tolerance

**Mixing:** Dissolve powder in 4-8 oz room temperature or cool water. Stir well. It has a mild, slightly sweet taste and dissolves readily. Can also be added to smoothies (but not hot beverages).

**Scientific Evidence:**
- **Strong evidence base.** Glutamine is used in hospital settings for critically ill patients and burn victims specifically to support gut barrier function.
- A 2017 randomized controlled trial in *Gut* showed glutamine supplementation significantly reduced intestinal permeability in patients with post-infectious IBS.
- A 2012 study demonstrated that glutamine supplementation reduced markers of intestinal permeability after intense exercise.
- Multiple studies show glutamine supports tight junction integrity in cell culture and animal models.
- Used in clinical oncology to protect gut lining during chemotherapy and radiation.
- A 2015 review in *International Journal of Molecular Sciences* comprehensively documented glutamine's role in intestinal barrier function.

**Safety Warnings:**
- Generally well-tolerated at recommended doses.
- **People with liver disease or hepatic encephalopathy:** Glutamine is metabolized to glutamate and ammonia. In people with impaired liver function, excess ammonia can accumulate and worsen hepatic encephalopathy. AVOID in liver cirrhosis without medical supervision.
- **Cancer considerations:** Because glutamine fuels rapidly dividing cells, there is theoretical concern about supplementing during active cancer. However, clinical studies in oncology patients have not shown harm, and many oncologists use it to protect the gut during treatment. Discuss with your oncologist.
- **Kidney disease:** High-dose glutamine increases the nitrogen load on kidneys. Use with caution in kidney disease.
- **MSG sensitivity:** Glutamine converts to glutamate. People highly sensitive to MSG may experience headaches, flushing, or anxiety at high doses.
- **Mania/bipolar disorder:** Case reports suggest glutamine may theoretically worsen manic episodes (glutamine converts to glutamate, an excitatory neurotransmitter). Use with caution in bipolar disorder.
- **Drug interactions:** Few significant interactions, but may affect medications that depend on amino acid transport.

### 2.3 Slippery Elm Bark Gruel (Ulmus rubra)

**Historical Background:**
Slippery elm is a North American tree whose inner bark has been used by Native American peoples for centuries as food and medicine. It was a survival food during famines and was standard issue in the American Civil War for treating wounds and dysentery. It was listed in the United States Pharmacopeia from 1820 to 1960 and remains one of the few traditional remedies still regarded favorably by mainstream pharmacology. The inner bark contains mucilage that swells enormously when mixed with water, creating a soothing, protective gel.

**Active Components:**
- **Mucilage (polysaccharides):** Forms a thick, slippery gel that coats and soothes inflamed mucous membranes throughout the GI tract
- **Antioxidants:** Including procyanidins and catechins
- **Tannins:** Mild astringent properties that help reduce inflammation

**Preparation -- Slippery Elm Gruel (Traditional):**

*Ingredients:*
- 1-2 tablespoons slippery elm bark powder (inner bark, finely powdered)
- 1 cup water or milk (dairy, coconut, or almond)
- Honey, cinnamon, or nutmeg to taste (optional)

*Method:*
1. Place slippery elm powder in a small saucepan
2. Add a small amount of cold water (2-3 tablespoons) and stir to form a smooth paste (this prevents lumps)
3. Gradually add remaining liquid, stirring continuously
4. Heat gently over medium-low heat, stirring constantly, until it thickens to a porridge-like consistency (3-5 minutes)
5. Remove from heat
6. Add honey, cinnamon, or nutmeg if desired
7. Eat/drink warm
8. The texture is thick, smooth, and somewhat mucilaginous -- this is correct

*Gut-Healing Protocol:*
- 1 bowl of gruel, 1-3x daily
- Take 30 minutes before meals (coats the stomach and intestinal lining before food arrives)
- Continue for 2-8 weeks during active gut healing
- Can also be taken as simple tea: 1-2 teaspoons powder stirred into warm water, 3-4x daily

**Other Preparations:**
- **Capsules:** 400-500 mg, 3-4 capsules, 3x daily
- **Lozenges:** Available commercially for sore throat; some gut healing benefit
- **Cold infusion:** Stir 1 tablespoon into cold water, let sit 30 minutes (forms a thick gel), drink

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Slippery elm is classified as a demulcent (mucilage-producing, soothing agent) in pharmacology.
- A 2010 pilot study in *Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine* found a slippery elm-containing formula improved bowel habits and reduced symptoms in IBS patients.
- The mucilage has been shown to stimulate mucus secretion in the GI tract, adding a layer of protection to inflamed tissue.
- In vitro studies show antioxidant activity of slippery elm bark extracts.
- **Honest assessment:** While the mechanism is well-understood (physical coating and soothing of inflamed tissue) and centuries of clinical use support its safety and efficacy, rigorous controlled clinical trials specific to slippery elm alone are limited.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Very safe.** One of the safest remedies in this entire chapter.
- **Drug absorption:** The mucilage coating may slow or reduce absorption of medications taken simultaneously. Take medications 2 hours before or after slippery elm.
- **Allergic reactions** are rare but possible.
- **Pregnancy:** Generally considered safe and was historically given to pregnant women for morning sickness. However, one source (American Herbal Products Association) has classified it as potentially unsafe for pregnancy based on theoretical concerns about bark adulterants. Most herbalists consider pure inner bark powder safe.
- **Sustainability concern:** Slippery elm trees are being over-harvested and are susceptible to Dutch elm disease. Source from sustainable suppliers and consider marshmallow root (entry 2.4) as an alternative demulcent.
- **Quality control:** Ensure you purchase inner bark powder, not whole bark (which includes the outer bark and may be less effective and potentially more irritating).

### 2.4 Marshmallow Root Tea (Althaea officinalis)

**Historical Background:**
Not to be confused with the confection (which was originally made from this plant), marshmallow root has been used medicinally since ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome. Its genus name "Althaea" comes from the Greek "altho" meaning "to heal." It was used for sore throats, digestive inflammation, urinary tract irritation, and wound healing. The plant grows wild throughout Europe and Western Asia and has been cultivated for centuries.

**Active Components:**
- **Mucilage (up to 35% of root):** Similar to slippery elm, forms a protective gel that coats mucous membranes. Marshmallow root has even higher mucilage content than slippery elm.
- **Pectin:** Prebiotic fiber that supports beneficial gut bacteria.
- **Flavonoids:** Anti-inflammatory and antioxidant.
- **Asparagine:** An amino acid that supports cellular repair.

**Preparation -- Cold Infusion (Preferred Method for Maximum Mucilage):**

*Ingredients:*
- 2-4 tablespoons dried marshmallow root (cut and sifted or powdered)
- 1 quart (4 cups) room-temperature or cold water

*Method:*
1. Place marshmallow root in a glass jar or pitcher
2. Pour room-temperature water over the root
3. Cover and let steep at room temperature for 4-8 hours or overnight (cold infusion extracts maximum mucilage without the tannins that hot water would extract)
4. Strain through a fine mesh strainer or cheesecloth, squeezing to extract all mucilage
5. The resulting liquid should be somewhat thick, slightly viscous, and slightly sweet
6. Refrigerate and use within 24-48 hours (mucilage preparations spoil relatively quickly)
7. Drink 1-2 cups daily

**Preparation -- Hot Tea:**
1. 1-2 tablespoons dried marshmallow root in 8 oz just-boiled water
2. Steep 15-20 minutes, covered
3. Strain and drink warm
4. Hot water extracts less mucilage but more flavonoids and tannins
5. 2-3 cups daily

**Gut-Healing Protocol:**
- 2-3 cups of cold infusion or hot tea daily
- Take 30 minutes before meals
- Can be combined with slippery elm for enhanced demulcent effect
- Duration: 4-12 weeks for gut healing, can be used long-term as a gentle tonic

**Scientific Evidence:**
- A 2010 study in *Journal of Ethnopharmacology* showed marshmallow root extract protected gastric mucosa against ethanol-induced damage in rats (gastroprotective effect).
- The mucilage has been shown to stimulate epithelial cell regeneration in vitro.
- Marshmallow root polysaccharides demonstrate prebiotic activity, supporting Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium growth.
- Anti-inflammatory effects are documented in multiple laboratory studies.
- Like slippery elm, the mechanism is well-understood but large-scale clinical trials specific to marshmallow root for gut healing are lacking.
- The German Commission E approves marshmallow root for irritation of oral and pharyngeal mucosa and associated dry cough, acknowledging its demulcent properties.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Extremely safe.** One of the safest herbs in the entire pharmacopoeia.
- **Drug absorption:** Same concern as slippery elm -- the mucilage may delay drug absorption. Separate by 2 hours.
- **Blood sugar:** May mildly lower blood sugar. Monitor if diabetic.
- **Pregnancy and nursing:** Considered safe.
- **No significant drug interactions** beyond the absorption-delay issue.
- **The only meaningful concern** is ensuring product quality and correct identification (Althaea officinalis, not a look-alike plant).

### 2.5 Aloe Vera Juice (Internal Use)

**Historical Background:**
Aloe vera (Aloe barbadensis miller) has been used internally and externally for at least 6,000 years. Ancient Egyptian, Greek, Roman, Chinese, and Indian civilizations all documented its use. Cleopatra reportedly used it for skin care. Internally, aloe juice has been used for constipation, digestive inflammation, and as a general tonic. The plant contains two distinct medicinal components: the inner gel (clear, mucilaginous) and the latex (yellow layer just under the outer skin, containing aloin -- a potent laxative).

**Important Distinction:**
- **Inner gel/juice:** Mucilaginous, soothing, healing -- this is what we want for gut healing
- **Latex/whole leaf:** Contains anthraquinone laxatives (aloin/barbaloin) that are harsh and can cause cramping, dependency, and electrolyte imbalances. Avoid whole-leaf preparations for gut healing purposes.

**Preparation from Fresh Plant:**
1. Select a mature, thick aloe leaf (outer leaves are oldest and most potent)
2. Cut leaf from plant, stand upright in a glass for 15-20 minutes to let yellow latex drain out (this step is critical -- you want to remove the laxative component)
3. Wash the leaf thoroughly
4. Slice off the flat side to expose the clear inner gel
5. Scoop out the clear gel with a spoon, avoiding any yellow/green tissue
6. Blend 2-4 tablespoons gel with water or juice
7. Consume immediately (fresh aloe gel oxidizes quickly)
8. Or blend a larger batch and refrigerate for up to 1 week

**Using Commercial Aloe Juice:**
- Choose "inner fillet" or "decolorized" aloe vera juice (these have had the anthraquinone latex removed)
- Look for aloe juice with IASC (International Aloe Science Council) certification
- Dose: 2-4 oz, 1-2x daily, on an empty stomach
- Can mix with water, juice, or smoothies

**Gut-Healing Protocol:**
- 2-4 oz pure inner fillet aloe vera juice, 1-2x daily
- Take on an empty stomach, 20-30 minutes before meals
- Duration: 4-12 weeks
- Start with 1 oz daily and increase to assess tolerance
- Can be combined with slippery elm, marshmallow root, and glutamine

**Scientific Evidence:**
- A 2004 randomized, double-blind trial in *Alimentary Pharmacology & Therapeutics* showed aloe vera gel was significantly more effective than placebo for active ulcerative colitis (clinical remission in 30% vs 7% of placebo group).
- Acemannan (a polysaccharide in aloe gel) has demonstrated wound-healing, immune-modulating, and anti-inflammatory properties in multiple studies.
- Aloe gel compounds stimulate fibroblast growth and collagen synthesis, supporting tissue repair.
- Anti-inflammatory effects are mediated through inhibition of cyclooxygenase and prostaglandin pathways.
- A 2016 systematic review confirmed aloe vera's gastroprotective effects.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **The latex component (aloin) is the primary concern.** Whole-leaf aloe preparations contain aloin and can cause: severe diarrhea, abdominal cramping, electrolyte depletion (particularly potassium), dehydration, and with chronic use, dependency (melanosis coli).
- **The FDA withdrew approval** of aloe latex as an over-the-counter laxative in 2002 due to insufficient safety data for this use.
- **Inner gel preparations are much safer** but may still contain trace amounts of latex depending on processing.
- **Pregnancy:** AVOID internal aloe (both gel and latex). Aloin may stimulate uterine contractions.
- **Blood sugar lowering:** Aloe vera juice can lower blood glucose. Monitor if diabetic.
- **Drug interactions:** May enhance effects of diabetes medications, diuretics (additive potassium loss), and digoxin (potassium depletion increases digoxin toxicity).
- **Kidney disease:** Avoid due to potential electrolyte effects.
- **Surgery:** Stop aloe vera juice 2 weeks before surgery (blood sugar and clotting effects).
- **Allergic reactions:** Possible in people allergic to Liliaceae family plants (garlic, onions, tulips).

### 2.6 Cabbage Juice for Ulcers

**Historical Background:**
Dr. Garnett Cheney at Stanford University published landmark studies in the 1940s and 1950s showing that raw cabbage juice dramatically accelerated healing of peptic ulcers. He called the active factor "Vitamin U" (now identified as S-methylmethionine, an amino acid derivative). This work was a significant contribution to gastroenterology, though it was largely overshadowed by the later development of H2 blockers and proton pump inhibitors.

**Active Components:**
- **S-methylmethionine ("Vitamin U"):** Protects and repairs gastric mucosal lining
- **Glutamine:** As discussed in entry 2.2, primary fuel for intestinal epithelial cells (cabbage is one of the richest food sources)
- **Glucosinolates:** Sulfur compounds that have anti-H. pylori activity
- **Vitamin C and K:** Antioxidant and wound-healing support

**Preparation:**
1. Use fresh, green cabbage (red cabbage works too but stains everything)
2. Wash thoroughly
3. Chop roughly and feed through a juicer
4. Approximately 1/2 medium cabbage yields about 16 oz (2 cups) of juice
5. Drink immediately -- the active compounds degrade rapidly when exposed to air
6. If you must store, refrigerate in a sealed glass jar with minimal air space; use within 24 hours
7. Taste: mild, slightly sweet, cabbage-y. Can add apple or carrot juice to improve flavor (but this dilutes the active compounds).

**Cheney Protocol (Original):**
- 1 quart (4 cups/32 oz) fresh raw cabbage juice daily
- Divided into 4-5 servings throughout the day
- Duration: Dr. Cheney's patients healed ulcers in an average of 10.4 days (vs. 37 days for controls)
- Continue for 2-3 weeks even after symptoms resolve
- The juice must be RAW -- cooking destroys the active compounds

**Modified Protocol (More Practical):**
- 8-16 oz fresh raw cabbage juice daily (1-2 glasses)
- Drink on an empty stomach, 30 minutes before meals
- Duration: 2-4 weeks minimum
- Can be combined with aloe vera juice and slippery elm

**Scientific Evidence:**
- **Dr. Cheney's original studies are compelling** though by modern standards would need replication with larger sample sizes and better controls. His 1949 paper in the *Western Journal of Medicine* showed healing of peptic ulcers in 10.4 days vs. 37 days standard.
- S-methylmethionine has been shown to protect gastric mucosa against various insults in animal studies.
- Cabbage juice has demonstrated anti-H. pylori activity in laboratory studies.
- Glutamine content provides direct support for mucosal cell regeneration.
- The glucosinolate sulforaphane (more concentrated in broccoli sprouts) has been extensively studied for gastroprotective and anti-cancer effects.
- **Modern research gap:** Despite the promising historical data, few modern clinical trials have revisited cabbage juice specifically, largely because pharmaceutical solutions became available and more profitable to study.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Generally very safe as a food.**
- **Goitrogens:** Cabbage contains goitrogenic compounds that can interfere with thyroid hormone production when consumed in very large quantities. People with hypothyroidism or iodine deficiency should be cautious with large daily amounts of raw cabbage juice. Cooking inactivates goitrogens, but cooking also destroys the therapeutic compounds.
- **Gas and bloating:** Raw cabbage juice causes significant gas in some people, especially initially. Start with smaller amounts and increase.
- **Blood thinning medications:** Cabbage is high in vitamin K, which counteracts warfarin. If on warfarin, maintain consistent cabbage intake and monitor INR.
- **FODMAPs:** Cabbage is moderate in FODMAPs and may worsen symptoms in some IBS patients.
- **Pesticides:** Use organic cabbage if possible, especially since you're consuming large amounts of raw juice.
- **Pregnancy and nursing:** Safe at food levels.

### 2.7 DGL Licorice (Deglycyrrhizinated Licorice)

**Historical Background:**
Licorice root (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is one of the most widely used herbs in the history of medicine -- it appears in Chinese, Ayurvedic, Greek, Egyptian, and Middle Eastern medical traditions spanning 4,000+ years. It has been used for coughs, digestive complaints, adrenal support, and as a harmonizing herb in herbal formulas. However, regular licorice root contains glycyrrhizin, which can cause serious side effects including hypertension, hypokalemia, and edema. DGL is licorice with the glycyrrhizin removed, retaining the gut-healing benefits while eliminating the most dangerous side effect.

**How DGL Works:**
- Stimulates mucus production in the stomach and intestines (increases the protective mucus layer by up to 50% in some studies)
- Supports prostaglandin synthesis (prostaglandins protect the stomach lining)
- Has anti-inflammatory effects in the GI tract
- May inhibit H. pylori adhesion to gastric cells
- Does NOT suppress stomach acid (unlike PPIs) -- it protects the lining while allowing normal digestion

**Protocol:**
1. **Form:** DGL chewable tablets are preferred (chewing activates the compounds with saliva, which enhances their gut-protective effects)
2. **Dose:** 380-760 mg chewable DGL, 20 minutes before meals and at bedtime
3. **Frequency:** 3-4 times daily
4. **Duration:** 4-16 weeks for active healing; can be used long-term for maintenance
5. **The chewing is important** -- studies that showed benefit used chewable tablets. Simply swallowing DGL capsules may be less effective.
6. **For acute heartburn/stomach pain:** Chew 2 tablets immediately

**Scientific Evidence:**
- A 1968 study in *Gut* showed DGL was as effective as the drug carbenoxolone for healing gastric ulcers, without the side effects.
- Multiple studies in the 1970s-80s demonstrated DGL's effectiveness for both gastric and duodenal ulcers.
- A 1978 study showed DGL healed ulcers at a rate comparable to cimetidine (Tagamet).
- DGL has been shown to increase the number of mucus-secreting cells in the intestinal lining.
- Anti-H. pylori activity demonstrated in vitro.
- **Honest assessment:** Most clinical studies are from the 1970s-80s and are not up to current methodological standards. However, the consistent positive results across multiple studies, combined with the excellent safety profile, make DGL a reasonable first-line approach for peptic concerns.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **DGL is one of the safest remedies in this chapter** because the glycyrrhizin (the compound responsible for most licorice side effects) has been removed.
- **However:** If using regular licorice root (NOT DGL), serious side effects include: hypertension, edema, hypokalemia (low potassium), headaches, and in severe cases, cardiac arrhythmia. Regular licorice should not be used for more than 4-6 weeks or at doses exceeding 3g/day of whole root.
- **DGL side effects** are minimal: occasional mild nausea or headache.
- **Pregnancy:** DGL appears safe. Regular licorice is NOT safe during pregnancy (associated with preterm delivery and developmental concerns).
- **Drug interactions (DGL):** Minimal. **Drug interactions (regular licorice):** Significant -- interacts with blood pressure medications, diuretics, corticosteroids, warfarin, digoxin, and oral contraceptives.
- **Verify your product is truly DGL** (deglycyrrhizinated) and not simply licorice root. The label should specify.

### 2.8 Zinc Carnosine (Zinc-L-Carnosine, ZnC, Polaprezinc)

**Background:**
Zinc carnosine is a chelated compound of zinc and L-carnosine developed in Japan in the 1990s as a pharmaceutical for gastric ulcers (marketed as Polaprezinc/Promac). It is now available as a dietary supplement worldwide. Unlike simple zinc supplements, zinc carnosine adheres to the gastric and intestinal lining, delivering zinc and carnosine directly where they're needed for repair.

**How It Works:**
- Zinc is essential for cell division and wound healing (the rapidly dividing gut lining has high zinc requirements)
- L-carnosine is an antioxidant dipeptide that protects cells from oxidative damage
- The chelated form allows the compound to adhere to damaged mucosal tissue and release its components locally
- Stabilizes mast cells in the gut (reducing histamine release and allergic-type inflammation)
- Supports tight junction integrity

**Protocol:**
1. **Form:** Zinc carnosine capsules (typically 75 mg zinc carnosine per capsule, providing approximately 16 mg elemental zinc and 59 mg L-carnosine)
2. **Dose:** 75 mg zinc carnosine, 2x daily
3. **Timing:** Take between meals (empty stomach or with a small amount of food)
4. **Duration:** 8-12 weeks for gut healing; can be used long-term at lower dose
5. **Combine with:** L-glutamine and DGL for synergistic gut-healing effects

**Scientific Evidence:**
- **This is one of the most evidence-backed gut-healing supplements.** Multiple randomized controlled trials support its use.
- A 2007 double-blind RCT in *Gut* showed zinc carnosine reduced small intestinal permeability caused by indomethacin (an NSAID) by 75%.
- Approved in Japan as a pharmaceutical for gastric ulcers since 1994.
- A 2012 study demonstrated zinc carnosine protected against alcohol-induced gastric damage.
- Shown to accelerate healing of oral mucositis in cancer patients receiving chemotherapy.
- In vitro studies show it stabilizes the gut lining at the cellular level and promotes fibroblast migration to wounds.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Generally well-tolerated at recommended doses.**
- **Copper depletion:** Long-term zinc supplementation (in any form) can deplete copper stores. If using zinc carnosine for more than 8 weeks, consider supplementing with 1-2 mg copper daily.
- **Nausea:** Some people experience mild nausea, especially on an empty stomach.
- **Maximum zinc:** Do not exceed 40 mg elemental zinc daily from all sources (including food and other supplements). Zinc carnosine at standard dosing provides about 32 mg elemental zinc, leaving little room for additional zinc supplements.
- **Drug interactions:** Zinc can reduce absorption of certain antibiotics (tetracyclines, quinolones) and penicillamine. Separate by 2+ hours.
- **Pregnancy:** Zinc is essential during pregnancy but supplemental forms should be discussed with a healthcare provider.
- **Kidney disease:** Use with caution due to altered mineral metabolism.

### 2.9 Colostrum Supplementation

**Background:**
Colostrum is the first milk produced by mammals in the first 24-72 hours after birth. Bovine (cow) colostrum supplements have been used for gut healing since the 1990s. Colostrum is extraordinarily rich in immunoglobulins (particularly IgA, IgG, IgM), growth factors, cytokines, and lactoferrin. It is sometimes called "nature's first supplement."

**Key Components:**
- **Immunoglobulins (especially IgA):** Coat the intestinal lining and neutralize pathogens
- **Lactoferrin:** Antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, supports iron metabolism
- **Growth factors (IGF-1, TGF-beta):** Stimulate intestinal cell growth and repair
- **Proline-rich polypeptides:** Regulate immune function (can upregulate or downregulate as needed)
- **Lysozyme:** Antimicrobial enzyme

**Protocol:**
1. **Form:** Bovine colostrum powder or capsules (from pasture-raised, grass-fed cows)
2. **Dose:**
   - Powder: 1-2 tablespoons (10-20g) daily
   - Capsules: 1000-3000 mg, 2-3x daily
3. **Timing:** On an empty stomach, 30 minutes before meals (protects the immunoglobulins from degradation by stomach acid when mixed with food)
4. **Duration:** 8-12 weeks for gut healing; can be used long-term
5. **Quality matters:** Look for colostrum collected within the first 6 hours after calving (highest immunoglobulin content), low-heat processed (preserves bioactive compounds), from hormone-free, antibiotic-free cattle
6. **Storage:** Refrigerate after opening; keep powder dry

**Scientific Evidence:**
- A 2017 RCT in *Nutrients* showed bovine colostrum supplementation reduced intestinal permeability and improved gut barrier function.
- Multiple studies show colostrum can prevent and treat NSAID-induced gut damage.
- A 2011 study found colostrum supplementation reduced exercise-induced intestinal permeability by 80% compared to placebo.
- Colostrum immunoglobulins have been shown to neutralize E. coli, Salmonella, Rotavirus, Cryptosporidium, and H. pylori in laboratory studies.
- Lactoferrin specifically has anti-H. pylori activity and is studied as an adjunct to standard H. pylori eradication therapy.
- Used clinically for HIV-associated diarrhea with documented benefit.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Dairy allergy:** Colostrum is a dairy product. People with true cow's milk protein allergy should avoid it. Most lactose-intolerant people tolerate colostrum (it contains minimal lactose), but caution is warranted.
- **IGF-1 concerns:** Colostrum contains insulin-like growth factor 1. Theoretical concern about promoting growth of existing cancers. People with active cancer or history of hormone-sensitive cancers should consult their oncologist before use.
- **Prion disease:** Extremely remote theoretical risk from bovine products. Source from countries with strict BSE (mad cow disease) surveillance (US, Australia, New Zealand).
- **Pregnancy and nursing:** Generally considered safe but discuss with provider due to growth factor content.
- **Quality variation:** Enormous variation between brands. Third-party testing and transparent sourcing matter significantly.
- **Cost:** High-quality colostrum is expensive ($40-80/month at therapeutic doses).

### 2.10 Gelatin and Collagen for Gut Healing

**Background:**
Gelatin (cooked collagen) and hydrolyzed collagen peptides provide specific amino acids -- glycine, proline, hydroxyproline, and glutamine -- that are the building blocks of connective tissue, including the intestinal mucosal lining. This is essentially the principle behind bone broth (entry 2.1) in concentrated, convenient supplement form.

**The Difference:**
- **Gelatin:** Dissolves in hot water, gels when cooled. Contains longer protein chains. Provides a coating/soothing effect in the gut similar to slippery elm.
- **Hydrolyzed collagen (collagen peptides):** Pre-broken into smaller peptides for easier absorption. Dissolves in hot or cold liquid. Does not gel. More bioavailable but less coating effect.
- **For gut healing:** Gelatin may be preferable (the gelling/coating effect adds demulcent benefit). For general supplementation, collagen peptides are more convenient.

**Protocol:**
1. **Gelatin:** 1-2 tablespoons (10-20g) daily, dissolved in hot liquid (broth, tea, hot water)
2. **Collagen peptides:** 10-20g daily, mixed into any liquid
3. **Timing:** Can be taken any time; some practitioners prefer on an empty stomach
4. **Duration:** Ongoing (this is essentially a food/protein supplement)
5. **Source:** Grass-fed, pasture-raised bovine gelatin/collagen, or wild-caught marine collagen

**Making Gut-Healing Gelatin Gummies:**
1. Heat 1 cup bone broth or herbal tea (not boiling)
2. Sprinkle 2 tablespoons grass-fed gelatin powder over the surface
3. Let bloom 5 minutes
4. Stir until completely dissolved
5. Add 1 tablespoon honey and 1 teaspoon fresh ginger juice (optional)
6. Pour into silicone molds
7. Refrigerate until set (2-3 hours)
8. Eat 4-6 gummies daily

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Glycine (abundant in gelatin/collagen) has documented anti-inflammatory effects in the gut, as discussed in the bone broth entry.
- A 2003 study showed gelatin tannate reduced duration and severity of acute diarrhea in children.
- Collagen peptide supplementation has been shown to improve skin, joint, and connective tissue health in multiple trials (the gut lining is also connective tissue).
- A 2017 study found specific collagen peptides reduced symptoms of inflammatory bowel disease in a mouse model.
- **The evidence is stronger for the individual amino acids (glycine, glutamine, proline) than for gelatin/collagen as whole supplements for gut healing specifically.**

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Very safe as a food supplement.**
- **Allergies:** Rare but possible (bovine, porcine, or marine source allergies).
- **Heavy metals:** Some collagen supplements have been found to contain trace heavy metals (particularly from marine sources). Choose brands with third-party heavy metal testing.
- **Histamine:** Collagen and gelatin are moderately high in histamine. People with histamine intolerance may have issues.
- **Calcium oxalate:** High-dose collagen supplementation has been theoretically linked to increased oxalate production (relevant for kidney stone formers). Evidence is preliminary.
- **Drug interactions:** Essentially none at food-level doses.
- **Vegan/vegetarian:** No plant-based equivalent. Supplementing with the individual amino acids (glycine, L-glutamine, L-proline) is an alternative approach.

### 2.11 Fermented Foods Protocol

**Historical Background:**
Every traditional food culture developed fermented foods: sauerkraut and pickles in Europe, kimchi in Korea, miso/natto/pickled vegetables in Japan, kefir and yogurt in the Caucasus and Middle East, kvass in Russia, injera in Ethiopia, dosa/idli in India. Fermentation was originally a preservation method, but the resulting probiotic-rich foods are now understood to be potent supporters of gut health. The modern gut microbiome research revolution has vindicated what traditional cultures practiced intuitively for millennia.

**Why Fermented Foods Work:**
- **Live probiotic bacteria:** Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Streptococcus thermophilus, Saccharomyces, and others
- **Postbiotics:** Metabolites produced during fermentation (short-chain fatty acids, vitamins, enzymes) that benefit the host even without live bacteria
- **Pre-digestion:** Fermentation breaks down anti-nutrients (phytates, lectins), increases vitamin bioavailability, and makes nutrients more accessible
- **Organic acids:** Lactic acid, acetic acid, and others that support a healthy gut pH
- **Diversity:** Different fermented foods contain different species, supporting microbiome diversity

**Protocol -- Building a Fermented Food Practice:**

*Phase 1 (Week 1-2) -- Introduction:*
- Start with 1-2 tablespoons of one fermented food daily
- Best starting options: raw sauerkraut, plain yogurt (with live cultures), or water kefir
- Take with or immediately after meals
- Watch for GI response (some bloating is normal initially)

*Phase 2 (Week 3-4) -- Increase:*
- Increase to 1/4 cup, 1-2x daily
- Introduce a second type of fermented food
- Vary the types: alternate between different fermented vegetables, dairy ferments, and beverages

*Phase 3 (Week 5+) -- Maintenance:*
- Include 1-3 servings of fermented foods daily
- Rotate through as many different types as possible for microbial diversity
- Target: at least 3-4 different fermented foods per week

**Making Basic Sauerkraut (The Gateway Ferment):**

*Ingredients:*
- 1 medium head green cabbage (about 2 lbs)
- 1 tablespoon non-iodized sea salt (approximately 2% of cabbage weight)
- Optional: caraway seeds, juniper berries, or other spices

*Method:*
1. Remove outer leaves of cabbage (save 1-2 clean ones)
2. Quarter, core, and shred cabbage finely
3. Place in a large bowl and sprinkle with salt
4. Massage and squeeze the cabbage firmly with clean hands for 5-10 minutes until it releases significant liquid (brine). This is the most important step -- the cabbage must produce its own brine.
5. Pack tightly into a clean glass jar (wide-mouth quart jars work well), pressing down firmly after each handful to eliminate air pockets
6. The brine should rise above the cabbage. If not, add a small amount of 2% salt water (1 tsp salt per cup water)
7. Place a reserved cabbage leaf on top and press down to keep shredded cabbage submerged
8. Weight it down (a smaller jar filled with water works, or fermentation weights)
9. Cover loosely (not sealed airtight -- CO2 needs to escape). Use a cloth secured with a rubber band, a loose lid, or a fermentation airlock lid.
10. Place in a cool area (65-75F is ideal) away from direct sunlight
11. Check daily and press cabbage below brine if it rises
12. Begin tasting after 3-5 days. Fermentation takes 1-4 weeks depending on temperature and preference (longer = more sour, more diverse bacteria)
13. When flavor is to your liking, seal and refrigerate (cold slows fermentation nearly to a stop)
14. Keeps 6+ months in refrigerator

**Making Milk Kefir:**

*Ingredients:*
- Milk kefir grains (not powdered starter -- live grains produce more diverse cultures)
- Whole milk (raw if available, otherwise pasteurized but NOT ultra-pasteurized/UHT)

*Method:*
1. Place 1-2 tablespoons kefir grains in a clean glass jar
2. Add 2 cups milk
3. Cover loosely (cloth + rubber band or loose lid)
4. Let sit at room temperature 18-24 hours (shorter in warm weather, longer in cold)
5. The milk will thicken and become slightly tangy
6. Strain through a plastic (not metal) strainer to remove grains
7. The strained liquid is kefir -- drink as is, add to smoothies, or use in cooking
8. Return grains to jar and add fresh milk to start the next batch
9. Grains grow over time; share extras or eat them
10. Kefir contains 30-50 different bacterial and yeast species (far more diverse than yogurt)

**Scientific Evidence:**
- A landmark 2021 Stanford study in *Cell* showed that a high-fermented-food diet (6+ servings/day for 10 weeks) significantly increased gut microbiome diversity and reduced markers of systemic inflammation, including decreases in 19 inflammatory proteins.
- Regular fermented food consumption is associated with reduced incidence of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers in epidemiological studies.
- Kefir specifically has been shown to improve lactose digestion, reduce inflammation, and modulate immune function in multiple clinical trials.
- Sauerkraut and kimchi provide not only probiotics but also prebiotics (fiber from the vegetables).
- Traditional yogurt consumption is one of the most consistent dietary factors associated with gut health across populations worldwide.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Histamine:** Fermented foods are high in histamine and other biogenic amines (tyramine, putrescine). People with histamine intolerance may experience headaches, flushing, hives, nasal congestion, rapid heartbeat, or digestive upset. Start with small amounts and monitor.
- **SIBO:** People with small intestinal bacterial overgrowth may initially worsen with fermented foods. Work with a practitioner.
- **Immunocompromised:** People with severely compromised immune systems should consult their doctor, as live bacteria (even beneficial ones) can theoretically cause infection in severely immunosuppressed individuals.
- **Tyramine interactions:** Fermented foods are high in tyramine. People taking MAO inhibitors (a class of antidepressant) must avoid or severely limit fermented foods due to risk of hypertensive crisis.
- **Food safety:** Properly fermented vegetables are actually safer than raw vegetables (the acid environment kills pathogens). However, failed fermentation (visible mold, foul odors, slimy texture) should be discarded.
- **Start slowly:** Introducing too many fermented foods too quickly can cause significant gas, bloating, and digestive upset as the gut microbiome adjusts.

### 2.12 Elimination Diet Approach

**Background:**
The elimination diet is not a remedy per se but a diagnostic and therapeutic protocol used to identify food sensitivities and reduce gut inflammation. It is the gold standard for identifying food-triggered symptoms, more reliable than IgG food sensitivity blood tests (which have poor clinical correlation). First formalized by Dr. Albert Rowe in the 1920s and refined by many practitioners since.

**How It Works:**
By removing the most common inflammatory foods for a period, the gut has time to heal and inflammation subsides. When foods are reintroduced one at a time, reactions become obvious, revealing which foods are personally problematic.

**Protocol:**

*Phase 1 -- Elimination (21-30 days):*

Remove ALL of the following:
- **Gluten** (wheat, barley, rye, spelt, kamut, and most oats)
- **Dairy** (all milk, cheese, yogurt, butter, cream, whey, casein)
- **Eggs**
- **Soy** (tofu, tempeh, soy sauce, soy lecithin, edamame)
- **Corn** (corn, cornstarch, corn syrup, corn oil)
- **Peanuts** (and often all legumes)
- **Sugar and artificial sweeteners** (refined sugar, high fructose corn syrup, aspartame, sucralose)
- **Alcohol**
- **Caffeine** (taper off over 3-4 days to avoid withdrawal headache)
- **Processed foods** (anything with ingredient lists longer than 5 items)
- **Common chemical additives** (MSG, sulfites, nitrates)

**Eat freely:**
- All vegetables (except corn and nightshades in stricter versions)
- All fruits (moderate amounts)
- All meats, poultry, and fish (unprocessed, organic preferred)
- Rice (white or brown)
- Sweet potatoes
- Olive oil, coconut oil, avocado oil
- Nuts and seeds (except peanuts)
- Herbs and spices
- Bone broth
- Herbal teas

*Phase 2 -- Reintroduction (6-8 weeks):*

Reintroduce ONE food group every 3-4 days:
1. **Day 1:** Eat the test food 2-3 times that day
2. **Days 2-3:** Return to elimination diet and observe for symptoms
3. **If NO reaction:** That food is likely safe. Keep it out of diet until all testing is complete.
4. **If reaction occurs** (bloating, headache, fatigue, skin changes, joint pain, mood changes, digestive symptoms): That food is a trigger. Remove it and wait until symptoms fully resolve before testing the next food.
5. **Suggested reintroduction order:** Eggs first (common and easy to test), then dairy, then gluten, then soy, then corn, then the others.

**Duration:** The elimination phase must be at minimum 21 days. Many practitioners recommend 30 days for thorough gut calming. The entire process (elimination + reintroduction) takes 2-3 months.

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Elimination diets are well-supported in medical literature for identifying food triggers in IBS, eczema, migraines, and other conditions.
- A 2006 study in *Gut* showed elimination diet was effective for identifying food triggers in 50-75% of IBS patients.
- Multiple studies support the link between specific food triggers and gut inflammation, and elimination diets as a valid diagnostic approach.
- The low-FODMAP diet (a specific type of elimination diet) has strong evidence for IBS symptom management.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Nutritional adequacy:** Ensure adequate caloric intake and nutrition during elimination. This is not a starvation diet.
- **Caffeine withdrawal:** Taper off caffeine over 3-4 days rather than stopping abruptly (withdrawal headaches can be severe).
- **Eating disorders:** People with a history of eating disorders should approach elimination diets with extreme caution and practitioner support, as the restriction can trigger disordered eating patterns.
- **Children and pregnant women:** Require modified, less restrictive versions with nutritional supervision.
- **Duration:** Do not stay on the elimination phase indefinitely. The goal is to identify triggers and then reintroduce as many foods as possible.
- **Social/psychological impact:** Highly restrictive diets are socially challenging and can cause anxiety around food. Have a clear endpoint and plan.

### 2.13 Prebiotic Foods and Preparation

**Background:**
Prebiotics are non-digestible food components (primarily specific types of fiber) that selectively feed beneficial gut bacteria. They are the "food for probiotics." The concept was formalized by Gibson and Roberfroid in 1995. While probiotic supplements introduce bacteria from outside, prebiotics nourish and expand the beneficial bacteria already living in your gut -- a complementary and arguably more sustainable approach.

**Key Prebiotic Compounds:**
- **Inulin:** Found in chicory root, Jerusalem artichoke, garlic, onion, leeks, asparagus, dandelion greens
- **Fructooligosaccharides (FOS):** Found in bananas, onions, garlic, asparagus
- **Galactooligosaccharides (GOS):** Found in legumes, certain root vegetables
- **Resistant starch:** Found in cooked-and-cooled potatoes, cooked-and-cooled rice, green bananas, plantains
- **Pectin:** Found in apples, citrus peel, berries
- **Beta-glucan:** Found in oats, mushrooms, seaweed

**Prebiotic-Rich Recipes:**

*Resistant Starch Rice:*
1. Cook white rice in a rice cooker with 1 teaspoon of coconut oil per cup of dry rice
2. After cooking, refrigerate for at least 12 hours (cooling converts a significant portion of starch to resistant starch)
3. Reheat and eat -- the resistant starch remains even after reheating
4. This simple technique reduces digestible calories by about 50-60% and creates a potent prebiotic food

*Jerusalem Artichoke (Sunchoke) Soup:*
1. 1 lb Jerusalem artichokes, scrubbed and chopped (no need to peel)
2. 1 onion, diced
3. 3 cloves garlic, minced
4. 4 cups bone broth
5. Salt and pepper to taste
6. Saute onion and garlic in olive oil, add sunchokes and broth, simmer 20 minutes, blend smooth
7. Sunchokes are the highest food source of inulin (~75% of dry weight)
8. Warning: Start with small portions. Jerusalem artichokes can cause extreme gas in people not accustomed to high inulin foods ("fartichokes" is their common nickname for a reason).

*Prebiotic Banana Smoothie:*
1. 1 green (unripe) banana or green banana flour (2 tbsp)
2. 1/2 cup kefir or yogurt
3. 1 tablespoon ground flaxseed
4. 1 teaspoon raw honey
5. 1/2 cup frozen berries
6. Blend and drink
7. The greener the banana, the higher the resistant starch content

**Protocol for Increasing Prebiotic Intake:**
1. **Week 1:** Add 1 serving of prebiotic food daily (a banana, garlic in cooking, oats for breakfast)
2. **Week 2:** Increase to 2-3 servings daily, varying sources
3. **Week 3+:** Aim for 5+ grams of prebiotic fiber daily from diverse sources
4. **Gradual increase is essential** to avoid gas and bloating as gut bacteria adjust
5. Drink plenty of water (fiber needs water to function properly)

**Scientific Evidence:**
- A 2017 meta-analysis in *British Journal of Nutrition* confirmed that prebiotic supplementation significantly increases Bifidobacterium populations in the gut.
- Resistant starch specifically increases butyrate production (a short-chain fatty acid that is the primary fuel for colon cells and has anti-inflammatory effects).
- The cooked-and-cooled rice/potato resistant starch technique has been validated in multiple studies.
- Prebiotic supplementation has been shown to improve mineral absorption (calcium, magnesium), support immune function, and reduce markers of systemic inflammation.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Gas and bloating:** The most common side effect of increasing prebiotic intake. Start slowly.
- **SIBO:** People with small intestinal bacterial overgrowth may worsen with prebiotic supplementation (the prebiotics feed bacteria in the wrong location). Treat SIBO first.
- **FODMAPs:** Many prebiotic foods are high in FODMAPs. People with FODMAP sensitivity need to choose low-FODMAP prebiotic sources (green bananas, cooked-and-cooled potatoes, oats).
- **IBD flares:** During active IBD flares, high-fiber foods (including prebiotics) may worsen symptoms. Use during remission phases.

### 2.14 GAPS Diet Principles (Gut and Psychology Syndrome)

**Background:**
The GAPS (Gut and Psychology Syndrome) diet was developed by Dr. Natasha Campbell-McBride, a neurologist and nutritionist, based on the Specific Carbohydrate Diet (SCD) pioneered by Dr. Sidney Haas and later popularized by Elaine Gottschall in *Breaking the Vicious Cycle*. The GAPS protocol is designed to heal the gut lining, rebalance the microbiome, and reduce systemic inflammation that Dr. Campbell-McBride links to neurological and psychological conditions including autism, ADHD, depression, and schizophrenia.

**Core Principles:**
1. Remove all grains, refined carbohydrates, sugar, starchy vegetables, and most dairy
2. Heal the gut lining with bone broth, fermented foods, and specific supplements
3. Restore beneficial gut flora with probiotics and fermented foods
4. Reintroduce foods gradually as the gut heals

**The GAPS Introduction Diet (6 Stages):**

*Stage 1 (3-7 days):*
- Homemade meat/bone broth (drink all day)
- Well-boiled vegetables in broth (carrots, onions, squash, zucchini, broccoli)
- Probiotic foods: start with 1-2 tsp juice from homemade sauerkraut
- Ginger or chamomile tea with honey between meals
- Boiled meats from the broth

*Stage 2 (add):*
- Raw organic egg yolks (stirred into soup/broth)
- Casseroles and stews with meat and vegetables
- Increase fermented foods (fermented vegetable juice, homemade yogurt if tolerated)
- Ghee (start with 1 tsp)

*Stage 3 (add):*
- Ripe avocado
- Pancakes made from nut butter + eggs + squash
- Scrambled eggs (cooked in ghee or animal fat)
- Fermented vegetables (not just the juice)
- Probiotic supplementation begins

*Stage 4 (add):*
- Roasted and grilled meats
- Olive oil (cold-pressed, on food, not heated)
- Freshly pressed vegetable juice (start with carrot)
- Nut flour baked goods (almond flour bread)

*Stage 5 (add):*
- Raw vegetables (start with soft: lettuce, peeled cucumber, tomato)
- Pressed fruit juices
- Small amounts of raw fruit (start with just apple)

*Stage 6 (add):*
- Raw apple, other raw fruits
- Increase honey
- Introduce baked goods with dried fruit as sweetener

**Full GAPS Diet (maintain for 1.5-2 years):**
After completing the introduction stages, the Full GAPS diet allows: all meats, fish, eggs, fermented dairy (yogurt, kefir, sour cream aged 24+ hours), all vegetables, all fruits, nuts and seeds, honey, healthy fats, and specific legumes (navy beans, lentils, split peas -- soaked and well-cooked).

**Still excluded on Full GAPS:** All grains, sugar, starchy vegetables (potatoes, parsnips, yams), corn, soy, all processed food.

**Scientific Evidence:**
- The Specific Carbohydrate Diet (GAPS's parent diet) has been studied in IBD patients with promising results. A 2016 study in *Journal of Clinical Gastroenterology* showed SCD was effective for pediatric Crohn's disease.
- The gut-brain axis (the core concept behind GAPS) is now well-established in mainstream neuroscience and gastroenterology.
- The individual components (bone broth, fermented foods, eliminating processed foods and refined sugar) all have supporting evidence.
- **However:** The GAPS diet specifically has not been tested in rigorous clinical trials. The claims about treating autism, ADHD, and other neurological conditions through the GAPS diet remain unproven.
- Some gastroenterologists express concern about the extreme restriction and lack of controlled evidence.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Extremely restrictive.** Risk of nutritional deficiencies (particularly calcium, vitamin D, B vitamins from grains) if not carefully planned.
- **Weight loss:** Significant caloric restriction, especially in introduction phases. Monitor weight.
- **Children:** The introduction diet is very restrictive for children. Pediatric nutritional supervision is strongly recommended.
- **Eating disorders:** High risk of triggering or worsening disordered eating patterns. Use with caution in susceptible individuals.
- **Social isolation:** Such restrictive diets can cause significant social difficulty (eating out, social events, family meals).
- **Duration:** 1.5-2 years is a very long commitment. Drop-out rates are high.
- **Detox symptoms:** The diet often causes initial worsening of symptoms (headache, fatigue, irritability) as the body adjusts. This can be severe and may require slowing the progression through stages.
- **Not a substitute for medical care** for serious conditions like autism, ADHD, or IBD.

### 2.15 Making Yogurt at Home

**Historical Background:**
Yogurt is among the oldest fermented foods, originating in the Middle East and Central Asia at least 5,000 years ago. Legend attributes its discovery to milk stored in animal stomachs (which contain natural bacterial cultures) during nomadic travel. The Bulgarian physician Stamen Grigorov first identified Lactobacillus bulgaricus as the fermenting organism in 1905, and Nobel laureate Elie Metchnikoff subsequently popularized yogurt consumption for longevity based on observations of long-lived Bulgarian peasants.

**Why Homemade:**
Commercial yogurt typically contains 2-3 bacterial species, is fermented for only 4-6 hours (maximizing shelf life rather than probiotic potency), and often contains sugar, thickeners, and other additives. Homemade yogurt fermented for 24 hours (as recommended in the GAPS and SCD protocols) is essentially lactose-free (bacteria consume the lactose) and contains significantly more live bacteria.

**24-Hour Yogurt Recipe:**

*Ingredients:*
- 1 quart (4 cups) whole milk (organic, grass-fed preferred; NOT ultra-pasteurized)
- 1-2 tablespoons plain yogurt with live cultures as starter (or a freeze-dried yogurt starter culture)

*Equipment:*
- Heavy-bottomed pot
- Thermometer (critical for proper temperature control)
- Glass jars or ceramic container
- Method to maintain 100-110F for 24 hours (yogurt maker, oven with light on, heating pad wrapped in towels, insulated cooler with a jar of hot water, or Instant Pot yogurt setting)

*Method:*
1. Heat milk to 180F in a heavy pot, stirring occasionally (this denatures whey proteins and creates a thicker yogurt)
2. Hold at 180F for 5-10 minutes (optional but improves texture)
3. Cool to 100-110F (use ice bath for speed, or let sit -- but monitor temperature)
4. Add starter culture (2 tablespoons per quart, stirred in gently but thoroughly)
5. Pour into clean glass jars
6. Maintain temperature between 100-110F for 24 hours (this extended fermentation consumes nearly all lactose and maximizes bacterial count)
7. The yogurt is done when it has set and is slightly tangy
8. Refrigerate for at least 4 hours before eating (sets further and stops fermentation)
9. Reserve 2 tablespoons as starter for your next batch
10. Keeps 2 weeks refrigerated

**Troubleshooting:**
- **Too thin:** Milk was ultra-pasteurized (UHT), temperature was too low during fermentation, or starter was inactive. Try a new starter.
- **Grainy texture:** Temperature was too high during fermentation or milk was heated too quickly.
- **Too sour:** Fermented too long or at too high temperature. Reduce time or lower temperature slightly.
- **Whey separation:** Normal. Stir back in or strain through cheesecloth for Greek-style yogurt.

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Yogurt consumption is one of the most consistently health-promoting dietary factors in epidemiological literature, associated with reduced risk of type 2 diabetes, obesity, cardiovascular disease, and improved gut health.
- A 2014 meta-analysis in *BMC Medicine* found yogurt consumption significantly associated with reduced type 2 diabetes risk.
- Live-culture yogurt has demonstrated benefits for antibiotic-associated diarrhea, lactose maldigestion, and gut barrier function in RCTs.
- The 24-hour fermentation technique is supported by SCD/GAPS practitioners but has not been independently studied versus standard fermentation times for clinical outcomes.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Dairy allergy:** Yogurt contains milk proteins (casein, whey). Not safe for milk protein allergy (distinct from lactose intolerance).
- **Contamination:** If yogurt has off smells, pink/orange/green discoloration, or slimy texture, discard -- this indicates contamination with unwanted organisms.
- **Histamine:** Yogurt is moderately high in histamine. 24-hour yogurt may be higher than commercial yogurt.
- **Temperature control:** Maintaining proper temperature is critical for safety. Below 90F, pathogenic bacteria can outcompete yogurt cultures. Above 120F, the cultures die.
- **Immunocompromised:** Generally safe, but discuss with healthcare provider.

### 2.16 Water Kefir

**Historical Background:**
Water kefir (also called tibicos, Japanese water crystals, or sugar kefir) is a fermented beverage made with water kefir grains -- not actual grains, but a SCOBY (symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast) that looks like small translucent crystals. The origin is debated: some sources trace it to Mexico (found on the pads of the Opuntia cactus), others to Tibet. Water kefir is dairy-free, making it an important probiotic option for those who cannot consume dairy.

**Recipe:**

*Ingredients:*
- 3-4 tablespoons water kefir grains
- 1/4 cup organic cane sugar (this feeds the grains, not you -- most sugar is consumed during fermentation)
- 1 quart filtered water (chlorinated water will damage the grains)
- Optional: 1 dried fig or 2 tablespoons dried fruit (provides minerals that nourish the grains)
- Optional: 1/8 teaspoon unrefined mineral-rich salt (trace minerals support grain health)

*First Fermentation:*
1. Dissolve sugar in warm water, then cool to room temperature
2. Add water kefir grains and dried fruit (if using)
3. Cover loosely (cloth + rubber band -- must breathe but keep insects out)
4. Ferment at room temperature 24-48 hours (24 hours in warm weather, 48 in cold)
5. The water will become slightly cloudy, mildly sweet-tart, and possibly lightly effervescent
6. Strain out grains (and fruit)
7. The strained liquid is water kefir -- drink as is, or proceed to second fermentation

*Second Fermentation (for carbonation and flavor):*
1. Pour strained water kefir into swing-top glass bottles, filling to 1 inch from top
2. Add flavoring: 2-3 tablespoons fruit juice per bottle, or fresh fruit pieces, or ginger slices
3. Seal tightly
4. Leave at room temperature 12-48 hours (the sealed environment builds carbonation)
5. **IMPORTANT:** "Burp" bottles every 12 hours (briefly open the cap to release pressure). Water kefir can build significant carbonation -- bottles can explode if left too long.
6. Refrigerate to slow fermentation and store
7. Serve cold

*Grain Care:*
- After straining, immediately start a new batch of sugar water for the grains
- Grains multiply over time; share extras or compost
- If taking a break, store grains in sugar water in the refrigerator (change water weekly)
- Grains are translucent/white. If they turn dark or develop off odors, the culture may be contaminated.

**Scientific Evidence:**
- Water kefir contains a diverse community of Lactobacillus, Leuconostoc, Acetobacter, and yeasts (Saccharomyces, Kluyveromyces).
- A 2016 study in *Frontiers in Microbiology* characterized water kefir microbiome and confirmed diverse probiotic species.
- Less studied than milk kefir or yogurt, but the probiotic principles are similar.
- Water kefir provides probiotics without dairy, making it a valuable option for dairy-free gut healing.

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Alcohol content:** Water kefir contains 0.5-2% alcohol depending on fermentation time and conditions. Extended fermentation produces more alcohol. Relevant for those avoiding all alcohol.
- **Sugar content:** While much of the sugar is consumed during fermentation, some remains. Diabetics should monitor.
- **Carbonation/explosion risk:** Take the burping instruction seriously. Fermenting in sealed glass under warm conditions has caused bottles to shatter.
- **Histamine and tyramine:** Like all fermented beverages, contains biogenic amines.
- **Safe for most people** including children (in moderation, considering alcohol content).
