## Understanding Tincture-Making

### What Is a Tincture?

A tincture is a concentrated liquid herbal extract made by soaking plant material in a solvent -- most commonly alcohol (ethanol) -- to extract the active chemical constituents. Alcohol serves as both the extraction medium and the preservative, giving tinctures an exceptionally long shelf life of 3-5 years or more when stored properly.

### The Two Methods

**Folk Method (Simpler's Method)**
The folk method is the traditional, intuitive approach used by home herbalists for centuries. No scales or precise measurements are needed.

- Fresh herbs: Fill jar 2/3 to 3/4 full with chopped plant material, cover completely with alcohol
- Dried herbs: Fill jar 1/3 to 1/2 full with plant material, cover completely with alcohol
- Macerate (soak) for 4-6 weeks, shaking daily
- Strain through cheesecloth, bottle in dark glass

**Weight-to-Volume Method (Pharmacopeial Method)**
The precise, reproducible method used by professional herbalists and pharmacies.

- Fresh herbs: 1:2 ratio (1 gram herb to 2 mL alcohol), using 95% alcohol
- Dried herbs: 1:5 ratio (1 gram herb to 5 mL alcohol), using 40-65% alcohol
- The alcohol percentage varies by herb depending on which constituents need extraction

### Choosing Your Alcohol

| Alcohol Type | Proof / % ABV | Best For |
|-------------|--------------|----------|
| 80-proof vodka | 40% ABV | Most dried herbs, general-purpose tinctures |
| 100-proof vodka | 50% ABV | Herbs needing slightly stronger extraction |
| 151-proof grain alcohol (Everclear) | 75.5% ABV | Resins, fresh juicy herbs |
| 190-proof grain alcohol (Everclear) | 95% ABV | Fresh plant tinctures, resinous herbs, gums |
| Brandy (80-proof) | 40% ABV | Palatable tinctures, especially for children's formulas (diluted) |

**Key principle:** The alcohol percentage on the bottle is half the proof number. 80-proof vodka = 40% alcohol, 60% water. Both the alcohol and water fractions extract different compounds, so the ratio matters.

### General Tincture-Making Procedure

1. **Prepare your herb**: Chop fresh herbs finely or use coarsely ground dried herbs
2. **Place in jar**: Use a clean, dry mason jar with a tight-fitting lid
3. **Add alcohol**: Pour alcohol over herbs until they are covered by at least 1-2 inches
4. **Label**: Date, herb name, alcohol type and percentage, ratio used
5. **Macerate**: Store in a cool, dark place for 4-6 weeks
6. **Shake daily**: Agitate the jar once daily to improve extraction
7. **Strain**: Pour through cheesecloth or fine mesh strainer, squeezing out all liquid
8. **Bottle**: Transfer to dark amber or cobalt glass dropper bottles
9. **Label final product**: Herb, date, alcohol %, ratio, dosage notes

### Storage

- Dark glass bottles (amber or cobalt blue)
- Cool, dark location away from direct sunlight and heat
- Shelf life: 3-5+ years for alcohol-based tinctures
- Glycerin-based tinctures: 1-2 years
- Vinegar-based tinctures: 6-12 months

## 1. Echinacea Tincture (Echinacea purpurea / E. angustifolia)

**Category:** Immune support

### Ingredients
- 1 cup fresh echinacea root, leaves, and/or flowers (or 1/2 cup dried root)
- 2 cups 80-proof vodka (or 50% ABV for dried root; 95% ABV for fresh root)

### Preparation
1. If using fresh root, scrub clean and chop into small pieces (no need to peel)
2. If using dried root, coarsely grind or break into small pieces
3. Place herb in a quart mason jar, filling 1/2 to 2/3 full
4. Cover completely with vodka, leaving 1 inch of headspace
5. Seal tightly and label with date, herb, and alcohol used
6. Store in a cool, dark place for 6 weeks, shaking daily
7. Strain through cheesecloth, squeezing thoroughly
8. Bottle in dark glass dropper bottles

**Weight-to-volume:** Fresh root 1:2 in 95% alcohol; dried root 1:5 in 50% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass bottles, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Immune stimulant at onset of colds and flu
- Upper respiratory infections
- Wound healing (topical application)
- General immune support during illness season

### Scientific Evidence
- **Moderately supported.** A 2007 Lancet meta-analysis found echinacea reduced the odds of developing a cold by 58% and reduced cold duration by 1.4 days.
- A 2024 meta-analysis found echinacea significantly reduced monthly respiratory tract infection occurrence (risk ratio 0.68) and reduced the need for antibiotic therapy.
- However, a 2014 Cochrane review found that while "some Echinacea products are more effective than a placebo for treating colds, the overall evidence for clinically relevant treatment effects is weak."
- The variation in study results is partly due to the many different species, plant parts, and extraction methods used across trials.
- Active compounds include alkamides, caffeic acid derivatives (cichoric acid, echinacoside), and polysaccharides.

### Dosage
- **Acute illness:** 2-4 mL (40-80 drops) every 2-3 hours for the first day, then 3 times daily for up to 10 days
- **Prevention:** 2.5 mL three times daily during cold season (not recommended for continuous long-term use beyond 8-12 weeks)

### Safety & Contraindications
- Generally well-tolerated; safety profile noninferior to placebo in clinical trials
- **Contraindicated** in autoimmune conditions (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis) due to immune-stimulating effects
- Theoretical concern with immunosuppressive drugs (cyclosporine, corticosteroids)
- Allergic reactions possible, especially in people allergic to the daisy family (Asteraceae)
- Not recommended for continuous use beyond 8-12 weeks
- Safe for short-term use during pregnancy according to some herbalists, but consult a provider

## 2. Elderberry Tincture (Sambucus nigra)

**Category:** Immune support, antiviral

### Ingredients
- 1 cup dried elderberries (or 2 cups fresh ripe berries)
- 2 cups 80-proof vodka
- Optional: 1 cinnamon stick, 5 whole cloves, 1 tablespoon fresh ginger (sliced)

### Preparation
1. Place dried elderberries in a quart mason jar (fill about 1/3)
2. Add optional spices if desired
3. Cover with vodka, ensuring berries are submerged by at least 1 inch
4. Seal, label, and store in a cool dark place for 4-6 weeks
5. Shake daily
6. Strain through fine mesh and cheesecloth, pressing berries to extract all liquid
7. Bottle in dark glass

**Weight-to-volume:** Dried berries 1:5 in 50% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Antiviral remedy for colds and influenza
- Fever reducer
- Immune tonic during winter months
- Sinus congestion relief

### Scientific Evidence
- **Mixed results.** A 2004 randomized study found elderberry extract relieved flu symptoms on average 4 days earlier than placebo, with significantly less rescue medication needed.
- A 2016 trial found elderberry supplementation reduced cold duration and severity in air travelers.
- However, a more recent 2020 randomized controlled trial found no evidence that elderberry benefits the duration or severity of influenza, and post hoc analysis suggested outcomes were 2 days worse than placebo when elderberry was taken alone.
- In vitro studies show elderberry flavonoids (particularly anthocyanins) can bind to and prevent the entry of H1N1 influenza virus.
- Contains anthocyanins, flavonoids, and lectins with demonstrated antioxidant activity.

### Dosage
- **Acute illness:** 5 mL (1 teaspoon) 4 times daily for up to 5 days
- **Prevention:** 2.5 mL (1/2 teaspoon) once or twice daily
- **Children (over 2):** Half the adult dose

### Safety & Contraindications
- Cooked/processed elderberries are generally safe; raw and unripe berries, leaves, bark, and stems contain cyanogenic glycosides and are TOXIC
- **Never consume raw elderberries** -- they must be cooked or tinctured
- Mild GI side effects (nausea, diarrhea) reported rarely
- Theoretical concern about cytokine storm in severe infections -- some practitioners advise caution in autoimmune conditions, though this risk is debated
- May interact with immunosuppressant medications, diabetes medications, and diuretics

## 3. Valerian Root Tincture (Valeriana officinalis)

**Category:** Nervine, sedative, sleep aid

### Ingredients
- 1 cup dried valerian root (coarsely chopped or ground)
- 2.5 cups 60% alcohol (mix 80-proof vodka with a small amount of 190-proof grain alcohol, or use 120-proof spirits)

### Preparation
1. Place dried valerian root in a quart mason jar, filling 1/3 to 1/2
2. Cover with alcohol mixture, ensuring root is submerged by 1-2 inches
3. Seal tightly -- valerian has an extremely strong odor
4. Label with date and contents
5. Store in cool, dark place for 6 weeks, shaking daily
6. Strain through cheesecloth, pressing firmly
7. Bottle in dark glass dropper bottles
8. Note: The smell is pungent (often described as "dirty socks") -- this is normal

**Weight-to-volume:** Dried root 1:5 in 60% alcohol. Fresh root 1:2 in 75% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, tightly sealed (odor will escape loose caps)
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Sleep aid and insomnia remedy
- Anxiety and nervous tension
- Muscle relaxation and cramping
- Stress relief
- Historically used as a sedative since ancient Greece and Rome

### Scientific Evidence
- **Moderately supported for sleep, weak for anxiety.** A 2020 systematic review and meta-analysis of 60 studies found valerian root "can likely improve sleep and reduce anxiety in many people."
- Some studies found valerian helped people fall asleep faster, improved sleep quality, and increased time in deep sleep.
- However, many studies found no statistically significant effect compared to placebo.
- The NIH notes that evidence is "not sufficient to determine whether valerian works for sleep problems."
- Benefits may require regular use over 2-4 weeks rather than single doses.
- Active compounds include valerenic acid (which modulates GABA-A receptors), isovaleric acid, and iridoids (valepotriates).
- The American Academy of Sleep Medicine advises against valerian for chronic insomnia due to insufficient evidence.

### Dosage
- **Sleep:** 2-4 mL (40-80 drops) 30-60 minutes before bed
- **Anxiety:** 1-2 mL three times daily
- **Equivalent dried root dose:** 450-1410 mg daily for sleep
- May take 2-4 weeks of consistent use to notice full effect

### Safety & Contraindications
- Generally well-tolerated at recommended doses
- **Do NOT combine with** sedative medications (benzodiazepines, barbiturates, sleep aids), alcohol, or other CNS depressants -- risk of excessive sedation
- Side effects may include headache, stomach upset, mental dullness, excitability (paradoxical in some people), and vivid dreams
- **Withdrawal symptoms** possible after chronic use if stopped abruptly (anxiety, irritability, insomnia, rarely hallucinations) -- taper gradually
- Not recommended during pregnancy or breastfeeding
- May impair driving or operating machinery
- Possible liver toxicity with very long-term, high-dose use (rare)

## 4. Milk Thistle Tincture (Silybum marianum)

**Category:** Liver support, hepatoprotective

### Ingredients
- 1 cup dried milk thistle seeds (crushed or coarsely ground)
- 2.5 cups 70% alcohol (mix equal parts 80-proof vodka and 190-proof grain alcohol)

### Preparation
1. Crush or coarsely grind milk thistle seeds (a mortar and pestle or coffee grinder works) -- this is essential as the hard seed coat must be broken to release silymarin
2. Place crushed seeds in a quart mason jar, filling 1/3 to 1/2
3. Cover with 70% alcohol (higher percentage needed for the lipophilic silymarin compounds)
4. Seal, label, and store in cool dark place for 6-8 weeks
5. Shake vigorously daily -- the crushed seeds will settle
6. Strain through fine cheesecloth, pressing firmly
7. Allow sediment to settle for 24 hours, then decant clear liquid
8. Bottle in dark glass

**Weight-to-volume:** Dried seed 1:5 in 70% alcohol. The higher alcohol percentage is important because silymarin is poorly water-soluble.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Liver protection and regeneration
- Hangover remedy
- Support during hepatitis and liver disease
- Gallbladder support
- Mushroom poisoning antidote (Amanita phalloides -- clinical use)
- General detoxification support

### Scientific Evidence
- **Well-supported for liver protection.** The Mayo Clinic classifies silymarin for hepatoprotection as Grade B ("Good scientific evidence for use").
- Silymarin (the active complex of silybin, silydianin, and silychristin) has well-documented antioxidant and hepatoprotective properties in preclinical studies.
- In a pooled analysis of trials in patients with cirrhosis, silymarin treatment was associated with a significant reduction in liver-related deaths.
- Supplementation with 420 mg/day silymarin reduced the 4-year risk of mortality in cirrhosis patients.
- A 2022 study found 8 weeks of silymarin supplementation improved fatty liver markers in bariatric surgery candidates.
- Silymarin acts as a free radical scavenger and modulates enzymes associated with fibrosis and cirrhosis.
- **Limitation:** Silymarin has poor oral bioavailability -- much of it is not well absorbed from the gut, which may limit the effectiveness of oral preparations including tinctures. Standardized extracts may be more effective than tinctures for therapeutic doses.

### Dosage
- **General liver support:** 3-5 mL (60-100 drops) three times daily
- **Therapeutic doses in studies:** 420-600 mg silymarin daily (difficult to achieve with tinctures alone)
- Best taken between meals for optimal absorption

### Safety & Contraindications
- Excellent safety profile -- very well tolerated with low incidence of adverse events
- Mild GI effects (bloating, diarrhea, nausea) in some people
- **May interact with** drugs metabolized by CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and UGT enzymes
- Use caution with diabetes medications (may lower blood sugar)
- Use caution with hormone-sensitive conditions (has weak estrogenic activity)
- Safe during pregnancy according to some sources, but consult a provider
- Allergy possible in people allergic to ragweed, daisies, marigolds (Asteraceae family)

## 5. Black Walnut Hull Tincture (Juglans nigra)

**Category:** Antiparasitic, antimicrobial

### Ingredients
- Green (unripe) black walnut hulls, enough to fill jar 2/3 full
- 80-proof vodka or 50% grain alcohol
- Must use GREEN hulls harvested in early-to-mid summer (before they turn black)

### Preparation
1. Harvest green black walnut hulls while still bright green (usually July-August) -- they oxidize quickly
2. Wearing gloves (the tannins stain everything dark brown), chop hulls into quarters
3. Immediately place in a quart mason jar, filling 2/3 full
4. Cover completely with vodka -- work quickly as exposure to air turns them black
5. The tincture will turn very dark brown/black within hours -- this is normal
6. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 6 weeks
7. Shake daily
8. Strain through cheesecloth, bottle in dark glass

**Weight-to-volume:** Fresh hull 1:2 in 50% alcohol. Must use fresh green hulls; dried hulls lose much of their potency.

### Storage
- Dark glass bottles, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 2-3 years (potency decreases over time as juglone degrades)
- Best used within 1 year for maximum potency

### Traditional Uses
- Antiparasitic (intestinal worms and parasites)
- Antifungal (ringworm, athlete's foot -- topical)
- Antimicrobial
- Often combined with wormwood and cloves in the "Hulda Clark parasite cleanse"
- Historically used by Native Americans for skin conditions and intestinal complaints

### Scientific Evidence
- **Weak for internal antiparasitic use.** Scientific evidence confirms black walnut's antibacterial properties (higher in green hulls due to juglone content), but lab findings have not been validated in human clinical trials for parasite treatment.
- Juglone, the primary active compound, demonstrates in vitro antimicrobial, antifungal, and antiparasitic activity.
- No randomized controlled human trials support the use of black walnut hull tincture for parasites.
- The "parasite cleanse" protocol (black walnut + wormwood + cloves) popularized by Hulda Clark lacks scientific validation.

### Dosage
- **Folk dosage:** 20-40 drops in water, 3 times daily for no more than 2 weeks
- **Topical:** Apply directly to fungal skin infections with cotton ball
- **Not for long-term internal use**

### Safety & Contraindications
- **Juglone is toxic in large amounts** -- can cause liver and kidney damage
- **Short-term use only** -- do not exceed 2 weeks of internal use
- Not safe during pregnancy or breastfeeding
- Not safe for children
- Can cause nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea
- May cause allergic reactions in people with tree nut allergies
- **Stains skin, clothing, and countertops** permanently -- handle with gloves
- May interact with blood pressure medications and lithium

## 6. Wormwood Tincture (Artemisia absinthium)

**Category:** Bitter digestive, antiparasitic

### Ingredients
- 1/2 cup dried wormwood aerial parts (leaves and flowering tops)
- 2 cups 50% alcohol (100-proof vodka)

### Preparation
1. Place dried wormwood in a pint mason jar, filling 1/3 full (wormwood is very potent -- use less than most herbs)
2. Cover with alcohol, ensuring herb is submerged
3. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 4 weeks only (longer extraction can make it excessively bitter and increase thujone concentration)
4. Shake every other day
5. Strain through fine cheesecloth
6. Bottle in dark glass dropper bottles
7. Label clearly with warnings about dosage limits

**Weight-to-volume:** Dried herb 1:5 in 50% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Digestive bitter (stimulates appetite and digestion)
- Antiparasitic (intestinal worms -- hence the name)
- Key ingredient in absinthe
- Fever reducer
- Part of traditional malaria remedies (related species Artemisia annua is the source of artemisinin)
- Gall bladder stimulant

### Scientific Evidence
- **Mixed.** Wormwood's anthelmintic (anti-worm) effects have been demonstrated in laboratory and animal studies, with thujone showing activity against roundworms and other parasites.
- Artemisia absinthium is distinct from Artemisia annua (sweet wormwood), which is the source of the Nobel Prize-winning antimalarial drug artemisinin.
- A. absinthium extracts show anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties in vitro.
- One clinical trial found wormwood extract improved symptoms of Crohn's disease, but this has not been replicated.
- Most evidence is from in vitro and animal studies; human clinical evidence for antiparasitic effects is lacking.

### Dosage
- **Digestive bitter:** 10-20 drops in a small amount of water before meals
- **Antiparasitic (folk use):** 20-30 drops 3 times daily for no more than 2 weeks
- **NEVER exceed recommended doses**

### Safety & Contraindications
- **THUJONE TOXICITY is a serious concern.** Thujone, the primary volatile oil, is a neurotoxin that can cause seizures, renal failure, and death in high doses.
- **Short-term use ONLY** -- do not use for more than 2-4 weeks
- **Contraindicated in pregnancy** -- can cause uterine contractions and miscarriage
- Not safe while breastfeeding
- Not safe for children
- **Contraindicated in seizure disorders** -- thujone lowers seizure threshold
- Avoid with kidney disease
- May interact with anticonvulsant medications
- Do NOT combine with other thujone-containing herbs (sage, tansy, cedar)

## 7. Clove Tincture (Syzygium aromaticum)

**Category:** Antimicrobial, analgesic, antiparasitic

### Ingredients
- 1/2 cup whole dried cloves (lightly crushed)
- 2 cups 60% alcohol (mix 80-proof vodka with some 190-proof grain alcohol)

### Preparation
1. Lightly crush whole cloves with a mortar and pestle (do not powder -- just crack them open)
2. Place in a pint mason jar, filling about 1/3
3. Cover with alcohol, ensuring cloves are fully submerged
4. Seal tightly, label, store in cool dark place for 4-6 weeks
5. Shake every other day
6. Strain through fine cheesecloth
7. Bottle in dark glass dropper bottles

**Weight-to-volume:** Dried clove 1:5 in 60% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Toothache and dental pain (topical -- eugenol is a proven dental analgesic)
- Antiparasitic (often combined with wormwood and black walnut hull)
- Digestive aid
- Antimicrobial
- Sore throat gargle
- Traditional remedy for nausea

### Scientific Evidence
- **Well-supported for dental/antimicrobial use.** Eugenol, the primary active compound (60-90% of clove oil), is a proven topical analgesic used in dentistry for centuries and recognized by dental associations.
- Strong in vitro antimicrobial activity against a wide range of bacteria, fungi, and parasites.
- Eugenol-based anti-parasitic activity demonstrated in laboratory studies.
- Antioxidant activity is among the highest of any spice.
- The WHO recognizes clove oil as a dental analgesic.
- **Limitation:** Most evidence for internal antiparasitic use is preclinical.

### Dosage
- **Toothache (topical):** Apply 1-2 drops directly to affected tooth/gum with a cotton swab
- **Internal use:** 10-30 drops in water, up to 3 times daily
- **Antiparasitic protocol (folk use):** 20 drops 3 times daily for up to 2 weeks
- **Gargle:** 10-15 drops in 1/4 cup warm water

### Safety & Contraindications
- Topical dental use is generally safe and well-established
- Internal use in large amounts can cause liver damage, GI irritation, and allergic reactions
- **Eugenol is hepatotoxic in high doses** -- do not exceed recommended doses
- May slow blood clotting -- discontinue 2 weeks before surgery
- **Contraindicated with blood-thinning medications** (warfarin, aspirin, heparin)
- Not recommended during pregnancy (may stimulate uterine contractions)
- Not safe for children in concentrated tincture form
- Can irritate mucous membranes at full strength -- always dilute for internal use

## 8. Oregano Oil Tincture (Origanum vulgare)

**Category:** Antimicrobial, immune support

### Ingredients
- 1 cup fresh oregano leaves and flowering tops (or 1/2 cup dried)
- 2 cups 80-proof vodka

### Preparation
1. Harvest fresh oregano at peak bloom (when flowers are just opening -- highest oil content)
2. Chop coarsely and bruise leaves to release oils
3. Fill jar 2/3 full with fresh herb (or 1/3 with dried)
4. Cover with vodka, submerging herb completely
5. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 6 weeks
6. Shake daily
7. Strain and bottle in dark glass

**Note:** This is an oregano herb tincture, which is milder than concentrated oregano essential oil. Commercial "oil of oregano" supplements are steam-distilled essential oil in a carrier, which is far more concentrated and potent.

**Weight-to-volume:** Fresh herb 1:2 in 75% alcohol; dried herb 1:5 in 50% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Antimicrobial and antibacterial
- Respiratory infections (coughs, bronchitis, sore throat)
- Digestive support
- Candida and yeast overgrowth
- Immune stimulant

### Scientific Evidence
- **Strong in vitro evidence, limited human clinical data.** Carvacrol (the primary active compound, ~22% of oregano oil) demonstrates potent antimicrobial activity against a wide range of bacteria and fungi, including drug-resistant strains like MRSA.
- Carvacrol disrupts bacterial cell membranes and shows bactericidal properties at MIC range of 0.25-8 mg/mL.
- Effective in laboratory studies against E. coli, Salmonella, Staphylococcus aureus, Candida albicans, and Bacillus cereus.
- **Limitation:** "Promising mechanisms do not automatically translate into proven outcomes in humans" -- results depend heavily on product form, dose, and duration. Very few human clinical trials exist.
- Tincture form is significantly less concentrated than essential oil, so antimicrobial effects may be reduced.

### Dosage
- **General immune support:** 30-60 drops in water, 3 times daily
- **Acute infections (folk use):** 60 drops every 3-4 hours for up to 10 days
- **Gargle for sore throat:** 20-30 drops in 1/4 cup warm water

### Safety & Contraindications
- Herb tincture is generally well-tolerated at recommended doses
- **Do NOT confuse** with undiluted oregano essential oil, which is caustic and can burn mucous membranes
- May cause GI irritation in sensitive individuals
- **May interact with blood thinners** (carvacrol may affect platelet aggregation)
- Use caution with diabetes medications (may lower blood sugar)
- Not recommended during pregnancy (traditionally used as an emmenagogue)
- May reduce iron absorption -- use away from iron supplements
- Allergic reactions possible in people allergic to mint family (Lamiaceae)

## 9. Goldenseal Tincture (Hydrastis canadensis)

**Category:** Antimicrobial, digestive bitter

### Ingredients
- 1/2 cup dried goldenseal root (coarsely ground)
- 2 cups 60% alcohol

### Preparation
1. Coarsely grind dried goldenseal root
2. Place in a pint mason jar, filling about 1/3
3. Cover with 60% alcohol, submerging root by 1-2 inches
4. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 6-8 weeks (goldenseal benefits from longer extraction)
5. Shake daily
6. Strain through fine cheesecloth
7. Bottle in dark glass -- the tincture will be a deep, bright yellow

**Weight-to-volume:** Dried root 1:5 in 60% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Natural antibiotic for infections (historically one of the most popular remedies in North America)
- Digestive bitter -- stimulates bile and digestive secretions
- Topical antimicrobial for wound care
- Mucous membrane infections (sinusitis, sore throat, UTIs)
- Eye infections (diluted wash)
- Often combined with echinacea for colds and infections

### Scientific Evidence
- **Limited for most claims.** NCCIH states there is "no good scientific evidence to support using goldenseal for the common cold and other upper respiratory tract infections."
- The primary active compound, berberine, shows broad-spectrum antibiotic activity in vitro against Chlamydia, E. coli, Salmonella, and Staphylococcus aureus.
- **Critical limitation:** Berberine is poorly absorbed from the gut into the bloodstream, limiting systemic antibiotic effects. Goldenseal's action is most likely localized to the GI tract.
- Some evidence supports berberine for reducing diarrhea, including IBS-related diarrhea.
- Goldenseal extracts synergistically enhance the antibacterial activity of berberine via efflux pump inhibition.
- **ENDANGERED SPECIES:** Goldenseal (Hydrastis canadensis) is listed by CITES as endangered. Consider using sustainably cultivated sources, or substitute Oregon grape root (Mahonia aquifolium) which contains berberine and is not endangered.

### Dosage
- **Standard:** 1-2 mL (20-40 drops) 3 times daily
- **Acute use:** Up to 3 mL 3 times daily for no more than 3 weeks
- **Not for long-term use** -- cycle 2 weeks on, 1 week off

### Safety & Contraindications
- **Do not use during pregnancy** -- berberine can stimulate uterine contractions
- Not safe while breastfeeding (berberine can be harmful to newborns)
- Not safe for infants or young children
- Large doses can cause seizures, respiratory failure, and cardiac effects
- May lower blood sugar -- use caution with diabetes medications
- May interact with blood pressure medications, blood thinners, and cyclosporine
- Berberine inhibits CYP3A4 and CYP2D6 -- broad drug interaction potential
- **Sustainability concern:** Buy only from cultivated sources. Wild harvest is depleting endangered populations.

## 10. Astragalus Tincture (Astragalus membranaceus)

**Category:** Immune tonic, adaptogen

### Ingredients
- 1 cup dried astragalus root (sliced or shredded)
- 2.5 cups 50% alcohol (100-proof vodka)

### Preparation
1. Astragalus root is very tough and woody -- buy pre-sliced or shredded
2. Place root slices in a quart mason jar, filling 1/3 to 1/2
3. Cover with alcohol, ensuring root is fully submerged
4. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 8 weeks (astragalus benefits from extended extraction due to tough root material)
5. Shake daily
6. Strain through fine cheesecloth
7. Bottle in dark glass

**Note:** Astragalus is also excellent as a decoction (long simmered tea) and is traditionally added to soups and broths in Chinese medicine.

**Weight-to-volume:** Dried root 1:5 in 50% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Immune tonic (preventive, not for acute illness)
- Energy and vitality (qi tonic in Traditional Chinese Medicine)
- Adaptogenic -- helps body resist stress
- Longevity tonic
- Recovery from prolonged illness
- Kidney and spleen support (TCM)

### Scientific Evidence
- **Moderately supported.** Astragalus polysaccharides (APS) have demonstrated immunomodulatory effects in numerous studies, stimulating macrophages, natural killer cells, and T-cell activity.
- Contains telomerase activators (cycloastragenol and astragaloside IV) that have shown anti-aging effects in preliminary research.
- A 2014 meta-analysis found astragalus as an adjunct therapy improved outcomes in hepatitis B patients.
- Animal and in vitro studies show antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, cardioprotective, and neuroprotective effects.
- Used in integrative oncology as an immune support adjunct (though not as cancer treatment).
- **Limitation:** Most compelling evidence is from animal studies and in vitro work; large-scale human clinical trials are limited.

### Dosage
- **Immune tonic:** 3-5 mL (60-100 drops) 2-3 times daily
- **Long-term use is traditional** -- astragalus is meant to be taken daily for weeks to months
- Best taken as a preventive during well periods, NOT during acute infections (in TCM, it is considered to "close the exterior" and may trap pathogens)

### Safety & Contraindications
- Very well-tolerated; one of the safest herbs in the materia medica
- **Do NOT use during acute infections** (per TCM theory -- may worsen illness by "trapping" the pathogen)
- May interact with immunosuppressant drugs (due to immune-stimulating effects)
- May interact with lithium (may affect excretion)
- Use caution with blood sugar medications (may lower blood sugar)
- Generally considered safe in pregnancy in traditional use, but consult a provider

## 11. Reishi Mushroom Tincture (Ganoderma lucidum)

**Category:** Immune modulator, adaptogen

### Ingredients

**You need BOTH alcohol AND water extraction for mushrooms (dual extraction).**

**Alcohol Phase:**
- 100g dried reishi mushroom pieces or slices
- 500 mL 190-proof grain alcohol (95% ABV)

**Water Phase:**
- The marc (spent mushroom material) from the alcohol phase
- 750 mL filtered water

### Preparation (Dual Extraction Method)

**Phase 1 -- Alcohol Extraction (Triterpenes):**
1. Break dried reishi into small pieces (1/4 inch or smaller) -- this is very tough material, use pruning shears or a heavy knife
2. Place in a quart mason jar
3. Cover with 95% grain alcohol
4. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 6-8 weeks
5. Shake daily
6. After 6-8 weeks, strain through cheesecloth, RESERVING BOTH the liquid AND the spent mushroom material (marc)
7. Store the alcohol extract in a sealed jar

**Phase 2 -- Water Extraction (Beta-Glucans and Polysaccharides):**
1. Place the reserved spent mushroom material in a pot
2. Add 750 mL water
3. Bring to a low simmer (NOT a rolling boil)
4. Simmer for 2-4 hours, covered, until reduced by approximately half
5. Allow to cool completely
6. Strain through fine cheesecloth

**Phase 3 -- Combining:**
1. Combine the alcohol extract with the water decoction
2. The final product should be approximately 25-35% alcohol (enough to preserve it)
3. If the final alcohol percentage is too low (below 20%), the tincture may not keep. Add more alcohol extract or store refrigerated.
4. Bottle in dark glass

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 2-3 years (shorter than pure alcohol tinctures due to water content)
- Refrigerate if final alcohol content is below 25%

### Traditional Uses
- "Mushroom of immortality" in Chinese and Japanese traditional medicine
- Immune modulation (both stimulates and regulates immune function)
- Stress adaptation and calm energy
- Sleep support
- Liver protection
- Longevity and anti-aging
- Spiritual practice and meditation support (traditional)

### Scientific Evidence
- **Moderately supported.** A 2023 study found immune cell populations grew significantly more among 126 human participants administered reishi beta-glucans.
- Ganoderic acids (triterpenes) show anti-inflammatory, hepatoprotective, and anti-tumor activity in vitro.
- Beta-glucans demonstrate immunomodulatory effects, activating macrophages, dendritic cells, and natural killer cells.
- Some evidence for improved sleep quality and reduced fatigue in neurasthenia patients.
- Anti-tumor effects demonstrated in vitro and in animal models, but NO clinical evidence supports using reishi as a cancer treatment.
- **Limitation:** The FDA has not approved any mushroom extracts for disease treatment. Most studies use standardized extracts, not homemade tinctures.

### Dosage
- **Daily tonic:** 2-4 mL (40-80 drops) 2-3 times daily
- **Concentrated extract equivalent:** 1.5-9 grams of dried fruiting body daily
- Can be taken long-term

### Safety & Contraindications
- Generally well-tolerated; adverse effects are uncommon
- GI upset, dry mouth, and dizziness reported in some studies
- **May interact with blood thinners** (antiplatelet/anticoagulant effects)
- May lower blood pressure -- use caution with antihypertensives
- May lower blood sugar -- use caution with diabetes medications
- Use caution with immunosuppressant medications
- Rare cases of hepatotoxicity reported with powdered reishi products -- likely from contamination or adulteration
- Stop use 2 weeks before surgery (bleeding risk)

## 19. St. John's Wort Tincture (Hypericum perforatum)

**Category:** Antidepressant, nervine

### Ingredients

2. Chop coarsely
3. Fill jar 2/3 to 3/4 full with fresh herb

5. The tincture should turn deep red within 1-3 days -- this is the hypericin releasing
6. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 6 weeks
7. Shake daily
8. Strain and bottle in dark glass -- the final tincture should be a deep ruby red

**Weight-to-volume:** Fresh flowering tops 1:2 in 95% alcohol; dried herb 1:5 in 50% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place (light degrades hypericin)
- Shelf life: 3-5 years
- Protect from light exposure

- Mild to moderate depression
- Seasonal affective disorder (SAD)
- Anxiety and nervous tension
- Nerve pain (neuralgia)
- Wound healing (topical -- the infused oil)
- Historically known as "chase-devil" for its mood-lifting properties

### Scientific Evidence
- **Well-supported for mild-to-moderate depression.** Multiple meta-analyses and Cochrane reviews confirm St. John's Wort is superior to placebo and comparable to SSRIs for mild-to-moderate depression, with fewer side effects.
- Active compounds: hypericin, hyperforin, and flavonoids.
- Mechanism: Increases levels of serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine via reuptake inhibition (similar to SSRIs).
- NCCIH states it is "not a proven therapy for depression" and notes "research suggests it's not useful for major depression."

- Must be taken consistently, not as needed

- **EXTREMELY IMPORTANT: St. John's Wort has more drug interactions than almost any other herb.**

The combination can cause life-threatening serotonin syndrome (confusion, agitation, rapid heart rate, hyperthermia, seizures, potentially death).
- **Reduces effectiveness of:** oral contraceptives (risk of pregnancy), warfarin (risk of blood clots), cyclosporine (risk of organ rejection), HIV protease inhibitors, digoxin, theophylline, and many chemotherapy drugs.
- **Mechanism of interactions:** St. John's Wort induces CYP3A4, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, and P-glycoprotein, accelerating the metabolism of many drugs.
- **Low-hyperforin extracts** (<1 mg hyperforin/day) may have fewer drug interactions.
- **Photosensitivity:** Can cause severe sunburn, especially in fair-skinned people.
- **Contraindicated in pregnancy** (increases uterine muscle tone).
- May cause mania in people with bipolar disorder.
- Do NOT stop antidepressant medications to start St. John's Wort without medical supervision.

## 22. Rhodiola Tincture (Rhodiola rosea)

**Category:** Adaptogen, mental performance

### Ingredients
- 1 cup dried rhodiola root (chopped)
- 2 cups 50-60% alcohol

### Preparation
1. Chop dried rhodiola root into small pieces (it has a distinctive rose-like scent)
2. Place in a pint or quart mason jar, filling 1/3 to 1/2
3. Cover with alcohol
4. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 6 weeks
5. Shake daily
6. Strain and bottle in dark glass

**Weight-to-volume:** Dried root 1:5 in 50% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Mental performance and focus
- Physical endurance and stamina
- Stress resilience
- Fatigue recovery
- Depression and mood support
- High-altitude adaptation
- Used for centuries in Scandinavian, Russian, and Chinese traditional medicine

### Scientific Evidence
- **Well-supported for stress and fatigue.** Rhodiola rosea can effectively combat physical stress-related fatigue, low mood, anxiety, and depression, and may improve physical and mental working capacity.
- Active compounds: rosavins (rosavin, rosarin, rosin) and salidroside.
- Mechanism: Regulates the HPA axis, modulates cortisol release, and affects serotonergic and dopaminergic neurotransmission.
- A 2012 systematic review found evidence supporting efficacy for physical and mental fatigue.
- Multiple RCTs show improved cognitive function under stress conditions.
- Clinical doses range from 290-1500 mg rhodiola extract daily.

### Dosage
- **Stress/fatigue:** 2-3 mL (40-60 drops) 1-2 times daily
- **Best taken in the morning or early afternoon** -- may interfere with sleep if taken late
- **Standardized extract equivalent:** 200-600 mg daily (standardized to 3% rosavins, 1% salidroside)

### Safety & Contraindications
- Generally well-tolerated with few side effects
- May cause insomnia, irritability, or agitation in some people (stimulating effects)
- Avoid taking late in the day
- May interact with antidepressants, stimulants, and blood pressure medications
- Limited pregnancy and breastfeeding data -- avoid
- Use caution with bipolar disorder (stimulating herbs may trigger mania)
- May affect blood sugar -- use caution with diabetes medications

## 23. Turmeric Tincture (Curcuma longa)

**Category:** Anti-inflammatory, antioxidant

### Ingredients
- 1 cup fresh turmeric root (grated) or 1/2 cup dried turmeric root (not powder)
- 2 cups 95% grain alcohol (for fresh) or 65% for dried
- 1 tablespoon whole black peppercorns (piperine dramatically increases curcumin absorption)

### Preparation
1. Grate fresh turmeric root finely (WEAR GLOVES -- turmeric stains everything bright yellow)
2. Place grated turmeric and peppercorns in a quart mason jar
3. Cover with alcohol
4. Seal, label, store in cool dark place for 6 weeks
5. Shake daily
6. Strain through fine cheesecloth (double layer -- turmeric is messy)
7. Bottle in dark glass -- the tincture will be a deep orange-gold

**Weight-to-volume:** Fresh root 1:2 in 95% alcohol; dried root 1:5 in 65% alcohol.

### Storage
- Amber glass, cool dark place (curcumin is light-sensitive)
- Shelf life: 3-5 years

### Traditional Uses
- Anti-inflammatory for joint pain, arthritis, and general inflammation
- Digestive support
- Liver protection
- Cardiovascular health
- Skin health
- Central remedy in Ayurvedic and Traditional Chinese Medicine for thousands of years

### Scientific Evidence
- **Well-supported for anti-inflammatory effects.** Curcumin, the primary active compound, has thousands of published studies demonstrating anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anticancer properties.
- Multiple RCTs show curcumin reduces pain and improves function in osteoarthritis, comparable to NSAIDs in some studies.
- Anti-inflammatory mechanism: Inhibits NF-kB, COX-2, and multiple inflammatory cytokines.
- **Critical limitation:** Curcumin has extremely poor bioavailability (~1% absorbed orally). Piperine (from black pepper) increases absorption by ~2000%. Even so, achieving therapeutic blood levels from a tincture is challenging.
- Most clinical trials use high-dose standardized curcumin extracts (500-2000 mg) with bioavailability enhancers, not traditional preparations.

### Dosage
- **Anti-inflammatory:** 3-5 mL (60-100 drops) 3 times daily with food
- **Always include black pepper** or take with a fat-containing meal
- **Standardized extract equivalent:** 500-2000 mg curcumin daily with piperine

### Safety & Contraindications
- Generally well-tolerated; GI upset is the most common side effect
- **May increase bleeding risk** -- stop 2 weeks before surgery; use caution with anticoagulants
- May lower blood sugar -- use caution with diabetes medications
- High doses may cause gallbladder contractions -- **avoid with gallstones or bile duct obstruction**
- May interact with chemotherapy drugs (both enhancement and interference reported)
- Avoid therapeutic doses during pregnancy (culinary amounts are fine)
- May interact with iron absorption
- Stains everything permanently -- handle with care
