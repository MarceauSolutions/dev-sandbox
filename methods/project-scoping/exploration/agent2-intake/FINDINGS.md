# Agent 2 Findings: Lead Qualification & Discovery Best Practices

## Executive Summary

This research explores best practices for qualifying web design leads and scoping projects for a solopreneur serving local small businesses in Naples, Florida. The focus is on practical, actionable frameworks that filter out problematic clients early while accurately scoping work to avoid over-promising.

**Key Finding**: The most successful freelancers use a structured qualification process that evaluates fit across 5 dimensions: Budget alignment, Timeline reality, Decision authority, Problem clarity, and Collaboration style. Using a scoring system during initial contact can reduce project failures by filtering out 80% of problematic engagements before they begin.

---

## 1. Lead Qualification Questions

### The BANT+ Framework for Freelancers

Traditional BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) adapted for solopreneur web design:

| Category | Question | Purpose | Red Flag Answers |
|----------|----------|---------|------------------|
| **Budget** | "What budget range have you set aside for this project?" | Determines if expectations align with your pricing | "Whatever it costs" (no planning), "As cheap as possible", refuses to discuss |
| **Budget** | "Have you gotten quotes from other designers?" | Gauges market awareness and price shopping | "Yes, yours is 3x higher" (price shopper), "I'm talking to 10 people" |
| **Authority** | "Who else will be involved in making this decision?" | Identifies if you're talking to decision-maker | "My business partner/spouse will decide", "I'll need to check with..." |
| **Authority** | "What's your process for approving the final design?" | Prevents design-by-committee nightmares | Vague answers, mentions many stakeholders, "everyone needs to love it" |
| **Need** | "What's the main problem your current website isn't solving?" | Tests if they have a real business need | "I just want it to look better" (vanity project), can't articulate problem |
| **Need** | "How will you measure if the new website is successful?" | Ensures realistic expectations | "I want to go viral", "Rank #1 on Google immediately", no clear metrics |
| **Timeline** | "When do you need this completed, and why that date?" | Identifies unrealistic deadlines | "ASAP", "Yesterday", no reason for urgency (indicates low priority) |
| **Timeline** | "Do you have the content (photos, text) ready to go?" | Tests if they've done their homework | "You'll write it", "I'll get it to you eventually", no preparation |
| **Fit** | "What does your ideal working relationship with a designer look like?" | Reveals collaboration style preferences | "I want to be hands-off" (then micromanages), expects 24/7 availability |
| **Fit** | "Tell me about your experience working with contractors before." | Surfaces past issues that may repeat | Horror stories about every past contractor, blames everyone else |

### Qualification Scoring System

Score each dimension 1-5, minimum 15/25 to proceed:

| Dimension | 1 (Disqualify) | 3 (Caution) | 5 (Ideal) |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Budget** | Won't discuss, price shopper | Has range but below yours | Clear budget matching your rates |
| **Authority** | Not decision-maker | Needs to consult partner | Sole decision-maker |
| **Need** | Vanity/"just because" | Vague business goal | Clear problem + success metrics |
| **Timeline** | "ASAP" or no deadline | Reasonable but tight | Realistic with buffer |
| **Fit** | Red flags in past contractor relationships | Some concerns | Values expertise, clear communicator |

## 2. Discovery Call Best Practices

### Pre-Call Preparation (15 minutes)

1. **Research their business**: Google, social media, current website, reviews
2. **Identify 3 specific observations**: "I noticed your booking system requires 5 clicks..."
3. **Prepare 2-3 hypotheses**: Potential problems you could solve
4. **Set your minimum engagement**: Know your walk-away point

### Discovery Call Script Template (30-45 minutes)

```
OPENING (5 minutes)
-------------------
"Thanks for taking the time, [Name]. I know you're busy running [business].
Before we dive in, I'd like to understand your business better so I can
see if I'm the right fit to help. Sound good?"

[RAPPORT] "How long have you been running [business name]?"

DISCOVERY (15-20 minutes)
-------------------------
SITUATION:
"Walk me through how customers typically find and book with you right now."
"What's working well? What's frustrating?"

PROBLEM:
"You mentioned [issue]. How is that affecting your business?"
"If you had to put a number on it, what's that costing you monthly?"
(lost bookings, time wasted, etc.)

IMPLICATION:
"If this stays the same for another year, what happens?"
"How does this compare to your competitors?"

NEED-PAYOFF:
"If we could solve [problem], what would that mean for your business?"
"What would make this project a home run for you?"

QUALIFICATION (10 minutes)
--------------------------
BUDGET:
"Projects like this typically range from $X to $Y depending on scope.
Does that align with what you had in mind?"

TIMELINE:
"When are you hoping to have this live? Any specific events driving that?"

AUTHORITY:
"Who else should be involved in this conversation?"

CONTENT:
"Do you have your content ready - photos, service descriptions, pricing?"

CLOSE (5 minutes)
-----------------
IF QUALIFIED:
"Based on what you've shared, here's what I'm thinking... [brief solution].
Would you like me to put together a detailed proposal?"

IF NOT QUALIFIED:
"I appreciate you sharing all this. To be honest, based on [budget/timeline/scope],
I'm not sure I'm the right fit for this project. I'd recommend [alternative]."

ALWAYS:
"What questions do you have for me?"
```

### Discovery Call Best Practices

1. **Talk less than 30%**: Your job is to listen, not pitch
2. **Take notes visibly**: "Let me write that down, it's important"
3. **Quantify the problem**: Always ask "What's that costing you?"
4. **Mirror their language**: Use their words back to them
5. **Don't solve on the call**: "That's a great question - I'll include my recommendation in the proposal"
6. **End with clear next steps**: "I'll send the proposal by Thursday. Can we schedule a call for Friday to review?"

## 3. Red Flags: Difficult Client Warning Signs

### Tier 1: Immediate Disqualification

| Red Flag | What They Say/Do | Why It's a Problem |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Price Shopping** | "Can you match this $500 quote?" | Will never value your work |
| **Spec Work Request** | "Show me some mockups first, then I'll decide" | Expects free work, won't respect boundaries |
| **Urgency Without Reason** | "I need this by Monday" (it's Friday) | Poor planning becomes your emergency |
| **Disrespect for Your Time** | No-shows, reschedules multiple times | Will do this throughout project |
| **Bad-mouths Everyone** | "My last 3 designers were terrible" | You'll be #4 on that list |
| **Won't Sign Contract** | "Can't we just shake on it?" | No legal protection, payment issues coming |

### Tier 2: Proceed with Caution

| Red Flag | What They Say/Do | Mitigation Strategy |
|----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Vague Requirements** | "I'll know it when I see it" | Fixed revision limits in contract |
| **Committee Decision** | "We have 4 partners who all need to approve" | One point of contact requirement |
| **No Budget Discussion** | Deflects budget questions | Provide range, require confirmation before proposal |
| **Feature Creep Hints** | "While you're at it, could you also..." | Scope locked in contract, change order process |
| **Communication Mismatches** | Expects instant responses, calls at odd hours | Set communication boundaries upfront |

### Tier 3: Yellow Flags (Watch Closely)

- First-time website owner (needs more hand-holding)
- Very small business (<1 year old) - may not have budget for revisions
- "My nephew started it but..." - may have unrealistic expectations
- Wants to "build a platform" or "the next [big thing]" - scope creep risk

## 4. Handling "I Don't Know What I Want" Clients

### Strategy 1: The Examples Approach

```
"No problem - most business owners aren't web designers. Let me ask it differently:

1. Show me 3 websites you love (doesn't have to be your industry)
2. Tell me what you like about each one
3. Now show me one you hate - what bothers you about it?"
```

### Strategy 2: The Goals Approach

```
"Let's forget the website for a second. Tell me:

1. What's your #1 business goal this year?
2. What's the biggest friction your customers face?
3. If your website could only do ONE thing, what would it be?"
```

### Strategy 3: The Competitor Approach

```
"Let's look at your competitors together:

1. Who do you lose business to most often?
2. Let's pull up their website - what do they do better than you?
3. What do YOU do better that doesn't show on your current site?"
```

### Strategy 4: The Day-in-the-Life Approach

```
"Walk me through what happens when someone finds your business:

1. How do they typically find you?
2. What questions do they ask before booking?
3. What makes someone choose you over the competitor?"
```

### Key Principle: You're the Expert

When clients don't know what they want, they need guidance, not more options:

- **Don't**: "Would you prefer style A or style B or maybe C?"
- **Do**: "Based on what you've told me, I recommend X because [reason]. Does that resonate?"

## 5. Scoping Without Over-Promising

### The 3-Tier Proposal Structure

Never give one price. Give three options:

| Tier | Name | Description | Pricing |
|------|------|-------------|---------|
| **Essential** | "Foundation" | Must-haves only, gets them live | $X |
| **Recommended** | "Growth" | Essential + features that drive results | $X + 40% |
| **Premium** | "Scale" | Everything + advanced features | $X + 100% |

**Why this works**:
- Anchors high, makes middle attractive
- Client feels in control of scope
- Easy to say "that's a Premium feature" when scope creeps

### Scoping Checklist (Include/Exclude)

Every proposal must explicitly state:

**Included:**
- Number of pages
- Number of revision rounds
- Specific features (contact form, booking, etc.)
- Timeline with milestones
- What YOU provide (design, development, hosting setup)

**Not Included (Additional Cost):**
- Content writing/copywriting
- Photography/stock images
- Logo design
- SEO/marketing
- Ongoing maintenance
- Additional pages/features
- Rush fees

### Time Buffers

| Project Complexity | Estimated Time | Quote Time (add buffer) |
|-------------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Simple (5 pages, template) | 1 week | 2 weeks |
| Medium (10 pages, custom) | 2-3 weeks | 4-5 weeks |
| Complex (e-commerce, booking) | 4-6 weeks | 8 weeks |

**Rule**: Estimate honestly, then add 50% buffer. Under-promise, over-deliver.

### Scope Lock Language

Contract language that prevents scope creep:

```
"This proposal covers [specific deliverables]. Any additional features,
pages, or functionality not explicitly listed will be quoted separately
and added via written change order. Changes requested after design
approval may incur revision fees of $X/hour."
```

### Managing Expectations Script

```
"I want to make sure we're aligned on a few things before we start:

1. REVISIONS: This includes [X] rounds of revisions. After that,
   it's $X per round. This keeps the project on budget and on time.

2. CONTENT: I'll need all your content by [date]. If it's late,
   the timeline shifts accordingly.

3. FEEDBACK: I'll need your feedback within 48 hours of each
   milestone. Delayed feedback = delayed launch.

4. SCOPE: If you want to add features mid-project, no problem -
   we'll quote it separately. This protects both of us.

Does that all sound fair?"
```

## 6. Local Small Business Specific Considerations

### Naples, FL Market Context

For gyms, fitness studios, and local services:

**Common Needs:**
- Mobile-first design (customers searching on phones)
- Google Business Profile integration
- Booking/scheduling integration (Mindbody, Vagaro, Acuity)
- Class schedules that are easy to update
- Before/after galleries (fitness)
- Testimonials/reviews display
- Location/hours/contact prominent

**Common Misconceptions:**
- "I need an app" (usually a mobile-responsive site suffices)
- "I want to rank #1 on Google" (SEO is ongoing, not one-time)
- "Can you make it go viral?" (websites don't go viral, content does)

**Discovery Questions for Local Businesses:**

| Question | Why Ask |
|----------|---------|
| "Where do most of your customers come from?" | Determines if website is even the priority |
| "Do you have a Google Business Profile?" | Often more impactful than website |
| "What booking system do you use?" | Integration requirements |
| "Who updates your current site?" | Determines CMS needs |
| "What's your busy season?" | Timeline implications |

## 9. Deliverable Templates

### Lead Intake Form Questions (Pre-Call Qualifier)

1. What's your business name and what do you do?
2. What's the main problem you're hoping a new website will solve?
3. Do you have a budget range in mind? ($1,500-3,000 / $3,000-5,000 / $5,000-10,000 / $10,000+)
4. When do you need this completed?
5. Who will be making the final decision on this project?

**Auto-disqualify if**: Budget under minimum, timeline impossible, "just exploring"

### Proposal Scope Section Template

```
## Project Scope

### Included in This Proposal:
- [X]-page responsive website
- Mobile-optimized design
- [Feature 1]: [description]
- [Feature 2]: [description]
- [X] rounds of design revisions
- Basic on-page SEO setup
- Google Analytics integration
- 30-day post-launch support

### Not Included (Available as Add-Ons):
- Copywriting/content creation (+$X)
- Professional photography (+$X)
- Logo design (+$X)
- Ongoing SEO services (+$X/month)
- Monthly maintenance (+$X/month)
- Additional pages (+$X/page)

### Timeline:
- Week 1: Discovery & wireframes
- Week 2: Design mockups
- Week 3: Development
- Week 4: Revisions & launch

### Your Responsibilities:
- Provide all content by [date]
- Feedback within 48 hours of each milestone
- Final approval before launch
```

## Summary

**Key Takeaways for Marceau Solutions:**

1. **Qualify before you call**: Use an intake form to filter obvious mismatches
2. **Score leads systematically**: 15/25 minimum across Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline, Fit
3. **Run discovery calls, not sales calls**: Listen 70%, talk 30%
4. **Trust red flags**: One "bad-mouths everyone" comment is enough to walk away
5. **Guide uncertain clients**: You're the expert - make recommendations, don't offer endless options
6. **Scope explicitly**: Everything in writing, change orders for additions
7. **Buffer your timelines**: Estimate honestly, add 50%, deliver early

**Bottom Line**: The 2 hours invested in proper qualification saves 20 hours of project rescue on bad-fit clients.
